**Purpose:** Students apply the Week 4 Whately habit of mapping practical syllogisms, naming terms, and testing whether the middle term truly connects the argument in Julius Caesar.

## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

- **Major term:** the predicate of the conclusion; the class or judgment being asserted of the minor term.
- **Minor term:** the subject of the conclusion; the person or case the conclusion is about.
- **Middle term:** the linking term that appears in the premises but not the conclusion. It must connect the minor term to the major term.
- **Pressure-point premise:** the premise on which the argument's success most depends, often because it decides whether the minor term really belongs under the middle term.

Grade for logical mapping and explanation, not for a single mandated wording.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Brutus's conclusion (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Brutus is trying to justify the conclusion that Caesar should be killed or stopped before he is crowned/gains supreme power. Strong answers may phrase it as “Caesar should be killed in the shell before he becomes dangerous.”

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: States the practical conclusion clearly
- 1 pt: Gives a vague anti-Caesar theme but not a clear conclusion
- 0 pts: Gives plot summary only

**Notes for grading:**
- Accept “stopped” or “killed” if the prevention logic is clear.

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Premises behind Brutus's argument (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong map includes a major premise such as “Anyone who will probably become a dangerous tyrant if given supreme power should be stopped before gaining it,” and a minor premise such as “Caesar will probably become a dangerous tyrant if crowned.”

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Gives plausible major and minor premises that support the conclusion
- 1 pt: Gives only one premise or gives premises with unclear relation
- 0 pts: Gives quotations or themes without premise structure

**Notes for grading:**
- Do not require exact wording; require the future-tyrant/prevention logic.

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – Brutus's terms (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Minor term: Caesar. Major term: people who should be stopped/killed before gaining power. Middle term: one who will probably become a dangerous tyrant if given supreme power.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Correctly identifies all three terms
- 1 pt: Correctly identifies two terms or gives partially clear terms
- 0 pts: Does not distinguish major, minor, and middle terms

**Notes for grading:**
- Equivalent wording is acceptable if roles are correct.

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Pressure-point premise (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The pressure point is the minor premise: that Caesar really will probably become a dangerous tyrant if crowned. Brutus would need evidence about Caesar's character, actions, plans, or likely use of power, not merely the possibility that power can corrupt.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names the minor premise and explains what must be proved
- 1 pt: Notices the Caesar premise is weak but explains vaguely
- 0 pts: Says the image itself proves the argument

**Notes for grading:**
- Credit answers that identify the middle-term application as the issue.

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Cassius's conclusion (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Cassius wants Brutus to conclude that Caesar should not stand above Rome/Brutus, that Rome should resist Caesar's one-man greatness, or that Brutus should join action against Caesar.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: States a clear practical or political conclusion
- 1 pt: Gives a general anti-Caesar attitude without conclusion form
- 0 pts: Summarizes imagery only

**Notes for grading:**
- Several formulations are acceptable because Cassius's passage is less formal.

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – Cassius's middle term (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Possible middle terms include “one man falsely treated as greater than Rome's nobles,” “a ruler whose dominance makes Romans underlings,” or “a threat to Roman liberty.” Cassius gives emotional and honor-based evidence, but he does not fully prove that Caesar belongs under the dangerous-ruler term.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names a plausible middle term and evaluates whether it is proved
- 1 pt: Names a plausible term but gives little evaluation
- 0 pts: Does not connect the term to Cassius's conclusion

**Notes for grading:**
- Accept different middle terms if they reflect Cassius's reasoning.

## Question 7: Part 4 Q1 – Comparing discipline (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Brutus's serpent's egg argument is usually more logically disciplined because it can be mapped into a clearer major premise, minor premise, conclusion, and middle term. Cassius is rhetorically forceful but mixes honor, resentment, equality, and liberty in a less precise structure. A strong answer may still criticize Brutus's weak minor premise.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Compares both arguments using premise, conclusion, and middle term
- 1 pt: Gives a reasonable comparison with limited logic vocabulary
- 0 pts: Chooses based only on preference or character judgment

**Notes for grading:**
- Do not require students to say Brutus is morally right; only more formally disciplined.

## Question 8: Part 4 Q2 – Whately's middle-term question (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong answer says the speaker must prove the middle term: that the person is truly a likely grave future danger/tyrant whose prevention is justified. Mere possibility, fear, vivid imagery, or dislike does not prove that violent prevention follows.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: States the needed middle term and warns against possibility-as-proof
- 1 pt: Gives a relevant warning but does not name the middle-term issue clearly
- 0 pts: Treats future danger as automatically proved

**Notes for grading:**
- Credit answers that generalize responsibly beyond Julius Caesar.

- Whole points only. No half points.
