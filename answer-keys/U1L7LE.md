**Purpose:** Students apply Week 7's fallacy diagnosis—appearance of proof, false claim vs bad inference, emotional vs logical force, fallacy autopsy—to Lady Macbeth's pressure and Macbeth's settlement in Act 1, Scene 7.

## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

- **Fallacy:** reasoning that seems to prove a conclusion but fails to prove it.
- **Appearance of proof:** language, tone, or pressure that makes a claim feel finished without earning it.
- **False claim:** an untrue statement used as support.
- **Bad inference:** a leap from reasons to conclusion that does not follow.
- **Emotional force:** the power of feeling (shame, fear, loyalty, desire) to settle a decision.
- **Rhetorical force:** skillful speaking, vivid images, identity pressure, and practical planning used to move a hearer.
- **Logical force:** whether the reasons, if granted, actually justify the conclusion.
- **Fallacy autopsy:** (1) conclusion, (2) apparent proof, (3) defect, (4) repair or narrowed claim.
- **Repair / narrowed claim:** a fairer statement of what has been shown, or a claim that would require different proof.

Grade for separating persuasion from proof. Plot summary alone does not earn points.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Conclusion pressed (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Lady Macbeth is pressing Macbeth to carry out the murder of Duncan / to act on the enterprise he named / to stop hesitating and prove himself by doing the deed. Strong answers state a clear action-conclusion, not merely “she insults him.”

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Clear conclusion about acting on the murder enterprise
- 1 pt: Vague pressure without naming the practical conclusion
- 0 pts: Plot summary only or no conclusion

**Notes for grading:**
- Accept “you must kill Duncan” or “hesitation proves you are no man / a coward.”

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Appearance of proof (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
At least two pressure moves, such as: questioning his hope/love; calling hesitation cowardice; redefining manhood as daring the deed; contrasting past bold speech with present fear; animal/cat image of desire without action; intimate shame.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Two or more concrete language pressure moves
- 1 pt: One clear move or only general “she shames him”
- 0 pts: No use of the passage

**Notes for grading:**
- Quotations or close paraphrase both fine.

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – False claim or bad inference (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Mainly a **bad inference**, often with a **loaded or doubtful claim** about what “man” means. Hesitation does not prove that Macbeth is no man, and shaming him does not prove that Duncan should be murdered. “Both” is strong if the student explains a doubtful manhood claim plus the leap to action.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Clear diagnosis of bad inference and/or both, with explanation
- 1 pt: Names a failure type with weak explanation
- 0 pts: Says the speech fully proves the murder is right

**Notes for grading:**
- Do not require a named classical fallacy beyond Week 7 vocabulary.

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Emotional vs logical force (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
**Emotional force:** shame, love-accusation, manhood anxiety, fear of looking cowardly. **Logical force:** whether reasons show the murder is justified. The exchange is strong in emotional force and weak in logical force: Macbeth is moved before the justice of the act is established.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Clear contrast using the exchange
- 1 pt: Defines the terms without tying them well to the scene
- 0 pts: Collapses emotion and proof into one idea

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Cover-up plan (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The plan supports **feasibility / concealment** (drugging chamberlains, planting daggers, staging blame). It fails to prove that the murder is **morally right, necessary, or justified**. Possibility and cover-up are not proof of rightness.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Separates practical support from missing moral/logical proof
- 1 pt: Notes the plan is practical with limited precision
- 0 pts: Treats the plan as full justification

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – “I am settled” (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Primarily **confidence that they can hide it** / resolve under pressure; not new proof that the murder is right. “Both” is acceptable only if the student explains that emotional settlement and practical confidence combine without moral proof.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Correctly prioritizes pressure/concealment over earned justification
- 1 pt: Partial
- 0 pts: Says Macbeth now has proof the murder is just

## Question 7: Part 4 Q1 – Full autopsy (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
(1) **Conclusion:** Macbeth should carry out Duncan’s murder / stop hesitating and act. (2) **Apparent proof:** manhood/shame pressure, love-test language, and later a workable cover-up plan. (3) **Defect:** bad inference from hesitation to unworthiness; emotional force substitutes for justification; cover-up proves possibility, not rightness. (4) **Repair:** a fairer claim would be “Macbeth is afraid” or “the plan may hide the crime,” not “the murder is proved necessary” or “hesitation proves he is no man.”

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: All four autopsy pieces coherent
- 1 pt: Two or three pieces clear
- 0 pts: Plot summary without diagnosis

## Question 8: Part 4 Q2 – Capstone paragraph (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong paragraph explains that speeches can feel conclusive through shame, intimacy, identity pressure, and practical confidence while still failing to prove the central claim. Lady Macbeth makes hesitation feel intolerable and the plan feel workable; neither step shows that Duncan must die. Week 7’s point: appearance of proof is not proof.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Clear explanation using these passages and Week 7 vocabulary
- 1 pt: General statement with thin passage use
- 0 pts: Collapses persuasion with proof or ignores the passages

- Whole points only. No half points.
- Total: 8 questions × 2 points = 16 points.
- Accept equivalent wording; do not require plot trivia beyond the provided passages.
