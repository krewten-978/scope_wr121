# U1L11LE – Lit Example Worksheet Answer Key & Rubric

**Total Points:** 16

**Paired reader:** `units/elements-of-logic-week11/lit-examples/U1L11LE_lit_example_reader.tex` / `.pdf`.

**Paired worksheet:** `graded-assignments/U1L11LE/worksheet.tex` / `.pdf` (Assignment ID **U1L11LE**).

**Purpose:** Students apply Week 11 analogy / relevant-similarity diagnosis to Brutus in *Julius Caesar* and Macbeth in *Macbeth*.

## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

- **Source case:** the comparison used to illuminate the argument.
- **Target case:** the person, decision, or situation being judged.
- **Relevant similarity:** the likeness that matters for the conclusion.
- **Disanalogy:** an important difference that limits the proof.
- **Overextension:** using a comparison to prove more than its likeness can support.
- **Narrow repair:** reducing the conclusion so it matches what the analogy actually proves.

Plot summary alone does not earn points.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Brutus's source and target (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The source case is a serpent's egg or adder that will become dangerous if allowed to hatch/gain power. The target case is Caesar if he is crowned or allowed to rise to greater power.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Correctly names both source and target
- 1 pt: Names only one side clearly
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Brutus's intended conclusion (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Brutus wants the comparison to support killing Caesar before Caesar becomes dangerous as a ruler. Strong answers may state the conclusion as preventive assassination: Caesar must be stopped now because future crowned Caesar would threaten Rome.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Clear preventive-killing conclusion
- 1 pt: Vague “Caesar is dangerous”
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – Persuasive similarity (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The relevant similarity is potential future danger: the egg is not yet harmful but will become harmful if it develops; Brutus fears Caesar may not yet be tyrannical but may become tyrannical if crowned. The comparison also suggests danger is easier to prevent before it is fully grown.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names future/potential danger and preventive timing
- 1 pt: Mentions danger only
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Limiting disanalogy (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A serpent's nature is fixed and predictable in the image, while Caesar is a human political actor whose future actions are uncertain. Brutus even admits he has not personally known Caesar's affections to overpower his reason. Unlike an egg, Caesar can deliberate, be checked by laws, or act differently.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Gives a clear disanalogy that limits the proof
- 1 pt: Gives a generic “people are not snakes” with little explanation
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Judgment on Brutus's analogy (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The analogy supports a narrower warning about the danger of unchecked power, but it does not by itself prove Caesar must be killed. It becomes overextended if Brutus treats possible future danger as settled proof of necessary assassination without additional evidence.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Balanced judgment: supports warning, not full assassination conclusion
- 1 pt: Says the analogy is good or bad without proof-limit explanation
- 0 pts: Treats the analogy as automatically proving killing is necessary

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – Narrow rewrite (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Acceptable narrowed conclusions include: “If Caesar is crowned, Romans should be cautious about concentrating unchecked power,” or “Caesar's rise gives reason to set safeguards before granting more authority.” A stronger conclusion would require added evidence of imminent tyranny, failed lawful remedies, or unavoidable danger.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Rewrites to a narrower caution/safeguards conclusion or names needed added proof
- 1 pt: Some narrowing but still overstrong
- 0 pts: Repeats “therefore kill him” without added evidence

## Question 7: Part 4 Q1 – Macbeth's bear-like comparison (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Source case: a bear tied to a stake and forced to fight. Target case: Macbeth near defeat. Relevant similarity: Macbeth is surrounded/trapped, cannot flee easily, and faces violent pressure. Disanalogy: Macbeth is a moral agent whose earlier choices helped create the trap; unlike a baited animal, he can still be judged for choices and motives.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Includes source, target, relevant similarity, and disanalogy
- 1 pt: Includes some elements but not all
- 0 pts: Plot summary only or incorrect

## Question 8: Part 4 Q2 – Macbeth's moral choice (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The comparison mainly describes Macbeth's sense of being trapped and forced into combat. It does not prove that he has no moral choice left or no responsibility. Strong answers may note that the image is psychologically powerful but should not erase agency.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Distinguishes vivid self-description from proof of no moral agency
- 1 pt: Thin answer about feeling trapped
- 0 pts: Says the analogy proves Macbeth has no choice without qualification

**Overall Notes for Grading This Worksheet:**
- Whole points only. Total: 8 × 2 = 16 points.
- Credit students who admire the literary force of the comparisons while still testing their logical reach.
