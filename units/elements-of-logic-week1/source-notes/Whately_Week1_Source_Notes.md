# Source Notes — Whately, Elements of Logic, Week 1

## Primary source

Richard Whately, *Elements of Logic*. Public-domain editions consulted through Internet Archive, especially:

- *Elements of logic*, New York: Harper & Brothers, 1853. Internet Archive item: `elementsoflogic04what`.
- OCR text consulted from: `https://archive.org/download/elementsoflogic04what/elementsoflogic04what_djvu.txt`

## Week 1 focus passages

Week 1 uses the Introduction and prefatory defense of logic, especially Whately's claims that:

1. Logic may be considered both the science and the art of reasoning.
2. As science, logic investigates the principles of argumentation and analyzes reasoning.
3. As art, logic gives rules for guarding against erroneous deductions.
4. Logic has been discredited when writers made extravagant claims for it, as if it could discover truth in every subject or teach the entire use of the intellectual faculties.
5. A science should not be blamed for failing to do what it never promised to do.

## Adaptation policy

The student reading is **adapted and modernized** rather than quoted continuously. It preserves Whately's order of thought and central claims while making the prose accessible for the revised 12th-grade long-unit plan. The reading does not attempt to summarize the whole book; it focuses on the opening question: what logic can and cannot do. The revised materials also introduce recurring *Macbeth* and *Julius Caesar* applications for the logic portfolio.

## Revision note

After Wayne reviewed the first version, the student reading was expanded so that the argument-lab procedure is taught inside the reading before students are asked to use it. The revised reading now amplifies conclusion, stated reason, hidden assumption, and test with multiple examples: popularity/justice, Macbeth and prediction, old ideas, and confidence versus proof.
