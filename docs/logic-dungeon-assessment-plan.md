# Logic Dungeon Assessment Plan

## Status and purpose

This document proposes the full recurring assessment structure for the `scope_wr121` Whately logic course. It is a design plan, not a finished student assessment. Its immediate purpose is to establish the series architecture and secure teacher approval before drafting **Logic Dungeon I: The Hall of Inscriptions**, the cumulative assessment for Weeks 1–3.

The plan makes the first two chapters comparatively concrete because Weeks 1–6 already have prepared curriculum materials. Chapters III–IV remain provisional until their weekly readers, Logic Labs, and Lit Examples are finalized. Chapter V is the final cumulative destination after Week 17; its exact contents must follow the completed course.

---

## 1. Assessment philosophy

### Why the course needs the Logic Dungeon series

The normal weekly rhythm remains:

1. Student Reading handout
2. Logic Lab worksheet
3. Lit Example reader and worksheet
4. Periodic larger puzzle assessment

Weekly Logic Labs provide focused transfer practice. Lit Examples connect the tools to sustained literary reading. The Logic Dungeon series serves a different purpose: it asks students to choose and combine several recently learned tools inside one coherent problem.

A narrow weekly concept does not always support an interesting puzzle. A three-week cluster gives students enough intellectual equipment to diagnose layered errors without asking them to use concepts they have not yet studied. Therefore:

- There will not be one puzzle test for every week.
- A cumulative Logic Dungeon chapter will normally follow each three-week instructional cluster through Week 12. The end-of-course cadence is not yet fixed because Weeks 13–19 include four content weeks, literary synthesis, portfolio workshop, and portfolio defense rather than two clean three-week clusters.
- The Dungeon does not replace the Logic Portfolio; it produces evidence of transfer that may be reflected on or selected for the portfolio.
- Each chapter emphasizes its current cluster while deliberately reusing earlier habits.

### What these assessments should measure

A successful Logic Dungeon chapter measures whether students can:

- determine which logic tool a new situation requires;
- distinguish an argument's conclusion, reasons, and assumptions;
- keep terms stable and claims precise;
- separate inference from fact, testimony, observation, and judgment;
- diagnose why a tempting answer fails;
- repair reasoning rather than merely attach a fallacy label;
- synthesize several clues into a justified decision;
- explain the degree of support a conclusion has, not merely call it right or wrong.

The assessment should reward disciplined judgment. It should not reward guessing, speed, familiarity with game conventions, or the ability to decode ornate fantasy prose.

### Whately's humane limit

The series must preserve Whately's modest account of logic. The dungeon is not a world in which formal logic magically supplies every truth. Logic can test whether a conclusion follows, clarify terms, expose unsupported scope, and distinguish proof from apparent proof. It cannot by itself discover whether a map is accurate, whether a witness is honest, whether a premise is factually true, or whether an action is wise or just.

This limit is not an occasional disclaimer. It should become a recurring decision students must make: **What can be settled by examining the reasoning, and what requires evidence, observation, testimony, expertise, wisdom, or moral judgment?**

### Design principles

Every chapter should be:

- **Cumulative but bounded:** primarily assess the current cluster and spiral only skills students have already learned.
- **Transfer-based:** present unfamiliar claims and situations rather than repeat weekly examples.
- **Puzzle-like:** contain a coherent problem whose clues constrain a defensible solution.
- **Transparent about skills:** students should know which learned tools are available, without being told which answer or diagnosis to choose.
- **Repair-oriented:** include opportunities to clarify, narrow, rewrite, or strengthen defective reasoning.
- **Self-contained:** provide all story information needed to solve the logical problem while clearly marking facts that cannot be established from the handout alone.
- **Readable:** use short scenes, inscriptions, reports, testimony, and charts rather than long fantasy exposition.
- **Serious:** strange and exact in the spirit of Lewis Carroll, but not childish or jokey.
- **Fair:** make wrong answers plausible, avoid trivia traps, and ensure that narrative interpretation does not determine the grade.

### Recurring assessment shape

A typical chapter should occupy approximately 6–8 student pages and contain 5–7 stations plus a final decision. The exact length may change when the content is typeset.

Recommended structure:

1. Brief expedition update and current location
2. Skill inventory naming the tools students have learned
3. Compact rules/evidence sheet
4. Five to seven stations, including several marked danger stations
5. At least one explicit reasoning-repair opportunity
6. A final decision that uses evidence from multiple stations
7. A short reflection on the limit or proper use of logic

Student tasks should use direct verbs such as **identify, classify, explain, rewrite, test, repair, compare, decide,** and **support**.

## 2. Recurring adventure premise

A company of twenty explorers is seeking the hidden fortress called **Castrum Malae Ratiocinationis**. The path does not chiefly test strength. Its gates, tunnels, courts, archives, and towers are defended by inscriptions and speakers that imitate good reasoning while concealing defects.

Some warnings are false. Some are true but irrelevant. Some use one word in two senses. Some state a rule too broadly. Some arguments are valid but rest on false premises. Some witnesses sound confident but lack knowledge. The explorers survive by distinguishing what has actually been proved from what merely feels persuasive.

Each assessment is one chapter of the expedition. The class receives a new location and problem, but the core procedure remains stable:

1. Read what is given.
2. State the exact conclusion under consideration.
3. Identify the reasons and assumptions.
4. choose the appropriate logic tool.
5. Determine what follows and what does not.
6. Identify what additional evidence or judgment would be needed.
7. Repair weak reasoning where possible.

The narrative should advance between chapters without requiring students to remember minor plot details. Every chapter must provide enough context to stand alone. Returning motifs—inscriptions, gates, guides, maps, witnesses, archives, and disputed routes—should create continuity without becoming lore students must memorize.

## 3. The role of Castrum Malae Ratiocinationis

**Castrum Malae Ratiocinationis** is the expedition's final destination and the unifying image for the series.

Student-facing rules:

- Keep the Latin name untranslated.
- Present it as a serious and mysterious proper name.
- Do not turn the name into a joke or use its meaning as a clue.
- Students who independently look it up may understand the allusion, but translation is not an assessment task.

Design role:

- Early chapters reveal fragments of the route or signs that the fortress exists.
- Middle chapters show that the fortress is protected by increasingly sophisticated imitations of proof.
- Later chapters bring together verbal confusion, defective form, weak evidence, and persuasive rhetoric.
- The final chapter reaches the fortress and requires broad cumulative judgment.

The fortress should symbolize the course's central problem: bad reasoning is dangerous not because it always looks foolish, but because it can look orderly, confident, true, or morally urgent. Reaching its gates should therefore represent increased discernment rather than victory in a fantasy campaign.

The Logic Portfolio remains the course's official final product. The final Dungeon chapter should complement the portfolio by providing a common cumulative performance, while the portfolio documents growth, revision, and transfer over time.

## 4. Final five-assessment arc

The course will use four three-week checkpoint Dungeons through Week 12, followed by one cumulative final after Week 17. There is no separate Weeks 13–15 checkpoint; those later tools accumulate toward the final approach to the fortress.

### Logic Dungeon I: The Hall of Inscriptions

**Cluster:** Weeks 1–3

**Planning status:** Ready for prototype after approval

Primary tools:

- conclusion, stated reason, and hidden assumption;
- the follow-test;
- limits of logic;
- terms, ambiguity, and equivocation;
- stable-term rewrites;
- subject, predicate, quantity, and quality;
- A/E/I/O forms;
- scope errors involving All, None, Some, and unsupported Most claims.

Assessment problem: The explorers must interpret a sequence of inscriptions and reports to choose the defensible route through the first underground hall.

### Logic Dungeon II: The Syllogism Labyrinth

**Cluster:** Weeks 4–6

**Planning status:** Substantially plannable because Weeks 4–6 are prepared

Primary tools:

- premises and conclusion in practical syllogisms;
- major, minor, and middle terms;
- whether the middle term actually connects the premises;
- mood, figure, and selected valid or invalid forms;
- validity versus premise truth and soundness;
- form test versus fact test.

Assessment problem: Several routes are endorsed by arguments that appear structurally similar. Students must map the arguments, reject invalid connections, and avoid treating a valid form as proof that its premises are true.

Likely danger pattern: A perfectly formed argument contains a false or unverified map premise, while a true destination claim is supported by an invalid argument.

### Logic Dungeon III: The Court of the False Oracle

**Provisional cluster:** Weeks 7–9

**Planning status:** Theme only; finalize after weekly materials

Likely tools based on the current course sequence:

- fallacy as apparent proof rather than mere falsehood;
- equivocation at the level of a full argument or ambiguous middle;
- circular reasoning and begging the question;
- distinguishing rhetorical force from independent support.

Assessment problem: Competing interpreters defend an oracle's claims. Students must determine which arguments only appear to prove their conclusions and which revisions would provide independent support.

Reason to wait: The exact taxonomy, vocabulary, and difficulty must match the eventual Week 7–9 readers and Logic Labs.

### Logic Dungeon IV: The Archive of Broken Proofs

**Provisional cluster:** Weeks 10–12

**Planning status:** Theme only; finalize after weekly materials

Likely tools based on the current course sequence:

- irrelevant conclusion and the exact question at issue;
- analogy and relevant similarity;
- induction, examples, counterexamples, and claim size;
- repair of nearby claims and overgeneralizations.

Assessment problem: An archive contains records that are authentic but misfiled, comparisons that conceal relevant differences, and examples that do not justify universal conclusions. Students reconstruct which evidence supports which claim.

Reason to wait: Final station design should reflect the actual treatment of analogy and induction and should not import a modern critical-thinking framework that displaces Whately's sequence.

### Logic Dungeon V: The Gates of Castrum Malae Ratiocinationis

**Cluster:** Final cumulative assessment after Week 17 and before the Week 18 portfolio workshop

**Planning status:** Destination and principles fixed; exact contents follow the completed Weeks 13–17 materials

Primary purpose:

- cumulative use of terms, propositions, inference, validity, evidence, fallacy, testimony, dispute, and rhetoric;
- selection of the right diagnostic tool rather than response to a named category;
- explicit return to what logic can and cannot decide;
- synthesis across multiple records or claims;
- a final justified decision at the fortress gates.

The advanced material from Weeks 13–17 forms the final approach to the castle. **The Tower of Persuasion** may appear as a region or major station inside this final rather than as a separate assessment.

The final is a low-stakes common performance completed before portfolio defense. It gathers evidence of unaided tool selection and synthesis from a shared unfamiliar case, then feeds one revision or reflection into the official Logic Portfolio. It is not a second competing final exam. The portfolio remains the official final assessment because it demonstrates growth, revision, literary transfer, and self-reflection across the course.

The final chapter should not attempt to test every vocabulary item. It should sample the major habits in a small number of layered problems.

## 5. The twenty-adventurer mechanic

### Purpose

The expedition begins with twenty fictional adventurers. Their survival provides shared narrative stakes and a memorable way to discuss classwide patterns of reasoning. The mechanic must remain subordinate to the assessment and must never convert student mistakes into public humiliation.

### Recommended prototype model

Use a light combination of built-in danger stations and a classwide post-assessment expedition report.

Student-facing use:

- State that the expedition begins with twenty adventurers.
- Mark only a few stations as dangerous traps.
- Explain narratively that accepting a tempting argument may place an adventurer in danger.
- Include repair questions through which careful explanation can avert the danger.
- Do not require students to maintain a complicated running life count.

Teacher-facing use:

- Grade the logic work with a normal academic rubric.
- After grading, identify broad class patterns, such as widespread confusion about a shifting term or an overbroad All claim.
- Optionally narrate an expedition result based on those patterns.
- Keep the class story separate from individual grades.

### Safe classroom-use rules

1. **No student-to-adventurer mapping.** No adventurer represents a named student.
2. **No public attribution.** Never announce that a particular student's answer caused a loss.
3. **No speed penalty.** Careful revision should be favored over quick guessing.
4. **Repair can save.** A correct explanation, narrowed claim, stable-term rewrite, or identification of needed evidence can prevent a narrative loss.
5. **Narrative losses are not automatic grade deductions.** The academic rubric evaluates the actual reasoning shown.
6. **Classwide reporting stays conceptual.** Report that the expedition was endangered by “unsupported Most claims,” not by a list of students.
7. **No irreversible demoralization.** The party should remain capable of continuing the arc. Recovery can occur through correction, review, or strong performance at later stations.
8. **Teacher discretion.** The teacher may use the mechanic privately, lightly, or not at all without changing the assessment's academic validity.
9. **No graphic treatment.** The default terms are **endangered, separated, delayed,** or **trapped**, not killed or permanently lost.
10. **Accessibility and tone check.** If the class story distracts or creates anxiety, reduce it to a teacher-facing expedition report.

### Prototype survival rule

For Logic Dungeon I, designate three or four major danger stations. Each may endanger one adventurer if the central reasoning error is accepted without diagnosis. Repair is the default narrative: after students receive an opportunity to explain or correct the reasoning, the adventurer is rescued if the trap is cleared.

Optional reproducible classwide rule: a danger is cleared on first attempt if at least 70% of submitted papers correctly diagnose the central flaw; if not, it remains “endangered” until correction. If at least 70% then complete the repair successfully, report the adventurer rescued. The teacher may change or omit the threshold before administration, but should not improvise it after seeing results. A non-narrative version reports only classwide skill patterns.

The student handout should not ask for a continuously recalculated party total.

This rule must be described in the eventual answer key before it is used. The answer key should distinguish the narrative survival judgment from the numeric assessment score.

## 6. Detailed prototype plan: Logic Dungeon I

### Working title

**Logic Dungeon I: The Hall of Inscriptions**

### Placement

Use after students complete the Week 3 instructional cycle. The assessment is cumulative across Weeks 1–3 rather than a replacement for the Week 3 Logic Lab or Lit Example.

### Prototype objective

Students guide the expedition through the Hall of Inscriptions by diagnosing what a series of signs, guides, and records do and do not prove. The final route decision should depend on the logical work completed at earlier stations.

### Expected student-document shape

- Approximately 6–8 pages after typesetting
- Five to seven stations plus a final decision
- Short opening and compact evidence/rules panel
- Clear answer boxes or tables
- Three or four visibly marked danger stations
- No dice, character sheets, inventory system, combat rules, or branching-page mechanics
- A separate self-contained teacher answer key and scoring guide

### Skill inventory shown to students

The opening may remind students that they can use these learned tools:

- conclusion, stated reason, hidden assumption;
- whether the conclusion follows;
- what logic can test and what requires facts or judgment;
- stable and shifting terms;
- subject and predicate;
- quantity and quality;
- A, E, I, and O propositions;
- precise scope using All, None, Some, and carefully supported Most claims.

This inventory should not identify which station contains which flaw.

### Evidence architecture

The final tunnel decision should not be guessable from story atmosphere. Each station should yield a usable result, such as:

- one guide's argument is not sufficient;
- one warning changes the meaning of a central term;
- one universal rule must be narrowed;
- one observation supports only a particular claim;
- one map claim requires testimony or inspection;
- one route remains better supported, though not absolutely certain.

The supplied evidence set should be explicitly described as complete for the handout’s inference tasks. Some factual questions are intentionally undecidable; “cannot be settled from the supplied evidence” must be an available answer, and the key must identify every such question.

The final answer should require students to cite at least two earlier station results and distinguish “best supported” from “proven beyond all doubt.” No single station result should control the route. Apply a carry-forward rule: score an original station error at that station, but award synthesis credit when a student reasons consistently and accurately from the results they recorded rather than penalizing the same error twice.

### Administration and accessibility target

- Core version: one 55–70 minute class period.
- Optional extension: the final reflection or one repair item may be completed later if needed.
- Keep each fictional source short and visually distinct.
- Provide answer space proportionate to the expected response and permit ordinary documented accommodations, including extended time or typed responses, without reducing the logical demand.
- If draft timing exceeds the target during teacher review, reduce repeated classifications before removing synthesis or repair.

### Assessment balance

A reasonable prototype balance is:

- Week 1 tools: approximately 30–35%
- Week 2 tools: approximately 25–30%
- Week 3 tools: approximately 30–35%
- Combined synthesis and limits-of-logic judgment: integrated throughout

The percentages are planning targets, not point allocations. No station should become a disguised definition quiz.

## 7. Station-by-station outline for Weeks 1–3

The examples below specify each station's function and constraints. Exact inscriptions and answer choices belong in the later prototype draft.

### Opening: The Twenty Adventurers

The company reaches the first underground passage on the route to Castrum Malae Ratiocinationis. Twenty adventurers enter the Hall of Inscriptions. A brief rule explains that inscriptions may be false, ambiguous, overbroad, or true without proving the conclusion someone draws from them.

Student orientation:

- Read each statement exactly.
- Do not assume confidence, age, decoration, or official placement makes an inscription true.
- Record what follows and what remains unknown.
- At danger stations, repair may prevent a loss.

The opening should fit comfortably on the first page and avoid unnecessary names or lore.

### Station 1: The Gate of Applause

**Primary tool:** Week 1 argument skeleton and follow-test

**Danger:** Yes

A gatekeeper argues that a route must be wise or true because generations of travelers have applauded the inscription above it. The applause is a given fact; the conclusion drawn from it is the issue.

Required student work:

- identify the gatekeeper's conclusion;
- identify the stated reason;
- state the hidden assumption connecting applause to truth or wisdom;
- decide whether the conclusion follows;
- name one kind of evidence that would actually bear on the route's safety.

Design constraint: The argument should be tempting because repeated public approval is relevant to reputation or tradition, but it must not itself prove the route safe or the inscription true.

Repair opportunity: Narrow the conclusion to something the applause does support—for example, that the route or inscription has long been approved or believed.

### Station 2: The Two-Voiced Guide

**Primary tool:** Week 2 term stability and equivocation

**Danger:** Yes

A guide uses one ordinary word in two natural senses while recommending a passage. Good candidate words include **clear, sound, right, fair, free, light, safe,** or **certain**, but the final draft should select only a word that produces a plausible argument rather than a pun.

Required student work:

- identify the term that changes meaning without the prompt naming it;
- explain both meanings in context;
- rewrite the argument so the term has one stable meaning;
- decide whether the rewritten conclusion follows;
- state what additional fact, if any, would strengthen the conclusion.

Design constraint: Do not label the flaw as equivocation in the question. The first use of the word should sound natural and provisional, not create a direct contradiction merely to manufacture wordplay.

Repair opportunity: A stable-term rewrite can save the endangered adventurer even if the corrected argument no longer proves the original conclusion.

### Station 3: The Beast Ledger

**Primary tool:** Week 3 propositions, A/E/I/O, and scope

**Danger:** Yes

The explorers find a small ledger of observations about creatures or tunnel markers. The draft must label it either a **sample** or a **complete census**. A sample can establish claims about recorded creatures, support Some claims, and refute a universal with a counterexample; it cannot establish that no unrecorded creature exists. Several inscriptions convert the observations into categorical rules. The evidence must be explicit enough that students can test scope without needing outside zoological knowledge.

Required student work:

- identify subject and predicate in selected claims;
- classify selected claims as A, E, I, O, or a nonstandard Most claim;
- identify which All, None, or Most claim exceeds the ledger's evidence;
- rewrite one overbroad claim at a supportable scope;
- explain why one counterexample defeats a universal or universal-negative claim.

Design constraint: Include enough ledger entries to make the inference test determinate, but do not turn the task into tedious data counting. Distinguish “none observed” from “none exist,” and use forms such as “All creatures recorded in this ledger…” when the domain is limited. Avoid debatable real-world facts by creating all needed dungeon evidence inside the handout.

Repair opportunity: Replace an unsafe universal with a supported particular or explicitly limited claim.

### Station 4: The Shortcut Inscription

**Primary tools:** Week 1 follow-test plus Week 3 quantified scope

**Danger:** Optional

An inscription states a quantified rule about marked doors. A character then infers more than the rule licenses—for example, converting a Some claim into an All claim or treating absence of evidence as a None claim. The final draft must remain within the categorical and ordinary-language tools students have actually learned and must not require conditional-logic or conversion rules not yet taught.

Required student work:

- translate the key inscription into a precise quantified claim;
- state the character's conclusion;
- decide exactly what follows and what does not;
- surface the assumption required for the stronger conclusion;
- repair the conclusion without inventing evidence.

Design constraint: Do not require formal conditional logic terminology if it has not been taught. The reasoning should be expressible through scope and the Week 1 follow-test.

### Station 5: The Mapmaker's Oath

**Primary tools:** Week 1 limits of logic plus Week 2 stable terms

**Danger:** No; this is a judgment station

A mapmaker offers a confident oath that the map is “true,” “sound,” or “faithful.” The wording may be stable or may conceal a shift, but the central issue is that logic alone cannot inspect the tunnel or certify the mapmaker's honesty and competence.

Required student work:

- identify what claim the oath actually makes;
- test whether the stated reason supports that claim;
- distinguish what logic can evaluate from what requires observation, testimony, or corroboration;
- name a relevant check on the map;
- avoid the opposite overreach of declaring the map false merely because it is unverified.

Design constraint: The strongest answer should be appropriately suspended or conditional, not cynically distrustful. Lack of proof is not proof of falsity.

### Station 6: The Door of Narrow Claims

**Primary tool:** Integrated repair

**Danger:** Recovery station

Students receive two or three unsafe expedition claims drawn from prior stations. They must revise them into claims that are clear, stable, and proportionate to the evidence.

Required student work for each selected claim:

- preserve the claim's subject matter;
- remove ambiguity or state the intended meaning;
- correct its quantity or quality;
- identify any remaining evidence needed;
- briefly explain why the revision is safer.

This station provides a fair recovery opportunity for the adventurer mechanic and reveals whether students can improve reasoning instead of only criticize it.

### Final Decision: The Supported Tunnel

**Primary tool:** Synthesis

**Danger:** Final expedition decision

Students choose among two or three tunnels using the conclusions established at earlier stations. One route should be best supported, but not necessarily proved absolutely safe. At least one tempting route should depend on applause, ambiguity, or overbroad scope. Another may lack enough evidence to choose confidently.

Required student work:

- choose the best-supported route, or explicitly justify why no route is yet supportable if that is the designed solution;
- cite results from at least two named stations;
- explain why one rejected route rests on defective reasoning;
- distinguish what the available logic establishes from what remains a factual risk;
- state one additional observation or testimony that would most improve the decision.

Scoring constraint: Credit must depend on the quality of cited reasoning. A tunnel letter without an explanation earns little or no credit, even if guessed correctly.

Carry-forward constraint: An earlier wrong answer is scored where it occurs. If the final explanation reasons coherently from that recorded result and uses the other evidence accurately, award the corresponding synthesis credit rather than counting the same misconception twice.

### Final reflection

One short prompt should ask students to complete or explain a principle such as:

> Careful reasoning helped the expedition decide ______, but it could not by itself establish ______.

The answer key should permit several well-supported answers and distinguish specific reasoning from vague claims that “logic cannot know anything.”

## 8. Prototype assessment blueprint

The content draft should use this blueprint to protect validity without locking points before the exact items exist:

| Learning target | Primary evidence | Observable student action | Approximate weight |
|---|---|---|---:|
| Argument skeleton, hidden assumption, follow-test | Stations 1 and 4 | Identify parts, state the bridge assumption, judge support | 20–25% |
| Stable terms and equivocation | Stations 2 and 5 | Find the shift, explain both senses, rewrite and retest | 15–20% |
| Subject/predicate, quantity/quality, A/E/I/O | Station 3 plus one mixed item | Analyze proposition parts and classify accurately | 15–20% |
| Scope and evidence-sized repair | Stations 3, 4, and 6 | Distinguish sample from census, narrow overreach, preserve what evidence supports | 15–20% |
| Limits of logic | Stations 1 and 5 | Separate inferential judgment from fact, observation, testimony, or moral judgment | 10–15% |
| Tool selection and synthesis | Mixed station(s) and final decision | Select a diagnostic tool, integrate multiple clues, state remaining uncertainty | 15–20% |

At least one station should mix tools or vary the instructional order so the assessment is not simply Week 1, then Week 2, then Week 3. Exact point values should be assigned only after drafting and timing the tasks.

## 9. Grading and answer-key plan

The prototype's answer key must stand on its own. It should begin by defining local grading language before using it. In particular:

- **Follow-test:** Ask whether the stated reason, together with any defensible assumption, actually supports the conclusion claimed.
- **Stable-term test:** Ask whether a key word keeps the same meaning throughout the reasoning.
- **Scope test:** Ask whether the claim's quantity—All, None, Some, or Most—is no broader than the evidence supports.
- **Repair:** A revision that clarifies the term, narrows the claim, corrects the inference, or identifies needed support without changing the subject merely to avoid the problem.

Each open response should describe:

- **Strong answer:** exact conclusion or flaw, accurate explanation, and evidence-based repair or limitation.
- **Partial answer:** correct judgment with incomplete explanation, or a plausible repair that does not fully identify the original failure.
- **Weak answer:** label without explanation, unsupported tunnel choice, invented evidence, or reversal from “not proved” to “false.”

The key should explain why each tempting answer is tempting and why it fails. It should also include:

- accepted variants for stable-term rewrites;
- exact A/E/I/O classifications where applicable;
- the evidentiary limit of each ledger claim;
- a model final synthesis response;
- a separate note for translating station performance into the optional classwide expedition report;
- a reminder that survival narration must not identify individual students.

A point structure should be designed with the prototype, after the exact number and complexity of tasks are known. The plan should not force arbitrary equal weighting among stations.

## 10. Student-facing writing and layout rules

The eventual prototype should follow these rules:

- Keep the story compact; most page space belongs to reasoning and response areas.
- Use mature, direct prose appropriate for 12th grade.
- Keep the opening premise together when possible.
- Start major work sections cleanly rather than splitting a station across pages.
- Use visible station headings and consistent icons or labels for danger and repair, but avoid decorative clutter.
- Do not identify the expected diagnosis in advance.
- Do not name the shifting term when identification is the task.
- Do not use absurd distractors that reward test-taking rather than reasoning.
- Give every needed fictional fact in the handout.
- Mark clearly when students must identify information that would require observation or testimony.
- Include enough writing space for the expected answer.
- Use answer tables only when structure helps; use prose boxes for explanation and synthesis.

The student test and teacher key should eventually be built as clean LaTeX/PDF artifacts, but content approval comes before final typesetting.

## 11. Relationship to weekly work and the Logic Portfolio

The Dungeon should not repeat weekly assignments verbatim.

- Student Readings introduce and explain the week's tool.
- Logic Labs provide focused transfer on fresh examples.
- Lit Examples direct attention toward logical problems in *Macbeth* or *Julius Caesar* without completing the worksheet analysis for students.
- Logic Dungeon chapters ask students to select and combine tools in a common unfamiliar case.
- The Logic Portfolio records growth across these forms of work.

The Dungeons intentionally emphasize non-literary transfer cases so success does not depend on remembering plot details. The recurring *Macbeth* and *Julius Caesar* anchors remain central in Lit Examples and the portfolio; a short post-Dungeon reflection may connect one Dungeon diagnosis to a Shakespearean case.

Possible portfolio connection after each Dungeon:

- Students choose one diagnosis or repair to revise after feedback.
- They add a short caption explaining which tool they selected and why.
- They may compare their first answer with the repaired answer.

This should remain a small reflective bridge, not an additional full assignment unless the teacher requests one.

After this plan is approved, Logic Dungeon I will be ready for teacher review only if it includes:

- a compact serious opening involving twenty adventurers;
- 5–7 coherent stations and a final decision;
- at least one conclusion/reason/hidden-assumption chart;
