## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

- **Analogy:** reasoning from a likeness between two cases.
- **Source case:** the familiar or comparison case used to illuminate the argument.
- **Target case:** the case, person, decision, or policy the speaker wants judged.
- **Relevant similarity:** the likeness that matters for the conclusion being argued.
- **Disanalogy:** an important difference between source and target that limits the comparison.
- **Overextension:** forcing an analogy to prove a broader claim than its relevant similarity supports.
- **Decorative comparison:** a vivid comparison that adds style or emphasis but does little proof-work.
- **Review terms:** prior-week skills only: irrelevant proof / missing the point and invalid converse-style syllogistic structure.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Lunch schedule and traffic pattern (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Source case: changing the traffic pattern outside school. Target case: changing the lunch schedule. Conclusion: the school should publish the new lunch schedule before the change begins. Students may add that the relevant similarity is that both changes require clear advance directions to avoid confusion.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Correctly identifies source, target, and conclusion
- 1 pt: Identifies two of the three elements
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – Captain and point guard (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Relevant similarity: both roles require awareness of the whole team/field, communication, and decisions that are not reducible to personal statistics. Important disanalogy: a point guard is a specific playing position during a game, while team captain is a broader leadership role that may involve conduct, practice habits, and off-court responsibilities.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: States relevant similarity and a real limiting disanalogy
- 1 pt: Gives only similarity or only disanalogy
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Dress code and job uniform (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Similarity: both involve clothing expectations tied to public conduct or professionalism. Disanalogy: students are not employees; school discipline is formative; violations differ in seriousness; a workplace uniform rule does not automatically justify detention for every school dress-code violation. The analogy may support some standards but not an automatic penalty.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Similarity plus disanalogy that limits the penalty conclusion
- 1 pt: Partial
- 0 pts: Treats the job comparison as automatically decisive

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Phones and candy (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The analogy supports at least a narrower claim: visible temptations can distract a group even if a few individuals resist them, so a phone plan during quizzes is reasonable. Strong answers may say it comes close to proving the stated practical conclusion if the claim is only that some plan is needed, not that the harshest possible ban is justified.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Judges the analogy as supporting a narrow/practical claim and explains why
- 1 pt: Says “good analogy” or “bad analogy” with little explanation
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – College and winter coat (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The analogy overextends. Choosing a college and choosing a coat may both involve price and fit, so the comparison may support considering cost. It does not prove the cheapest option is always smartest, because college choice also involves program quality, aid, debt, distance, fit, graduation rates, and long-term consequences.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Identifies overextension and gives relevant disanalogies
- 1 pt: Notes price is not everything but weakly explains
- 0 pts: Accepts cheapest-always conclusion

## Question 7: Part 4 Q1 – Tutoring garden paragraph (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Conclusion: start the tutoring program with ninth graders only, measure results, then expand. Source case: planting one section of a garden/field first. Target case: launching a tutoring program. Relevant similarity: a small pilot can teach what works before scaling. Disanalogy: students are not plants; older students may have different needs; educational programs involve fairness, urgency, staffing, and varied learners. Best judgment: the analogy gives real but limited support for a pilot program, especially with added evidence about ninth-grade need, but it does not by itself settle every fairness or priority concern.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Includes conclusion, source/target, similarity, disanalogy, and balanced judgment
- 1 pt: Includes some elements but incomplete judgment
- 0 pts: Plot/paraphrase only or incorrect

## Question 9: Part 5 Q2 – Review: reliable sources (1 point)

**Expected Answer:**
Structure: Only reliable sources correct errors publicly; this website corrected an error publicly; therefore this website is reliable. This is invalid converse-style reasoning / affirming the predicate or undistributed-middle problem. The conclusion does not follow from the stated premise.

**Rubric:**
- 1 pt: Identifies structural problem and says the conclusion does not follow
- 0 pts: Says valid without qualification or misses structure
