# Source Notes — Whately, Elements of Logic, Week 2

## Primary source

Richard Whately, *Elements of Logic*. This unit draws primarily from Book I ("Of Terms"), especially Whately's treatment of the meaning of terms, ambiguity, and the danger of using the same word in different senses within a single argument (equivocation).

## Week 2 focus

Week 2 builds directly on the four-step analysis habit taught in Week 1 by zooming in on one of the most common reasons arguments fail the "test" step: unclear or shifting terms. The reading teaches students to notice when a key word is being used in two different senses and to "fix the term" before evaluating the reasoning.

## Adaptation approach

The student reading is adapted and modernized for the revised 12th-grade long-unit plan. It preserves Whately's core insight that many apparent disagreements and faulty inferences arise not from bad logic per se, but from words that carry multiple meanings. Multiple original examples are provided so students can practice the skill of identifying and repairing ambiguous terms. The revised lab and reading also connect this habit to recurring Shakespeare anchors, especially terms such as fate, manhood, honor, ambition, liberty, and tyranny in *Macbeth* and *Julius Caesar*.

## Connection to Week 1

This unit treats ambiguity as a major source of the "hidden assumptions" students learned to surface in Week 1. By making terms precise, students make the hidden assumptions visible and testable.

## Macbeth lit-anchor sources

The Week 2 Macbeth lit-anchor, "Words That Lie Like Truth," uses original Shakespeare text from *Macbeth* Act 4, Scene 1 and Act 5, Scene 8. The guided passage focuses on the apparitions' ambiguous promises: "none of woman born" and "Birnam wood to high Dunsinane hill / Shall come." The independent passage focuses on Macduff's revelation and Macbeth's recognition that the fiends "palter with us in a double sense."
