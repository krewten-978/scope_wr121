# Source Notes — Whately, Elements of Logic, Week 3

## Primary Source
This unit draws from Book II of Richard Whately’s *Elements of Logic*, which treats the nature of propositions, their division according to quality and quantity, and the distribution of terms.

## Focus of the Unit
Week 3 introduces students to the structure of propositions as the basic units of reasoning. The goal is not formal symbolic logic but practical clarity in stating and understanding claims. Students learn to identify subject and predicate, distinguish affirmative from negative and universal from particular statements, recognize the four standard forms, and translate ordinary language into precise logical form.

## Adaptation Approach
The student reading expands on Whately’s treatment by providing numerous concrete examples for each concept. Whately’s original text is relatively concise; this version adds extended illustration and practice in translation because students benefit from seeing the same idea demonstrated in multiple contexts before being asked to apply it independently. The revised version is aligned to a 12th-grade long-unit plan and includes recurring *Macbeth* and *Julius Caesar* applications.

## Pedagogical Emphasis
Special attention is given to the difference between everyday language and logical form. Many students initially resist the idea that “some” means “at least one” or that “all S are P” does not imply “all P are S.” The unit therefore includes repeated practice in identifying what is actually being asserted about the subject class. This revision also corrects the O-proposition distribution rule: in “Some S are not P,” the subject is undistributed and the predicate is distributed.
