# Scope and Sequence — Whately's *Elements of Logic*

## Working unit title

**Thinking Straight: Richard Whately and the Art of Reasoning**

## Unit purpose

This unit uses Richard Whately's *Elements of Logic* as the spine for a tenth-grade course in disciplined reasoning. The goal is not to turn students into technical logicians for its own sake. The goal is to train students to read, write, speak, and judge arguments with greater care.

Whately is especially useful because he presents logic as both modest and powerful. Logic cannot discover every truth, supply missing facts, or make a foolish person wise. But it can teach a thinker to examine whether a conclusion really follows from the reasons offered. That makes the unit valuable for literature, history, rhetoric, civic argument, theology, philosophy, and ordinary classroom discussion.

## Central question

**How can we tell the difference between real reasoning, verbal confusion, and persuasive nonsense?**

## Final unit question

**What does it mean to be a disciplined thinker?**

## Grade level and format

- **Intended level:** 10th grade.
- **Primary format:** weekly student reading handout, argument/focus lab, teacher guide, source notes, and PDF deliverables.
- **Reading approach:** adapted and modernized from Whately rather than quoted continuously, while preserving the order of thought and major distinctions.
- **Student work pattern:** each week should ask students to produce something: an argument skeleton, a term definition, a proposition translation, a fallacy autopsy, a repaired argument, a window note, a paragraph claim, or a field-guide entry.

## Existing sequence status

The first three weeks are already represented in the repository.

### Week 1 — Logic Is Not Magic

- **Source base:** Whately's introductory defense of logic.
- **Essential question:** What can logic actually do for a thinker, and what should we not expect it to do?
- **Core idea:** Logic studies reasoning as reasoning. It helps us ask whether a conclusion follows from premises, but it does not supply missing facts or replace wisdom.
- **Student skill:** identify conclusion, stated reason, hidden assumption, and test whether the conclusion follows.
- **Major product:** argument skeleton and exit claim paragraph.
- **Current repo status:** built.

### Week 2 — One Word, Two Ideas

- **Source base:** Whately's treatment of terms and ambiguity.
- **Essential question:** How can the same word mean two different things inside a single argument, and what can we do about it?
- **Core idea:** Arguments often fail because a key term shifts meaning without being noticed.
- **Student skill:** identify equivocation, fix the meaning of a term, and then retest the argument.
- **Major product:** term-fixing exercise and original equivocation example.
- **Current repo status:** built.
- **Revision note before further buildout:** the student reading source note should be aligned with Book I / Whately's treatment of terms rather than the Introduction.

### Week 3 — What We Say and What We Mean

- **Source base:** Whately's treatment of propositions.
- **Essential question:** How can we state claims clearly enough that others know exactly what we are asserting?
- **Core idea:** Before an argument can be judged, its claims must be made precise. Students must know what the statement is about, what is being said, whether it affirms or denies, and whether it concerns all, none, or some of the subject.
- **Student skill:** identify subject, predicate, quality, quantity, and the A/E/I/O forms of categorical propositions.
- **Major product:** proposition translation and window note.
- **Current repo status:** built.
- **Revision note before syllogisms:** correct the O-proposition distribution point. In standard categorical logic, the O form, “Some S are not P,” leaves the subject undistributed and distributes the predicate.

## Proposed full-unit sequence

The sequence below extends the current three weeks into a complete logic unit. It keeps Whately as the main guide while making the progression classroom-friendly.

### Week 4 — Two Claims and a Conclusion

- **Likely title:** *Because: How Syllogisms Work*
- **Source base:** Whately on arguments and syllogistic structure.
- **Essential question:** When do two claims actually force a conclusion?
- **Core concepts:** syllogism, major term, minor term, middle term, major premise, minor premise, conclusion, validity, truth.
- **Core idea:** A syllogism tests whether a conclusion follows from two propositions because of their form and shared terms.
- **Student skill:** separate premises from conclusion; identify the middle term; distinguish valid form from true content.
- **Student product:** syllogism map with labeled major/minor/middle terms and a short explanation of whether the conclusion follows.
- **Why it belongs here:** Weeks 1-3 have prepared the tools: conclusion/reason, stable terms, and clear propositions. Week 4 combines those tools.

### Week 5 — Valid Does Not Mean True

- **Likely title:** *Good Form, Bad Facts*
- **Source base:** Whately on the province of logic and the distinction between formal correctness and material truth.
- **Essential question:** Can an argument be logically valid and still not prove what we should believe?
- **Core concepts:** validity, soundness, false premise, true conclusion, valid form, invalid form.
- **Core idea:** Logic can show whether a conclusion follows from premises, but other disciplines are needed to determine whether the premises are true.
- **Student skill:** classify arguments as valid/invalid and then ask whether the premises are true.
- **Student product:** two-column argument diagnosis: form test and fact test.
- **Why it belongs here:** It prevents students from thinking syllogisms are truth machines and returns to Whately's modest account of logic.

### Week 6 — When an Argument Looks Stronger Than It Is

- **Likely title:** *What Is a Fallacy?*
- **Source base:** Whately's opening treatment of fallacies.
- **Essential question:** Why do bad arguments often feel persuasive?
- **Core concepts:** fallacy, appearance of proof, defect in reasoning, emotional force, rhetorical force.
- **Core idea:** A fallacy is not merely a false statement. It is reasoning that seems to prove something but fails to do so.
- **Student skill:** distinguish false claims from bad inferences and explain where the reasoning breaks.
- **Student product:** fallacy autopsy: conclusion, offered reason, hidden premise, point of failure.
- **Why it belongs here:** Students are now ready to diagnose failure because they know terms, propositions, and basic argument structure.

### Week 7 — The Word That Changed Meaning Halfway Through

- **Likely title:** *Equivocation and the Ambiguous Middle*
- **Source base:** Whately's treatment of ambiguity and fallacies involving language.
- **Essential question:** How can a single shifting word make an argument appear valid?
- **Core concepts:** equivocation, ambiguous middle, verbal ambiguity, same word / different idea.
- **Core idea:** Some arguments appear to connect premises and conclusions only because one term is quietly used in two senses.
- **Student skill:** expose the shifting term and rewrite the argument with fixed meanings.
- **Student product:** ambiguous-middle diagnosis and repaired version.
- **Why it belongs here:** This revisits Week 2 at a higher level. Students first learned to fix terms; now they see why term-shifts corrupt full arguments.

### Week 8 — When the Proof Secretly Assumes the Answer

- **Likely title:** *Circular Reasoning and Begging the Question*
- **Source base:** Whately on petitio principii and arguments that assume what they must prove.
- **Essential question:** How can an argument sound like proof while merely repeating its conclusion?
- **Core concepts:** circular reasoning, begging the question, assumed conclusion, hidden premise.
- **Core idea:** An argument fails when the reason only works if the conclusion is already granted.
- **Student skill:** identify when a premise smuggles in the conclusion and rewrite the argument so it gives independent support.
- **Student product:** circular-argument rewrite.
- **Why it belongs here:** It deepens the hidden-assumption work from Week 1 and gives students a common tool for essay revision.

### Week 9 — Answering a Question No One Asked

- **Likely title:** *Irrelevant Proof and Missing the Point*
- **Source base:** Whately on ignoratio elenchi and irrelevant conclusion.
- **Essential question:** How can an argument prove something and still fail to prove the point at issue?
- **Core concepts:** irrelevant conclusion, question at issue, redirection, partial proof, nearby claim.
- **Core idea:** A speaker may prove a true or interesting point while failing to prove the conclusion that actually needed proof.
- **Student skill:** identify the exact question under debate and determine whether the evidence answers it.
- **Student product:** prompt-match diagnosis: claim, actual proof, missing question, repaired proof.
- **Why it belongs here:** This connects directly to student writing. Many weak essays prove a nearby claim instead of the assigned claim.

### Week 10 — Finding Evidence Is Not the Same as Proving a Claim

- **Likely title:** *Induction, Examples, and General Claims*
- **Source base:** Whately's discussion of induction and its relation to reasoning.
- **Essential question:** When do examples justify a general conclusion?
- **Core concepts:** induction, example, observation, generalization, evidence, counterexample.
- **Core idea:** Evidence matters, but examples must be weighed carefully. A few cases do not automatically prove a universal claim.
- **Student skill:** judge whether evidence supports the size of the conclusion being drawn.
- **Student product:** evidence ladder: example, pattern, limited claim, overclaim, revised claim.
- **Why it belongs here:** It moves from deductive form to the kind of evidence students often use in literary, historical, and civic argument.

### Week 11 — Can Logic Discover Anything New?

- **Likely title:** *Reasoning, Discovery, and Judgment*
- **Source base:** Whately's discussion of the discovery of truth and the limits of reasoning alone.
- **Essential question:** Does reasoning discover truth, or does it only arrange what we already know?
- **Core concepts:** discovery, proof, observation, testimony, memory, imagination, judgment.
- **Core idea:** Logic helps test and arrange thought, but discovery often requires observation, experience, testimony, and imagination.
- **Student skill:** distinguish kinds of intellectual work: discovering facts, defining terms, drawing inferences, testing proof, judging application.
- **Student product:** intellectual-tool map showing what logic can and cannot do in a real inquiry.
- **Why it belongs here:** This returns to Week 1 at a more mature level and prevents overclaiming for formal logic.

### Week 12 — Are We Actually Disagreeing?

- **Likely title:** *Verbal and Real Disputes*
- **Source base:** Whately on verbal and real questions.
- **Essential question:** How can we tell whether people disagree about reality or only about words?
- **Core concepts:** verbal dispute, real dispute, definition, contested term, shared claim, genuine disagreement.
- **Core idea:** Some arguments continue because people use words differently; others remain even after terms are clarified because the disagreement is real.
- **Student skill:** classify a disagreement as verbal, real, or mixed; clarify terms; identify what remains disputed.
- **Student product:** dispute map with contested terms, clarified definitions, and remaining real disagreement.
- **Why it belongs here:** This gives the unit a powerful civic and classroom application: students learn how to reduce confusion without pretending all disagreements disappear.

### Week 13 — Logic Field Manual / Final Synthesis

- **Likely title:** *What It Means to Think Straight*
- **Source base:** Whately as a whole, especially the recurring distinction between real reasoning and apparent proof.
- **Essential question:** What habits make someone a disciplined thinker?
- **Core concepts:** disciplined reasoning, proof, terms, propositions, inference, evidence, fallacy, disagreement.
- **Core idea:** Logic is a set of intellectual habits: define terms, state claims clearly, test inference, inspect evidence, notice hidden assumptions, and distinguish real disputes from verbal confusion.
- **Student skill:** synthesize the unit into a reusable method for reading and writing arguments.
- **Student product options:**
  1. Logic field manual.
  2. Final argument paper.
  3. Literary logic application.
  4. Public-argument analysis portfolio.
- **Why it belongs here:** The unit should end with transfer. Students should be able to use Whately outside the logic unit.

## Optional extension weeks

If the course needs to be more comprehensive, the following weeks could be added after Week 13 or folded into earlier weeks.

### Extension A — Analogy and Comparison

- **Question:** When does a comparison actually prove something?
- **Use case:** literature, history, civic argument, classroom discussion.
- **Why add it:** Students often reason by analogy without testing whether the compared cases are alike in relevant ways.

### Extension B — Testimony and Authority

- **Question:** When should we trust a source?
- **Use case:** research papers, historical claims, news, expert disagreement.
- **Why add it:** Whately's logic can support a broader unit on evidence and credibility.

### Extension C — Rhetoric and Logic

- **Question:** How do style, emotion, and persuasion relate to proof?
- **Use case:** speeches, advertising, political argument, literary rhetoric.
- **Why add it:** This would connect Whately to rhetoric and prevent students from assuming persuasive force equals logical strength.

### Extension D — Literary Self-Deception

- **Question:** How do characters reason badly about themselves?
- **Use case:** Macbeth, Julius Caesar, Antigone, Oedipus, The Odyssey.
- **Why add it:** It makes logic a tool for literary reading rather than an isolated technical unit.

## Comprehensive-enough test

Before deciding that the unit is complete, use the following questions.

A student who finishes the unit should be able to:

1. Explain what logic can and cannot do.
2. Identify the conclusion and stated reasons in a simple argument.
3. Surface hidden assumptions.
4. Fix ambiguous terms.
5. State claims in clear proposition form.
6. Distinguish universal, particular, affirmative, and negative claims.
7. Identify basic syllogistic structure.
8. Distinguish validity from truth.
9. Explain why a fallacy can be persuasive.
10. Diagnose equivocation.
11. Diagnose circular reasoning.
12. Diagnose irrelevant proof.
13. Judge whether evidence supports the size of a claim.
14. Distinguish verbal disputes from real disputes.
15. Repair weak arguments by narrowing claims, clarifying terms, or adding relevant support.
16. Apply logic to literature, public speech, classroom debate, and their own writing.

If the unit reaches those outcomes, it is comprehensive enough for a strong tenth-grade introduction to logic as disciplined reasoning. It would not be a complete college course in formal logic, but that is not the goal. The unit would give students a durable toolkit for argument, reading, writing, and discussion.

## Possible final assessments

### Option 1 — Logic Field Manual

Students create a field manual for disciplined thinking. Each entry must include a definition, a student-created example, a diagnosis method, and a repaired version when relevant.

Required entries:

- conclusion and premise
- hidden assumption
- ambiguous term
- proposition
- syllogism
- validity vs. truth
- fallacy
- equivocation
- circular reasoning
- irrelevant proof
- induction and overclaiming
- verbal vs. real dispute

### Option 2 — Final Argument Paper

**Prompt:** Which danger most often prevents people from thinking clearly: ambiguous words, hidden assumptions, irrelevant proof, or weak evidence? Defend your answer using Whately and at least two examples from literature, public argument, history, or classroom life.

Requirements:

- clear thesis
- at least two Whately concepts
- at least two concrete examples
- fair treatment of an opposing answer
- explanation of why the chosen danger is especially serious

### Option 3 — Literary Logic Application

Students choose a character or speaker from a major text and analyze the reasoning behind that character's choice, self-justification, or persuasion.

Possible texts:

- *Macbeth*
- *Julius Caesar*
- *Antigone*
- *Oedipus Rex*
- *The Odyssey*
- a public speech or trial scene

Required analysis:

- claim or conclusion
- stated reason
- hidden assumption
- key terms
- evidence used or ignored
- fallacy or weakness, if present
- repaired argument or clearer judgment

### Option 4 — Public Argument Portfolio

Students collect three short public arguments and analyze each using Whately's tools.

For each argument:

- What is the conclusion?
- What reasons are offered?
- Are the key terms stable?
- Is the claim clear in quantity and quality?
- Does the conclusion follow?
- Is the evidence strong enough?
- Is the disagreement verbal, real, or mixed?
