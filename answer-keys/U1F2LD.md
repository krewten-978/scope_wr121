## Grading Vocabulary

- **Minor term (S):** Subject of the conclusion.
- **Major term (P):** Predicate of the conclusion.
- **Middle term (M):** Term in both premises but not the conclusion; it is supposed to connect S and P.
- **Major premise / minor premise:** Premise containing P / premise containing S.
- **Mood:** A/E/I/O pattern in major-premise, minor-premise, conclusion order. A = universal affirmative; E = universal negative; I = particular affirmative; O = particular negative.
- **Figure:** Placement of M. Figure 1 is M–P / S–M. Figure 2 is P–M / S–M.
- **Form test:** If the premises were granted, would the conclusion have to follow?
- **Fact test:** Are the premises established, doubtful, or false?
- **Undistributed middle:** M never refers to its whole class, so sharing M does not connect S and P. Beginner pattern: All P are M; All S are M; therefore All S are P.
- **Two negative premises:** Two premises that only separate classes cannot establish the required connection between S and P.
- **Pressure point:** Premise most in need of factual proof and most important to the argument's success.
- **Valid:** The conclusion follows from the premises because of form. Validity alone does not make premises true.
- **Sound:** Valid form plus true or established premises.
- **Not yet proved / valid but doubtful:** Valid form with at least one needed premise unsupported.
- **Invalid with a true observation:** A premise can report a fact while the form still fails to prove the conclusion.
- **Route consequence:** What the expedition may record, investigate, rule out, or risk because of the analysis.
- **Best supported:** Strongest conclusion under the supplied evidence; not a guarantee of safety.
- **Carry-forward reasoning:** Score an early error once. In later synthesis, award credit for reasoning consistently from the student's recorded result while accurately handling other evidence; do not deduct twice for one misconception.
