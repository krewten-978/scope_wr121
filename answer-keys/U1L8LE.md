**Purpose:** Students apply Week 8 equivocation / ambiguous-middle diagnosis to Macbeth’s prophetic language: *fair*, present vs future titles, and the security promises.

## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

- **Equivocation:** one word used in two senses inside one chain of reasoning.
- **Ambiguous middle:** a linking term that only appears to be the same idea in both premises.
- **Sense A / Sense B:** two ordinary meanings of a contested term.
- **Fixed-meaning rewrite:** restating the claim with one sense held steady.
- **Present fact vs future claim:** what is already true versus what is only promised or predicted.
- **Retest:** ask whether the original conclusion still follows after senses are fixed.

Plot summary alone does not earn points.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Two senses of fair (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Possible senses include: morally good / just; attractive or pleasant; promising or fortunate-sounding; clear weather vs foul weather (paired with foul). Strong answers give two distinct ordinary senses tied to the passage.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Two clear senses
- 1 pt: One clear sense or two vague senses
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Fact vs future (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Glamis is present fact (already true for Macbeth). Cawdor is treated as news/near fact in the play’s sequence; “king hereafter” is future claim. The difference matters because a stable argument cannot treat a future title as if it were already proved by the same kind of fact as Glamis.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Correctly separates present/future and explains why it matters
- 1 pt: Partial separation
- 0 pts: Treats all three titles as the same kind of claim

**Notes for grading:**
- Do not require detailed Cawdor plot beyond the passage’s surface.

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – Risky inference (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Informal reconstruction such as: Those greeted with these fair/prophetic titles are destined for greatness / kingship; Macbeth is so greeted; therefore Macbeth will be king (or should act to become king). Linking term: *fair* prophecy, *hail*/title language, or promised kingship.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Coherent reconstruction + linking term
- 1 pt: Vague inference without a clear linking term
- 0 pts: Plot only

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Why unstable (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The link is unstable because *fair*/prophetic greeting shifts between attractive speech, supernatural pressure, present title, and future claim. Greeting is not a stable middle that proves crime is required or that kingship is already secured as fact.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Clear term-shift / ambiguous-middle explanation
- 1 pt: Says “prophecy is tricky” without term analysis
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – None of woman born (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Sense A (Macbeth): no person born of a woman in the ordinary way can harm him / he is safe from men. Sense B (undercutting): someone not “born” in the ordinary sense (Macduff) can still harm him. Accept equivalent classroom wording.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Two distinct senses, one reassuring and one undercutting
- 1 pt: One sense only
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – Birnam wood (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Sense A: the forest will not literally uproot and walk. Sense B: soldiers using branches as camouflage / wood “coming” against the hill. Under Sense A, “never” seems safe; under Sense B, the guarantee fails. Original confidence does not follow under both fixed senses.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Both senses + retest judgment
- 1 pt: Senses without retest
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 7: Part 4 Q1 – Repair (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Example repair: “No one born by ordinary childbirth will harm Macbeth” is still not a proved universal safety claim; or “Macbeth is safe only if every enemy is of woman born in the ordinary sense and Birnam wood never advances in any sense.” Strong repairs remove false guarantee.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Fixed-meaning rewrite that blocks false safety
- 1 pt: Partial repair
- 0 pts: Restates the prophecy unchanged

## Question 8: Part 4 Q2 – Capstone (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong paragraph explains that prophetic lines can look like proof because repeated fair/security words seem stable, but Week 8 requires fixing senses and retesting. Once senses split, the argument from words to guaranteed safety (or necessary crime) fails.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Clear Week 8 tool applied to these passages
- 1 pt: General statement with thin passage use
- 0 pts: Magic/plot summary only
