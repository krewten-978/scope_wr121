# U1L17LE – Lit Example Worksheet Answer Key & Rubric

**Total Points:** 16

**Paired reader:** `units/elements-of-logic-week17/lit-examples/U1L17LE_lit_example_reader.tex` / `.pdf`.

**Paired worksheet:** `graded-assignments/U1L17LE/worksheet.tex` / `.pdf` (Assignment ID **U1L17LE**).

**Purpose:** Students apply Week 17 multi-tool self-deception autopsy to Macbeth’s private reasoning before Duncan’s murder and Brutus’s orchard justification of preventive killing.

## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

- **Self-deception autopsy:** desire; conclusion; stated reasons; hidden assumption; exposing tool; remaining need; repaired claim.
- **Private argument / public cover:** inner case versus cleaner civic or moral story.
- **Motivated reasoning:** desire-first conclusion dressed as necessity.
- **Exposing tool:** any prior Whately habit (hidden assumption, analogy test, missing proof, term shift, rhetoric-vs-proof, source test, etc.).

Plot summary alone does not earn points.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Macbeth desire and conclusion (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Desire/feared loss: kingship/success without consequence; fear that the act will not be “the be-all and end-all.” Conclusion being tested: whether to assassinate Duncan / whether the deed can be done cleanly and finally.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names desire-pressure and the conclusion under test
- 1 pt: Thin or partial
- 0 pts: Plot only

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Reasons against and spur (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Against: double trust—kinsman, subject, and host duties; he should shut the door against a murderer, not bear the knife. Spur named: vaulting ambition, with no other spur to prick intent.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Duties against and ambition spur both clear
- 1 pt: One side only
- 0 pts: Plot summary

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – Hidden assumption and tool (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Possible hidden assumption: if consequences could be trapped/ended here, the murder would be acceptable; or ambition plus opportunity can outweigh trust duties. Exposing tools for later self-deception risk: hidden-assumption test, rhetoric-vs-proof (manhood shame), source test (witches/apparitions), equivocation on safety/fate, form-vs-fact. Strong answers name a real assumption and a fitting tool.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Plausible assumption plus fitting tool
- 1 pt: One element only
- 0 pts: No analytical content

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Repaired Macbeth claim (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A repaired narrower claim: I am ambitious and tempted; I know kinship, loyalty, and hospitality count against murdering Duncan; wanting the crown does not make the murder necessary or justified.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Narrower honest claim without false necessity
- 1 pt: Partially repaired
- 0 pts: Restates that he should kill or merely “don’t do it” without claim craft

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Brutus desire and conclusion (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Desire: protect Rome/the general good; preserve republican liberty; maintain his honorable identity as Rome’s guardian. Conclusion: Caesar must die (“It must be by his death”).

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Desire/honor-aim and death conclusion
- 1 pt: One element
- 0 pts: Plot only

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – Reasons, analogy, unproved (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Stated reasons: no personal cause; for the general; crown may change nature. Analogy: serpent’s egg—kill in the shell before mischief. Unproved: that crowning will necessarily change Caesar into a mischievous tyrant; that death is required rather than lesser restraints; that analogy of fixed animal kind fits a human political actor.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Reasons/analogy and clear unproved burden
- 1 pt: Partial
- 0 pts: Says only Brutus is noble or wrong

## Question 7: Part 4 Q1 – Brutus full autopsy (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Desire: Rome’s general good / honorable preventive guardianship. Conclusion: Caesar must die. Stated reasons: crown danger; general not personal cause; serpent-egg analogy. Hidden assumption: future nature-change is sure enough to require present killing; analogy’s relevant similarity holds. Exposing tool: analogy/disanalogy, hidden assumption, missing proof, or public-cover of necessity. Remaining need: evidence of tyranny trajectory, limits of preventive killing, political alternatives. Repaired claim: Caesar’s possible crowning worries me for Rome; that fear alone does not prove he must be killed in the shell.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Completes coherent autopsy
- 1 pt: Incomplete map
- 0 pts: Plot summary

## Question 8: Part 4 Q2 – Comparison (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Both speakers can see pieces of truth—Macbeth names duties and ambition; Brutus names public worry—yet desire recruits reasons toward a wanted act. Logical forms appear (duties listed; analogy offered) while necessity is overclaimed. Private reasoning hides by dressing want as must.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Compares both with tool-aware explanation
- 1 pt: One character well or thin compare
- 0 pts: Plot only

**Overall Notes for Grading This Worksheet:**

- Credit multi-tool readings; do not require one official tool name if the diagnostic work is precise.
