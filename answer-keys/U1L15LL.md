## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

- **Verbal dispute:** a disagreement caused mainly by different uses of a word or phrase.
- **Real dispute:** a disagreement that remains even after the key terms are clarified.
- **Mixed dispute:** a disagreement involving both word-confusion and real disagreement underneath.
- **Contested term:** the word whose meaning is part of the disagreement.
- **Shared claim:** something both sides would accept after the term is clarified.
- **Remaining disagreement:** what still must be argued after clarification.
- **Dispute map:** disputed claim; contested term; Side A meaning; Side B meaning; shared claim; remaining disagreement; classification.
- **Review terms:** prior-week discovery/proof/hypothesis tool and Week 13 source credibility/corroboration test.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Finished project (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The contested term is “finished.” One side means the slides exist or the basic product has been created. The other means the project is fully ready: sources checked and speaking notes practiced. Strong answers avoid choosing a winner before separating the meanings.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Identifies the contested term and both meanings accurately
- 1 pt: Identifies the term but gives only one meaning or vague meanings
- 0 pts: Treats one side as simply wrong without clarifying the term

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Expensive trip (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The contested term is “expensive.” One side means high total cost. The other means too costly relative to this month's budget. Both can be true: the trip may have a high price, or even a moderate price, that is unaffordable under current constraints.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Identifies the term and both meanings
- 1 pt: Identifies cost disagreement but not the two senses
- 0 pts: Only states whether the trip is expensive

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – Successful practice (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
This is likely a mixed dispute or at least a verbal dispute that may become real. The contested term is “successful.” The coach means successful by attendance; the captain means successful by improvement in weak plays. Clarification is needed: are they judging attendance, skill improvement, or both? After that they may still disagree about which standard matters most.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Classifies plausibly and explains the needed clarification
- 1 pt: Names the term but gives weak classification
- 0 pts: Chooses a side without dispute analysis

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Speed bumps and safety (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
This is a real dispute because both sides already share a definition of safe street: fewer crashes and fewer serious injuries. Evidence now matters: crash data, injury records, before/after comparisons, traffic speed, near-miss reports, or similar relevant data.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Classifies as real and names relevant evidence
- 1 pt: Says real dispute but gives little evidence detail
- 0 pts: Treats it as merely verbal despite shared definition

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Committed member (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
After clarification, both sides may agree that attendance and completing work are both signs of commitment, or that the member attends meetings but may not complete work. The remaining disagreement is which standard should count more, whether this member satisfies the relevant standard, or whether commitment requires both presence and follow-through.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names a plausible shared claim and remaining disagreement
- 1 pt: Gives only shared claim or only remaining disagreement
- 0 pts: Gives no clarification of the dispute

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – Original essay (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Clarification can settle that “original” may mean not plagiarized at the sentence level or intellectually fresh in argument. It may be agreed that the essay uses no copied sentences. What still needs judgment is whether the argument itself is fresh, independent, or merely rearranges common points.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Separates what clarification settles from what still needs judgment
- 1 pt: Mentions plagiarism or fresh argument only
- 0 pts: Treats originality as one obvious meaning without analysis

## Question 7: Part 4 Q1 – Lunch policy dispute map (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Disputed claim: whether the lunch policy is equal. Contested term: “equal.” Side A meaning: same written thirty-minute period for every student. Side B meaning: comparable actual eating time or practical access. Shared claim: everyone receives thirty minutes on the written schedule, and actual eating time matters. Remaining disagreement: whether formal sameness is enough, whether location differences make the policy unequal, and what adjustment is required. Classification: mixed, because clarifying “equal” helps but a real policy disagreement remains.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Completes the map with classification and remaining disagreement
- 1 pt: Includes some map elements but incomplete
- 0 pts: Accepts one side without dispute mapping

## Question 8: Part 5 Q1 – Review: courtyard hypothesis (1 point)

**Expected Answer:**
Hypothesis: the drain is clogged. What must be discovered: whether the courtyard actually floods and whether the drain is clogged, through observation, inspection, records, or testimony. Proof would require evidence connecting the clogged drain to the flooding, not merely imagining the explanation.

**Rubric:**
- 1 pt: Identifies hypothesis, needed discovery, and proof requirement
- 0 pts: Treats the imagined explanation as proof

## Question 9: Part 5 Q2 – Review: celebrity endorsement source (1 point)

**Expected Answer:**
The corroboration is weak because three advertisements may repeat the same non-independent celebrity endorsement. More relevant evidence would include independent reviews, user results, teacher evidence, learning research, expert evaluation, or transparent performance data.

**Rubric:**
- 1 pt: Explains shared-source/irrelevant-authority weakness and names better evidence
- 0 pts: Counts repeated ads as independent proof
