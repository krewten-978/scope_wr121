**Total Points:** 16

**Purpose:** Students apply Week 5 formal-validity work to Brutus and Antony: mood, figure, middle term, and the difference between a formally valid map and proved evidence.

## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

- **Mood:** the A/E/I/O sequence of the major premise, minor premise, and conclusion.
- **Figure pattern:** the placement of S, P, and M terms in the premises.
- **Form test:** the question whether the conclusion would have to follow if the premises were true.
- **Evidence test:** whether the premises, especially the pressure-point premise, are actually supported.

Do not require one exact formalization if the student's map is coherent and the form judgment follows from it.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Reconstructing Brutus (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong reconstruction: Major premise: All men whose ambition would enslave Rome are people who deserve death for Rome's freedom. Minor premise: Caesar is/was a man whose ambition would enslave Rome. Conclusion: Caesar deserved death for Rome's freedom.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Gives a coherent three-claim syllogism
- 1 pt: Gives the general argument but with incomplete premise structure
- 0 pts: Gives plot summary only

**Notes for grading:**
- Accept singular wording for Caesar if the structure is clear.

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Mood and validity (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The reconstructed argument is AAA if written as All M are P; All S are M; therefore All S are P. In that form, it is valid: if the premises are true, the conclusion follows.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Correct mood and correct validity judgment
- 1 pt: Correct mood or validity judgment but not both
- 0 pts: Confuses formal validity with factual truth

**Notes for grading:**
- If the student uses a different coherent map, grade mood and validity according to that map.

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – S, P, M, and figure (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
S = Caesar. P = people who deserved death for Rome's freedom. M = men whose ambition would enslave Rome. Figure pattern: All M are P; All S are M; therefore All S are P.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Correctly identifies S, P, M and pattern
- 1 pt: Correctly identifies most terms but pattern is unclear
- 0 pts: Does not identify roles of terms

**Notes for grading:**
- Equivalent wording is acceptable.

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Valid form versus proved premise (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
No. Brutus's formal shape only shows that the conclusion follows if the premises are true. It does not prove Caesar was dangerously ambitious. That claim is a factual/premise question and still needs evidence.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Clearly separates valid form from proof of the premise
- 1 pt: Notices the premise needs proof but explanation is thin
- 0 pts: Says valid form proves Caesar's ambition

**Notes for grading:**
- This is a key Week 5 distinction.

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Antony's evidence (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Antony cites that Caesar brought captives whose ransoms filled Rome's coffers, wept for the poor, and refused the crown three times.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Lists all three pieces of evidence
- 1 pt: Lists one or two pieces accurately
- 0 pts: Gives only Antony's emotional effect or “honourable man” irony

**Notes for grading:**
- Wording may vary.

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – Antony's logical role (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Antony mainly attacks Brutus's minor premise that Caesar was dangerously ambitious. He may imply Caesar was not ambitious, but his most direct logical work is to make Brutus's classification doubtful by giving counter-evidence.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Correctly identifies the attack on the minor premise and explains it
- 1 pt: Says Antony challenges ambition but not how
- 0 pts: Claims Antony only appeals to emotion with no logical pressure

**Notes for grading:**
- Credit answers that mention Antony's irony as supporting the premise attack.

## Question 7: Part 4 Q1 – Antony's possible syllogism (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Answers may vary. A reasonable map: No people who refuse kingship and act for Rome's public good are dangerously ambitious tyrants; Caesar was someone who refused kingship and acted for Rome's public good; therefore Caesar was not a dangerously ambitious tyrant. This is an EAE-style negative argument if phrased that way, though students may also produce a weaker “Some evidence against ambition is present” argument. The validity judgment should match the student's premises.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Gives a plausible syllogism, mood if possible, and a matching validity judgment
- 1 pt: Gives a plausible map but mood or validity is incomplete
- 0 pts: Gives evidence list only with no syllogism attempt

**Notes for grading:**
- Do not punish students for noting Antony's argument is harder to formalize than Brutus's.

## Question 8: Part 4 Q2 – Stronger form versus stronger evidence (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Brutus gives the cleaner formal syllogism. Antony gives stronger counter-evidence against the pressure-point premise that Caesar was dangerously ambitious. A strong answer distinguishes formal neatness from evidential strength.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Correctly distinguishes form strength from evidence strength
- 1 pt: Makes a reasonable comparison but blurs form/evidence
- 0 pts: Chooses a speaker based only on sympathy

**Notes for grading:**
- Accept nuanced answers if the form/evidence distinction is clear.
