## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

- **Testimony:** a claim received from another person, witness, record, report, or source.
- **Authority:** a source treated as worth hearing because of office, training, experience, or access.
- **Credibility:** the degree to which a source deserves trust in this case.
- **Access:** whether the source was in a position to know the fact claimed.
- **Relevant expertise:** training or knowledge that actually matches the question being judged.
- **Bias / pressure:** a motive or pressure that may tilt a source toward one answer.
- **Corroboration:** support from independent sources, records, or evidence.
- **Careful conclusion:** a trust judgment stated at the right strength: possible, reasonable, strongly supported, contradicted, or needing more investigation.
- **Review terms:** prior-week skills only: Week 12 induction/overclaim/revised claim and Week 11 analogy source/target/relevant similarity.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Debate club adviser (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Claim: the debate club will move to Wednesdays. Source: the club adviser cited by the student newspaper. Access: strong or relevant access because the adviser approves or knows the club calendar. The newspaper report is more credible if it accurately quotes or names the adviser.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Identifies claim, source, and strong access
- 1 pt: Identifies two elements or gives a vague trust judgment
- 0 pts: Treats the report as unsupported or ignores the adviser source

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Robotics rumor (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The student who skipped the meeting can directly know only that they heard a report or rumor. The cancellation itself needs a better source, such as the coach/adviser, team officer, official email, calendar update, or someone who attended the meeting.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Separates direct knowledge from claim needing better source
- 1 pt: Says “needs confirmation” without explaining access
- 0 pts: Accepts the rumor as direct knowledge

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – Musician and phone security (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The musician has fame, product-endorsement influence, or perhaps user experience, but not automatic cybersecurity expertise. Relevant expertise would come from security engineers, independent technical reviews, encryption audits, vulnerability records, or specialists who can test the phone's security.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Distinguishes celebrity authority from relevant technical expertise
- 1 pt: Vaguely says the musician is not an expert
- 0 pts: Treats fame as sufficient security proof

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Nurse and council president (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
For the health question, the nurse has more relevant expertise and professional access to health guidance. The student council president may have relevant concerns about event attendance but not equal authority on fever transmission or staying home. A careful answer can include both concerns while giving the health judgment more weight on the health issue.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Compares relevance of the two sources accurately
- 1 pt: Chooses the nurse but gives little explanation
- 0 pts: Treats event attendance concern as equal medical expertise

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Restaurant inspection score (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The restaurant owner has a business incentive to present the score favorably, and the visible sign may be outdated. Corroboration could include the current inspection report, health department website, dated certificate, or an independent public record. Bias does not prove the owner is lying, but the claim needs current evidence.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names pressure/bias and specific corroboration
- 1 pt: Mentions bias or corroboration only
- 0 pts: Accepts last year's sign as proof of the current claim

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – Group chat bus report (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
This is not independent corroboration if all three students rely on the same group chat message. It would be stronger with a bus-tracking app, a school transportation notice, a timestamped photo, a driver report, or separate witnesses who directly observed the late bus.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Explains the shared-source problem and gives stronger corroboration
- 1 pt: Says “not independent” without details
- 0 pts: Counts three repetitions as three independent sources

## Question 7: Part 4 Q1 – Shuttle credibility map (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Claim(s): the shuttle company says ridership is strong enough to expand; the neighborhood association says the shuttle is mostly empty. Sources: company, neighborhood volunteers, and city transit office data. Access/expertise: the company has operating data but may count boardings in a self-serving way; volunteers have direct but narrow observation; the transit office's six months of fare-card data is likely more relevant and broader if released. Bias: company benefits from expansion; association may oppose the service or have too small a sample. Corroboration/conflict: current claims conflict and both have limits. Careful conclusion: expansion or cancellation is not settled; wait for full fare-card summary and clarify counting methods.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Includes source, access/expertise, bias, corroboration/conflict, and careful conclusion
- 1 pt: Includes some map elements but incomplete
- 0 pts: Accepts one side without credibility analysis

**Rubric:**

- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing
