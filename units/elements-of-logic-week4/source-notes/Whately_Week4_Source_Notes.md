# Source Notes — Whately, Elements of Logic, Week 4

## Primary source

Richard Whately, *Elements of Logic*. Public-domain editions consulted through Internet Archive, especially:

- *Elements of logic*, New York: Harper & Brothers, 1853. Internet Archive item: `elementsoflogic04what`.
- OCR text consulted from: `https://archive.org/download/elementsoflogic04what/elementsoflogic04what_djvu.txt`

## Week 4 focus passages

Week 4 draws from Whately's treatment of reasoning, arguments, terms, distribution, and syllogistic structure. The reading especially uses Whately's explanation that:

1. reasoning proceeds by drawing conclusions from premises;
2. a proposition has terms, and syllogistic reasoning depends on how those terms relate;
3. the middle term stands between the two terms of the conclusion and serves as the medium of proof;
4. failures in the middle term can make an argument appear connected when it does not actually prove the conclusion.

## Adaptation approach

The student reading is adapted and modernized rather than quoted continuously. It preserves Whately's practical sequence—premises, conclusion, terms, and the middle term as the bridge of proof—while avoiding a full technical treatment of all valid moods and figures. That formal classification is reserved for Week 5, in keeping with the approved long-unit scope.

## Pedagogical emphasis

The main goal is practical mapping. Students should leave Week 4 able to take an ordinary argument and identify the conclusion, major term, minor term, middle term, major premise, and minor premise. The lab asks them to test whether the middle term really connects the conclusion and to produce a portfolio-ready syllogism map/window note.

## Literary connection note

The Week 4 reading and lab include light *Macbeth* and *Julius Caesar* applications, but the full lit anchor is intentionally deferred. The Shakespeare cases preview the next deliverable by asking students to map Brutus's argument about Caesar's future danger and Macbeth's reasoning from prophecy toward action.
