## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

- **Judgment mirror:** a literary failure used to clarify a reasoning habit, not to claim moral superiority.
- **Warrant:** the principle that explains why textual evidence supports a claim.
- **Counterpressure:** evidence, alternative, or objection that tests a conclusion.
- **Qualification:** a limit that prevents a claim from outrunning the passage.
- **Transfer:** a concrete use of the same habit outside literary analysis.

Plot summary alone does not earn points.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Macbeth observation and responses (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Observation: the messenger reports Birnam Wood appearing to move. Macbeth first attacks the witness (“Liar and slave!”); then, as the report persists, he “begin[s] / To doubt the equivocation” and reconsiders the fiend's promise.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Observation and both contrasting responses
- 1 pt: Observation plus one response
- 0 pts: Plot summary without judgment sequence

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Macbeth claim and warrant (3 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A limited claim: disciplined judgment remains open to disconfirming observation and retests source and terms when facts resist expectation. Warrant: willingness to revise confidence in response to relevant evidence is an observable mark of intellectual humility; Macbeth's delayed revision shows the cost of resisting that habit.

**Rubric:**
- 3 pts: Limited claim, accurate habit, and explicit warrant
- 2 pts: Sound claim/habit with implied warrant
- 1 pt: Tool name without logical connection
- 0 pts: Plot or moral verdict only

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – Macbeth qualification (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The passage does not prove that source testing prevents every tragedy, that Macbeth becomes fully disciplined, or that one corrected belief repairs prior choices. A valid qualification limits the claim to this moment's warning about evidence, equivocation, and delayed revision.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Real passage-sensitive limit and qualified claim
- 1 pt: General limit without revising scope
- 0 pts: Universalizes from the scene

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Macbeth transfer (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Names a concrete setting, pressure, and action—for example, when a workplace report contradicts a favored plan, pause before attacking the messenger, inspect the evidence, and retest the plan's assumptions. Other specific, aligned transfers are acceptable.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Setting, pressure, and action are concrete and aligned
- 1 pt: Sensible but generic transfer
- 0 pts: “Do not be like Macbeth” only

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Brutus pressure point (3 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A precise claim identifies the move from “might change his nature” to “It must be by his death.” Textual evidence may include the confessed lack of present personal cause and the serpent's-egg analogy. The pressure point is treating possible future tyranny as enough for present lethal necessity.

**Rubric:**
- 3 pts: Precise claim with quoted/accurate evidence and inferential pressure
- 2 pts: Correct claim with thin evidence
- 1 pt: Moral judgment or plot plus a phrase
- 0 pts: No reasoning analysis

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – Brutus warrant and counterpressure (3 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Warrant: disciplined thinkers distinguish possibility from demonstrated necessity, test analogies for relevant similarity, and examine less extreme alternatives. Fair counterpressure: Rome may face a genuine risk, but the argument still needs evidence of likely tyranny and an account of why institutional restraint or other options cannot answer it.

**Rubric:**
- 3 pts: Explicit warrant and fair, relevant counterpressure
- 2 pts: One strong and one partial
- 1 pt: Tool name or objection without connection
- 0 pts: No test of the conclusion

## Question 7: Part 4 Q1 – Compare judgment failures (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Both characters need intellectual humility before a desired or settled conclusion, but the pressures differ. Macbeth protects confidence in an equivocal source until observation breaks it; Brutus turns uncertainty and analogy into preventive necessity before sufficient evidence. Shared habits may include testing assumptions, source/term clarification, proportioning conclusions, and attending to counterevidence.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Shared habit plus meaningful difference in pressure
- 1 pt: One side only or generic comparison
- 0 pts: Plot comparison

## Question 8: Part 4 Q2 – Miniature portfolio defense (3 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Includes all five elements: a precise claim about disciplined judgment; passage evidence; an explicit warrant; a fair qualification; and a concrete transfer beyond the course. The paragraph should function as an argument, not a list of labels.

**Rubric:**
- 3 pts: Coherent argument with all five elements
- 2 pts: Sound argument missing one element
- 1 pt: Several elements listed but not connected
- 0 pts: Plot summary only
