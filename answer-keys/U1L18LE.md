## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

- **Revision trail:** original claim, precise diagnosis, substantive revision, and reflective caption.
- **Substantive revision:** a change to the reasoning, not merely style or proofreading.
- **Criterion:** the logical standard that tests the original claim.
- **Equivocation/source test:** clarification of an ambiguous promise and evaluation of whether the source warrants confidence.
- **Possibility-to-necessity move:** treating what might happen as sufficient proof that one action must happen.

Plot summary alone does not earn points.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Macbeth model: notice and oversimplification (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The early claim correctly notices that Macbeth is dangerously misled. It oversimplifies by treating the apparition's words as a straightforward falsehood. The promise is equivocal and later technically fulfilled; the key failure includes Macbeth's preferred interpretation and inference to practical certainty.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names both valid notice and precise oversimplification
- 1 pt: One side only
- 0 pts: Plot summary or “witches are bad”

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Macbeth model: revised judgment (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Macbeth accepts the most reassuring sense of an ambiguous promise, fails to test terms/source, infers security, and uses that confidence while choosing further violence. This is stronger because it names textual evidence, the inferential failure, and relevant tools rather than calling every word a lie.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Accurate revised judgment plus why it improves the original
- 1 pt: Sound revised idea without explanation
- 0 pts: Repeats the early claim

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – Macbeth evidence, inference, action (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Evidence: “none of woman born / Shall harm Macbeth.” Inference: Macduff need not be feared / Macbeth is secure from ordinary human harm. Added action: Macbeth will “make assurance double sure” and have Macduff killed. Fitting habits include term clarification/equivocation, source credibility, hidden-assumption test, or inference/claim test.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: All three roles plus a fitting habit
- 1 pt: Partial map or fitting tool without clear application
- 0 pts: Plot only

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Macbeth reflective caption (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A complete caption explains that the original called the promise a lie; the diagnosis found oversimplification and a missed inference; the revision distinguishes equivocal wording from Macbeth's chosen interpretation and action; the change shows more precise term, source, and inference judgment.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Original, diagnosis, change, and growth all visible
- 1 pt: Sound but incomplete caption
- 0 pts: General praise of the revision

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Brutus early-response diagnosis (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Caring about Rome may explain motive but does not prove the conclusion. The response ignores the gap from “might change” to “must be by his death,” the serpent's-egg analogy's uncertain fit, the lack of present personal cause, and untested alternatives. Any one precise, passage-grounded criterion earns full credit.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Exact passage-grounded weakness with fitting criterion
- 1 pt: Correct concern stated generally
- 0 pts: Praises or condemns Brutus without logic

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – Revised Brutus judgment (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong revision preserves that Brutus expresses genuine concern for Rome but judges that concern insufficient to prove necessary death. The possibility of corruption, supported by an analogy, still requires evidence of likely tyranny, a defense of the analogy, and examination of less extreme alternatives.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Balanced, precise revision testing possibility-to-necessity
- 1 pt: Directionally correct but thin
- 0 pts: Mere plot or moral verdict

- 0 pts: General reflection only
