# U1L12LE – Lit Example Worksheet Answer Key & Rubric

**Total Points:** 16

**Paired reader:** `units/elements-of-logic-week12/lit-examples/U1L12LE_lit_example_reader.tex` / `.pdf`.

**Paired worksheet:** `units/elements-of-logic-week12/lit-examples/U1L12LE_lit_example_worksheet.tex` / `.pdf` (Assignment ID **U1L12LE**).

**Purpose:** Students apply Week 12 induction / evidence-ladder diagnosis to Cassius in *Julius Caesar* and Lady Macbeth in *Macbeth*.

## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

- **Example:** a particular observed case offered as evidence.
- **General claim:** a broader conclusion about character, motive, or pattern.
- **Overclaim:** a conclusion larger than the evidence supports.
- **Counterexample / missing evidence:** evidence that would challenge or test the broader claim.
- **Revised claim:** a narrower conclusion that fits the examples actually given.

Plot summary alone does not earn points.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Cassius's two examples (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Cassius uses the Tiber swimming story, where Caesar cried for help, and the fever in Spain, where Caesar shook. Strong answers identify these as anecdotes about physical weakness or vulnerability.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names both examples accurately
- 1 pt: Names one example or gives vague “Caesar was weak” summary
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Limited claim about Caesar (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The examples support a limited claim that Caesar is physically vulnerable, not superhuman, and not literally godlike. They may also support that Cassius has personally seen Caesar need help.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Gives a narrow, evidence-fitting claim
- 1 pt: Gives a partly narrow but vague claim
- 0 pts: Jumps directly to a broad political conclusion

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – Cassius's larger desired inference (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Cassius likely wants Brutus to infer that Caesar is unworthy of Rome's awe, unfit to tower over other Romans, or dangerous to honor as a ruler. Some students may say Cassius wants Brutus to resist Caesar's rise.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Identifies a broader character/political inference
- 1 pt: Vague “Caesar is bad” answer
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Why examples are insufficient (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Physical weakness in two moments does not prove political unfitness, tyranny, or moral inferiority. A leader can be physically vulnerable and still competent; the examples may be cherry-picked; other evidence about judgment, policy, ambition, and public conduct would be needed.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Explains the gap between anecdotes and broad conclusion
- 1 pt: Says “not enough evidence” with little detail
- 0 pts: Treats Cassius's anecdotes as decisive proof

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Lady Macbeth's general claim (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Lady Macbeth claims Macbeth wants greatness and has ambition, but is too full of human kindness or lacks the ruthless “illness” to seize the nearest way. In short, he is ambitious but not naturally ruthless enough.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Accurately states ambition plus kindness/not ruthless enough
- 1 pt: Mentions ambition or kindness only
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – Visible and stronger evidence (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Visible evidence includes Macbeth's letter, the prophecy/title news, and Lady Macbeth's intimate judgment of his nature. Stronger evidence would include multiple actions or speeches showing Macbeth hesitating, choosing conscience, pursuing ambition, or resisting cruelty across scenes. Students should distinguish visible evidence from evidence known later in the play.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names visible evidence and what additional evidence would strengthen the claim
- 1 pt: Only one side of the answer
- 0 pts: Plot summary without evidence diagnosis

## Question 7: Part 4 Q1 – Revise Cassius's argument (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong revision narrows the conclusion: “Caesar is not superhuman; Romans should not worship him as if he were above ordinary weakness,” or “These stories give reason to question Caesar's godlike image, but more evidence is needed to judge his political fitness.”

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Revises to a narrower conclusion that fits the anecdotes
- 1 pt: Some narrowing but still overstrong
- 0 pts: Repeats a decisive anti-Caesar conclusion without added evidence

## Question 8: Part 4 Q2 – Capstone evidence ladder paragraph (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong paragraph explains that Week 12 helps readers value powerful examples without turning them into oversized claims. Cassius's anecdotes and Lady Macbeth's judgment are meaningful evidence, but students must ask what pattern they actually show, what evidence is missing, and how to revise a claim so it fits the sample.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Applies the evidence ladder to literary character claims with nuance
- 1 pt: Thin application
- 0 pts: Plot summary only

**Overall Notes for Grading This Worksheet:**
- Whole points only. Total: 8 × 2 = 16 points.
- Credit students who distinguish “supports a limited claim” from “proves nothing.” Week 12 is about sizing claims to evidence, not dismissing examples.
