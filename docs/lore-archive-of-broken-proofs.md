# Lore Bible: The Archive of Broken Proofs

**Status:** Lore locked by teacher approval (2026-07-21). The culminating decision and station outline below are authoritative for U1F4LD.
**Purpose:** Give Logic Dungeon IV (Weeks 10–12) a place, history, people, procedures, objects, and real expedition decision before the assessment is built.
**Continuity:** Follows Logic Dungeon III (*The Court of the False Oracle*), in which the company rejects ceremonial finality and takes the **Records Service Corridor** into the working stacks.
**Later handoff:** The company should leave with a defensible but limited **Dossier of Approach**, not with guaranteed entry to *Castrum Malae Ratiocinationis*. Weeks 13–17 and Logic Dungeon V will test the sources, disputes, discoveries, and rhetoric encountered near the Gates.

Keep *Castrum Malae Ratiocinationis* untranslated and serious for students. The twenty adventurers remain a collective expedition resource, never stand-ins for individual students. Prefer endangered, delayed, separated, or trapped over graphic loss language.

---

## 1. Design inheritance from the first three Dungeons

The older Dungeons establish a consistent world:

1. **Hall of Inscriptions:** public signs, guide-speech, ledgers, and naturalist records may be true, false, ambiguous, or too broad. Slate is the best-supported next step toward daylight, not a guarantee of safety.
2. **Surveyor’s Rotunda:** the complex was built and repaired by multiple hands. Official seals, professional blue thread, cold air, insects, and damaged masonry all have limited evidentiary meanings. Rootbridge becomes the best-supported safe route only after mason, maintenance, and naturalist records are joined.
3. **Court of the False Oracle:** the Upper Archive is an institution, not an enchanted ruin. Passage, Seal, and Charge manufacture finished rulings from incomplete records. The company advances through the unceremonial Records Service Corridor because independent service records support it.

Dungeon IV should preserve the same principles:

- objects have layered physical and institutional histories;
- authentic evidence may still answer the wrong question;
- attractive order can conceal missing support;
- a defective argument may preserve a true, useful fragment;
- later evidence changes what earlier evidence means;
- the company must record a **degree of support**, not claim certainty it has not earned.

Dungeon IV should also advance the series rather than repeat it. It is not another choice among four tunnels, not another tribunal of three speaking offices, and not another case in which an ugly pre-existing route simply wins late. Its culminating achievement is **construction**: the company must rebuild one defensible claim from records that were separated from the questions they were meant to answer.

## 2. Where the Records Service Corridor leads

The corridor leaves the ceremonial Court behind and descends only a short distance. Its low steps, dry floor, narrow turn, and tray-height clearance match the Warden’s inspection ledger from Dungeon III. It was built for clerks carrying sealed records between the Court and the deeper working archive.

At its far end, the company reaches the **Receiving Floor of the Archive of Broken Proofs**.

The first view is not of a grand library. It is an industrial records room:

- stone receiving tables cut with shallow grooves for packet cords;
- tall claim rails bearing movable brass question-plates;
- hanging proof chains from which document packets once depended;
- numbered bays for comparisons, case rolls, objections, and exceptions;
- a wide central collation table scarred by knives, wax, and water;
- an outbound transfer stair that once carried completed dossiers toward the fortress approaches.

Much of the Archive is intact. What is broken is the connection between **claim and support**.

A quake cracked the ceiling and tore many proof chains from the claim rail. Later water entered through an abandoned copying cistern. Clerks and salvagers dried the records, rebound them, and restored their seals—but often placed them under whichever surviving claim-plate seemed closest. Thus an authentic survey can sit beneath the wrong question, a useful comparison can be treated as a complete proof, and a handful of striking cases can masquerade as a universal record.

The danger is not forged paper. The danger is **misjoined proof**.

## 3. What the Archive originally was

The Archive was never merely a place where finished reports were stored. It was a **proof workshop** for the fortress administration.

When an officer, architect, courier, or governor proposed a conclusion—repair this gate, trust this route, provision this tower, admit this company—the Archive’s clerks were supposed to assemble a dossier answering one precise question.

### Original workflow

1. **Question entered:** A brass claim-plate stated the exact point at issue.
2. **Nearby matters separated:** Records that proved something related but not sufficient were hung on a neighboring rail.
3. **Comparisons tested:** Plans from a known structure were paired with the target structure; relevant likenesses and important differences were entered on opposite leaves.
4. **Instances gathered:** Case slips recorded successful, failed, ordinary, and exceptional outcomes.
5. **Exceptions attached:** Contrary cases remained physically tied to the proposed generalization.
6. **Conclusion sized:** The completed dossier received a conclusion no broader than its evidence.
7. **Revision preserved:** If the claim changed, the old claim-plate was crossed through but retained so later readers could see what had and had not been proved.

The Archive’s founding maxim, engraved above the collation table, is:

> **Let no record answer a question that was not entered. Let no likeness conceal a difference. Let no list become a law while an exception is hidden.**

This is not magical law. It is the professional rule the institution gradually ceased to obey.

## 4. Layered history of the Archive

### Layer A — The First Surveyors and Collators

The original fortress architects needed practical judgments under uncertainty. Their records are terse, material, and often limited: dimensions, loads, water levels, crew sizes, seasons, observed failures, and conditions under which a procedure worked.

They distinguished sharply among:

- a record being genuine;
- a record being relevant;
- a comparison fitting the disputed point;
- a set of cases supporting a limited pattern;
- a conclusion being strong enough to guide action.

They created the claim rail, paired comparison drawers, instance rolls, and exception cords.

### Layer B — The Imperial Archive Authority

The Imperial Authority expanded the workshop into a legal-administrative archive. Every major conclusion acquired a handsome cover, seal class, and summary page. This improved preservation but encouraged readers to treat a completed packet as settled proof.

The Authority also created the **Hall of Nearest Answers**, where useful but insufficient records were kept near the main question. Originally the hall was a warning against confusing nearby claims with the point at issue. Later administrators treated proximity as relevance and moved convenient records onto the main rail.

### Layer C — The Continuance Office

During a prolonged crisis, the fortress government adopted a policy called the **Doctrine of Continuance**: every office was required to show that the fortress remained governable, supplied, defensible, and properly ordered.

The Archive’s task quietly changed. Instead of asking, “What do the records support?” clerks were increasingly asked, “Which records support the required conclusion?”

Three corruptions followed:

- records proving ceremonial importance were used to prove present usefulness;
- successful repairs in one structure were used as analogies for unlike structures;
- favorable cases were copied into clean rolls while failures and exceptions were moved to a basement bay called the **Broken Shelf**.

“Broken” originally meant “the proof chain is incomplete or defeated.” Under the Continuance Office, it became a stigma placed on inconvenient evidence.

### Layer D — Quake, Water, and Salvage

A quake tore the central proof chains loose. Water from an abandoned copying cistern soaked the lower instance bays. A small salvage staff saved thousands of records by drying pages, replacing cords, and restamping covers.

They preserved documents but not always their logical position. Similar seals, similar words, or similar drawings became shortcuts for reassembly.

This is why the Archive now contains many records that are materially authentic and logically misplaced.

### Layer E — Later Expeditions

Later travelers came looking for a quick route to the fortress. They copied dramatic examples, left confident summaries, and removed some maps from their original packets. Several departure notes report success without stating which travelers returned, under what conditions, or whether the writer saw the full journey.

Their additions are recent enough to be tempting and incomplete enough to be dangerous.

## 5. Why it is called the Archive of Broken Proofs

A **broken proof** is not necessarily a false statement or forged record.

A proof is broken when one or more joins have failed:

- the evidence proves a nearby claim rather than the point at issue;
- the source and target cases share a vivid but irrelevant likeness;
- an important disanalogy has been cut from the comparison leaf;
- the listed cases are too few, too uniform, or selectively preserved;
- a counterexample has been detached from the general claim it limits;
- a cautious original conclusion has been replaced by a broader summary;
- the evidence chain is genuine but incomplete.

The place therefore embodies the Weeks 10–12 movement:

1. **What exact question must be answered?**
2. **Does this comparison fit that question in the relevant respect?**
3. **How large a conclusion can these cases support?**

## 6. The physical districts

These are parts of the world, not a future station list. Assessment design must use only objects that already exist here.

### A. Receiving Floor

The service corridor opens here. Incoming records were unsealed, dried, numbered, and sent to the appropriate claim rail. A freight tally shows that entire clerk crews—sometimes more than twenty—used the outbound transfer stair, but this establishes the stair’s historical function, not its present condition.

### B. The Great Claim Rail

A long brass rail carries question-plates. Some still state precise issues:

- “Which approach can admit a supplied company in the wet season?”
- “Which gate was ceremonially principal?”
- “Which wall repair endured after three winters?”
- “Which route was used by mounted couriers?”

Other plates have fallen to the floor. Packets hang under neighboring questions because their cords were reattached after the quake. This is the physical heart of irrelevant proof: authentic answers under the wrong question.

### C. The Hall of Nearest Answers

This side gallery contains records that establish something useful but insufficient. Heraldic precedence, speed of messengers, beauty of masonry, ceremonial processions, and successful single-scout passages may all be true. None automatically answers whether twenty supplied travelers can make a controlled approach now.

The hall was designed to preserve partial proof. Later officials treated “nearest” as “good enough.”

### D. The Paired Works, or Hall of Similitudes

Scale plans and repair models are stored in paired drawers: source structure on the left, target structure on the right. A narrow center leaf once listed relevant likenesses and disanalogies.

Many center leaves are missing. Some were removed to make recommendations appear cleaner. Others were used as scrap during the salvage. The surviving comparisons are visually persuasive: matching arches, similar water marks, equal spans, repeated blue thread, identical stone dressing. The missing differences concern what actually bears on safety and movement: foundation rock, load direction, drainage, season, repair age, crew size, and whether a passage carries people or only water.

### E. The Registry of Instances

Thousands of small case slips record crossings, repairs, inspections, returns, closures, and failures. The slips were once arranged by season, load, destination, and observer.

After the flood, dry and legible slips were rebound first. This created accidental sample bias: wet-season failures and lower-bay exceptions were the records most likely to remain stuck together, illegible, or uncopied.

The cleanest surviving roll is therefore not necessarily the most representative.

### F. The Broken Shelf and Counterexample Cages

The Continuance Office moved contrary cases here. “Cages” are open iron frames designed to keep exception slips visibly tied to the claims they limit. Many cords were cut. A few counterexamples remain folded behind summary boards or written on the reverse of repair orders.

This district should not feel like a secret room containing “the truth.” Some objections are weak, obsolete, or irrelevant. Its importance is procedural: a general claim cannot be judged honestly if contrary cases were excluded in advance.

### G. The Collation Table

At the center of the Archive stands a broad stone table with channels for aligning four things:

1. the question-plate;
2. records that directly bear on it;
3. comparison leaves with similarities and differences;
4. instance rolls with their exceptions.

A dossier assembled here could be revised rather than merely accepted or rejected. The table’s old tools remain: blank conclusion slips, narrow-claim seals, “support incomplete” tags, red exception cord, and a knife for cutting an overlarge summary from its packet.

### H. The Outbound Transfer Stair

This is the Archive’s ordinary exit toward a chain of muster rooms and exterior approach galleries. It is not one of the disputed fortress gates. The stair is physically blocked at first by fallen packet chests and a jammed counterweight cage, creating pressure to choose what to recover and what to leave.

The company can clear it, but the damaged cage can carry only the twenty travelers, essential gear, and **one approach chest**. The expedition cannot take the entire Archive forward.

That practical limit forces the culminating decision: what claim and supporting records deserve to travel onward?

## 7. Voices in the Archive

Dungeon III used three present, speaking offices. Dungeon IV should be quieter. The Archive speaks through hands separated by time rather than another tribunal.

### The First Collator

Founding voice preserved in procedural plaques and terse margin rules. Insists that every packet begin with a precise question and end with a conclusion sized to its support. Severe but not infallible; early surveys can still be incomplete.

### Prefect of Continuance

Crisis-era administrator whose cover memoranda are elegant, urgent, and predetermined. The Prefect does not forge records. The corruption is selection and redirection: records are valuable insofar as they can be made to support continuance.

### Comparator Ilyan (working name)

Architect-clerk responsible for paired structural cases. His early leaves list both likenesses and differences. Later copies preserve only the vivid likeness. He becomes an example of a careful source made misleading through excerpting.

### Enumerator Sera (working name)

Field clerk who maintained instance rolls. Her tallies distinguish season, load, crew type, successful arrival, failed attempt, and unknown outcome. Several summary pages cite her totals while omitting the categories that made them meaningful.

### The Red-Cord Reviser

An unidentified late clerk who retied counterexamples to broadened claims using red cord. The Reviser did not leave a grand theory or a hidden final answer. The work consists of small acts: restoring a missing exception, crossing out “all,” replacing “proven” with “observed in these cases,” and returning a record to its original question.

The Reviser provides a humane recurring figure: repair is not defeat. Narrowing a claim can save what is true in it.

### Present human presence

No active archivist needs to be waiting for the party. Fresh wax crumbs, recently shifted chests, and one warm lamp cup may indicate that someone else uses the approaches. This should seed Weeks 13–17 questions about testimony, authority, dispute, and rhetoric without resolving who that person is.

## 8. The approach dossiers

Three prominent chests survived near the outbound stair. Each contains genuine material and a broken proof. They are not “three bad answers” pasted onto a map; they are products of the Archive’s history.

### The Dossier of the Crowned Gate

**Claim on its cover:** The Crowned Gate is the proper approach for the company.

**Why it looks strong:** Oldest seals, ceremonial plans, records of royal entries, repeated references to “the principal gate.”

**What its records genuinely establish:** The Crowned Gate was ceremonially principal and symbolically important.

**Broken join:** Ceremonial primacy is a nearby claim. It does not establish current access, present repair, or suitability for twenty supplied travelers.

### The Dossier of the Twin Arches

**Claim on its cover:** The East Water Arch can bear the company because its construction matches a repaired western bridge.

**Why it looks strong:** Paired scale drawings show the same span, arch profile, and stone dressing. A successful repair record survives for the western source structure.

**What its records genuinely establish:** The structures share several visible design features; the western repair may suggest questions and inspection methods.

**Broken join:** Missing center leaves concern different foundation rock, reverse water pressure, different load direction, and a later modification to the eastern target. The analogy may guide inspection but cannot carry the full safety conclusion.

### The Dossier of Forty-Seven Returns

**Claim on its cover:** The Wagon Gate is the most reliable approach because forty-seven recorded parties returned through it.

**Why it looks strong:** A clean, impressive roll of successful return slips, many from identifiable imperial couriers.

**What its records genuinely establish:** Numerous recorded dry-season courier parties used the gate successfully under some conditions.

**Broken join:** The roll consists largely of mounted couriers traveling light in two dry seasons. Wet-season records, supplied crews, failed attempts, and parties with unknown outcomes were stored in lower bays. The sample cannot represent every company or every season.

### Why none of the three should simply “win”

Each dossier preserves something worth carrying forward:

- Crowned Gate: historical function and ceremonial jurisdiction;
- Twin Arches: a relevant inspection analogy if limited carefully;
- Forty-Seven Returns: a real pattern under specified conditions.

But no inherited cover claim is strong enough for the company to seal unchanged. Dungeon IV should culminate in rebuilding rather than choosing the least defective old packet.

## 9. Recommended culminating decision and delayed synthesis

### The company’s real decision

The damaged transfer cage can take only one approach chest. The company must decide:

> **What conclusion about the fortress approach is strong enough to enter in the expedition’s Dossier of Approach, and which records should be carried to support and limit it?**

This is a consequential expedition choice. A careless dossier could direct all twenty toward an unproved gate. Refusing to make any conclusion would waste the Archive’s genuine evidence and leave the company unable to plan its approach.

### Recommended designer lock

The best result is a **new Composite Dossier**, assembled at the Collation Table. It is not present as a finished fourth option at the beginning.

Its exact student-facing wording should be settled only after the later station outline, but the world-level conclusion is:

> **The surviving records do not yet justify committing all twenty to any named gate. They do justify carrying a controlled approach plan: inspect the Wagon Gate first under conditions matching the recorded successes, while using the Twin-Arches comparison only to specify structural checks and retaining the Crowned-Gate records as evidence of ceremonial status, not safety.**

This is the best-supported **next authorization**, not a guaranteed safe entrance and not a declaration that all three old dossiers are worthless.

### Why Wagon Gate receives the first controlled inspection

Late recovery from the lower instance bay should provide a more representative, though still incomplete, pattern:

- successful and failed attempts;
- more than one season;
- light couriers and supplied work crews;
- known returns and unknown outcomes clearly separated;
- at least one counterexample to “always reliable”;
- enough cases close to the company’s size and load to justify inspection priority, not full commitment.

The late records do not prove the Wagon Gate universally safe. They repair the field by showing that its evidence is broader and more relevant than the clean roll first suggested, once the omitted cases are restored.

The Twin-Arches dossier contributes a limited but valuable checklist: compare foundation, pressure, load direction, drainage, and repair age before treating the structures alike.

The Crowned-Gate dossier contributes a warning about the future Gates: an office may recognize ceremonial standing without certifying physical passage.

### Delayed-field pattern

1. **Arrival:** Three finished inherited dossiers compete for the one chest; each has authentic appeal.
2. **Early examination:** Students can identify what each packet really establishes, but no full-company commitment is justified.
3. **Middle recovery:** Missing comparison leaves and cut exception cords weaken the broad cover claims without making every record useless.
4. **Late recovery:** Lower-bay instance rolls produce a more varied pattern for Wagon Gate and clarify conditions under which its prior successes matter.
5. **Construction:** The company makes a Composite Dossier whose conclusion is narrower than the old covers and whose records include their own limitations.
6. **Departure:** The company clears the transfer stair and moves toward the muster galleries with a plan to inspect before committing the twenty.

This pattern differs intentionally from Dungeons II and III: the late answer is not an overlooked ugly route. It is a **repaired claim assembled from partial proofs**.

## 11. What logic can and cannot establish in this place

Logic can help the company:

- state the exact question;
- distinguish direct proof from a nearby true claim;
- judge whether a likeness bears on the conclusion;
- identify limiting differences;
- inspect sample variety and missing cases;
- use a counterexample to defeat or narrow an overlarge claim;
- preserve partial proof while revising a conclusion;
- decide what limited action is best supported.

Logic alone cannot:

- make an old measurement current;
- restore an illegible case slip;
- determine why every traveler failed to return;
- guarantee a gate has not changed since inspection;
- establish the honesty or expertise of the recent unknown visitor;
- decide every prudential or moral question about risking the company.

The best decision therefore includes an observation plan, load limits, retreat provision, and a willingness to revise.

## 12. Continuity into later weeks and Dungeon V

Dungeon IV should end with the company leaving the Archive, not entering the final fortress.

The Composite Dossier prepares later work:

- **Week 13 — testimony and authority:** Who created each surviving record? Were they competent, honest, relevant, and corroborated?
- **Week 14 — discovery, proof, and judgment:** Some records may have helped discover a possibility without yet proving it.
- **Week 15 — kinds of dispute:** Some apparent disagreement may concern words, facts, or mixed questions.
- **Week 16 — rhetoric and proof:** A Gate speaker may use the Archive’s own repaired dossier persuasively while exceeding what it says.
- **Week 17 — self-deception:** The expedition may be tempted to read “best-supported inspection” as “destined safe entry” because it wants the journey to be over.

The fresh wax and warm lamp cup seed another party or resident administration without determining its reliability. The Crowned Gate’s ceremonial status can matter later without becoming proof of safety. The Wagon Gate inspection plan creates a live approach, but Dungeon V must still require broad cumulative judgment at the Gates.

Recurring objects carried forward:

- the Composite Dossier;
- a red exception cord;
- one narrow-claim seal;
- the Crowned-Gate ceremonial record;
- the Twin-Arches inspection checklist;
- the restored mixed-season Wagon Gate roll.

## 13. Tone and visual identity

- Serious, exact, dusty, procedural, and materially grounded.
- More workshop than library: rails, cords, drawers, models, rolls, and collation tools.
- No magical books, whispering shelves, spectral librarians, or truth-detecting mechanisms.
- No comic bureaucracy. The institutional failure is plausible: preservation without context, policy-driven selection, and attractive summaries replacing careful files.
- The Archive should be intellectually strange: conclusions literally hang from questions, counterexamples are tied by red cord, and claims can be cut narrower at a stone table.
- Student-facing lore must later be compressed severely. The world bible carries the depth; the assessment carries only what affects diagnosis and consequence.

## 14. One-paragraph summary

The Records Service Corridor carries the twenty adventurers into the working Archive, where the fortress once assembled evidence beneath precise question-plates. A quake, water damage, crisis-era propaganda, and hurried salvage preserved thousands of genuine records while breaking their connection to the claims they were meant to support. The company finds three inherited approach dossiers: one confuses ceremonial primacy with present suitability, one overextends a structural analogy, and one turns a biased roll of successful returns into a universal guarantee. Because the damaged transfer cage can carry only one chest, the company must recover missing comparison leaves and cut-away exceptions, then build a new Composite Dossier at the old Collation Table. Its repaired conclusion does not promise entry: it authorizes a controlled first inspection of Wagon Gate under conditions closest to the broader recovered record, carries an analogy-based checklist rather than an analogy-based verdict, and preserves ceremonial evidence without mistaking it for safety. The company leaves the Archive with a claim strong enough to guide action and narrow enough to remain honest.

## 15. Locked teacher decisions

Teacher approval on 2026-07-21 locks the following:

1. **Core identity:** The Archive is a proof workshop whose claim-to-evidence links were broken, not a conventional ruined library.
2. **Historical corruption:** The Doctrine of Continuance explains the policy-driven selection and exile of inconvenient evidence.
3. **Culminating action:** The damaged transfer cage has a one-chest capacity. Records may be removed from the inherited chests and repacked into one new Composite Dossier chest; this is not an inventory-capacity puzzle.
4. **Recommended result:** The Composite Dossier authorizes Wagon Gate for the first controlled inspection under conditions nearest the repaired record. It does not authorize full-company entry or guarantee safety.
5. **Voices:** The Archive remains mostly silent and record-driven. The First Collator, Prefect of Continuance, Comparator Ilyan, Enumerator Sera, and Red-Cord Reviser survive through documentary hands rather than a new live tribunal.
6. **Future seed:** Fresh wax and a warm lamp cup remain subtle signs of a recent unknown visitor.
7. **Names:** Comparator Ilyan and Enumerator Sera remain named; the Red-Cord Reviser remains anonymous.

## 16. Locked object-bound station outline

Every part has two jobs: evaluate proof and determine what the expedition may record or do. Student-facing lore remains lean.

1. **Great Claim Rail — recover the point at issue.** Use the brass question-plates and detached packets to distinguish the exact operational question from ceremonial, architectural, and historical nearby questions. Expedition consequence: establish the standard every approach dossier must answer.
2. **Crowned Gate in the Hall of Nearest Answers — preserve a nearby truth.** Use royal entry rolls and ceremonial plans to identify what they establish and why they do not establish current suitability. Expedition consequence: narrow the Crowned claim instead of discarding authentic records.
3. **Twin Arches in the Paired Works — test an analogy.** Use Comparator Ilyan's western source model, eastern target model, surviving likenesses, and recovered center leaf. Expedition consequence: convert the analogy from a safety verdict into an inspection checklist.
4. **Forty-Seven Returns in the Registry of Instances — inspect the sample.** Use the clean dry-season courier roll to distinguish observations, pattern, sample limits, and overclaim. Expedition consequence: keep Wagon Gate provisional rather than accept or reject it.
5. **Broken Shelf and Counterexample Cages — restore the field.** Use Enumerator Sera's mixed-season, mixed-load lower-bay roll, unknown outcomes, withdrawals, and red-cord counterexample. Expedition consequence: defeat universal reliability while establishing a broader, more relevant basis for controlled inspection priority.
6. **Collation Table — rebuild rather than choose.** Align the exact question, Crowned records, Twin-Arches comparison leaf, Wagon instance rolls, exceptions, narrow-claim seal, and support-incomplete tag. Expedition consequence: construct the Composite Dossier with each record assigned only the work it can do.
7. **Outbound Transfer Stair — authorize limited action.** Under the one-chest cage constraint, select the records that must travel, authorize Wagon Gate for first controlled inspection, specify checks and retreat conditions, and distinguish best-supported action from guaranteed entry. Fresh wax and the warm lamp cup remain facts whose source and trustworthiness logic alone cannot supply.

## 17. Locked culminating language

The design-level conclusion for the assessment is:

> The surviving records do not justify committing all twenty adventurers to any named gate. They justify carrying a Composite Dossier that gives Wagon Gate priority for a controlled first inspection under conditions nearest the recovered successful cases. The Twin-Arches comparison supplies structural checks rather than a safety verdict. The Crowned-Gate records establish ceremonial standing rather than present passage. The mixed Wagon record includes failures, withdrawals, and unknown outcomes, so inspection priority is not a guarantee.

For U1F4LD, a **controlled first inspection** means that a small inspection party approaches Wagon Gate under stated checks and retreat conditions while the full company remains uncommitted at a secured muster point. The exact point at issue is: **What approach action do the surviving records best support now for this company?**

The assessment must apply carry-forward grading: an early misconception is scored once, while later synthesis receives credit when the student reasons coherently from the results recorded. No assessment object may be invented outside this bible merely to host a question.
