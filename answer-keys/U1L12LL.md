## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

- **Induction:** reasoning from observed cases toward a broader claim.
- **Example / observation:** a particular case offered as evidence.
- **General claim:** a conclusion about a larger group, pattern, or rule.
- **Sample:** the set of examples actually examined.
- **Representative sample:** examples that fairly reflect the larger group being judged.
- **Counterexample:** a case that challenges or disproves an overlarge claim.
- **Overclaim:** a conclusion broader than the evidence supports.
- **Revised claim:** a narrower conclusion that fits the evidence.
- **Review terms:** prior-week skills only: analogy source/target/relevant similarity and irrelevant proof / missing the point.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Flash cards (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Example: one student used flash cards before one vocabulary quiz and scored higher than usual. General claim: flash cards are the best study method for everyone. Size of claim: very broad/universal or near-universal across students and study situations. The example is too small for that conclusion.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Identifies example, general claim, and broad size
- 1 pt: Identifies two elements or vaguely says the claim is too broad
- 0 pts: Treats one quiz as enough proof for everyone

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Club reminders (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Examples: three meetings in one month had higher attendance when same-day reminders were sent. Limited claim: same-day reminders probably help attendance for this club or are worth continuing/testing. The claim is cautious because it says “probably” and stays close to the observed pattern.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names examples and limited claim accurately
- 1 pt: Partial identification
- 0 pts: Misses the evidence-to-claim relation

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – Dessert-line survey (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The sample shows what dessert-line customers or bakery-interested people may want. It does not fairly show whether the whole town needs more bakeries. A larger conclusion would need evidence from a broader mix of residents, businesses, costs, demand, and existing options.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Distinguishes what the biased sample shows from the larger claim needing better evidence
- 1 pt: Notes bias but weakly explains
- 0 pts: Treats the line as representative of the town

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Parking complaints (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Five senior complaints may show that some seniors dislike the parking rules. Missing evidence includes views from juniors, sophomores, students without cars, staff, safety/cost information, and whether complaints represent most seniors. The whole-student-body conclusion is an overclaim.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names missing/unrepresentative evidence and the overclaim
- 1 pt: Says “needs more evidence” without specifics
- 0 pts: Accepts five complaints as enough for the whole student body

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Group projects (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A counterexample could be a group project where work was divided fairly and members completed assigned roles. A revised claim might be: “Some group projects fail when roles and accountability are unclear,” or “Group projects often need clear structure to prevent unequal work.”

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Gives plausible counterexample and useful narrowed revision
- 1 pt: Gives only counterexample or only revision
- 0 pts: Repeats the universal claim

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – Online tutorials (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Two weak tutorials can support a limited claim such as “these tutorials did not teach this difficult skill well” or “some online tutorials are poor.” They cannot support “no online tutorial can teach a difficult skill,” which is universal and would be challenged by any strong tutorial or learner success.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Separates supported limited claim from unsupported universal claim
- 1 pt: Partial
- 0 pts: Treats two tutorials as proof against all tutorials

## Question 7: Part 4 Q1 – Writing center paragraph (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Observations: four Thursday after-school sessions; on three Thursdays more students came after 3:30 than lunch; after-school students mostly wanted long-essay help while lunch students wanted quick citation help. Pattern: after-school may be better for long essay help. Limited claim: keep or expand some after-school writing-center hours for essay help. Overclaim: lunch tutoring is useless and all hours should move. Revised conclusion: shift some hours after school or test a mixed schedule before eliminating lunch hours.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Includes observations, pattern, limited claim, overclaim, and revised conclusion
- 1 pt: Includes some elements but incomplete
- 0 pts: Accepts the all-hours conclusion without qualification

**Rubric:**

- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing
