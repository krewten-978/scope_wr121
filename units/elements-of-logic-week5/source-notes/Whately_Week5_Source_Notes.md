# Source Notes — Whately, Elements of Logic, Week 5

## Primary source

Richard Whately, *Elements of Logic*. Public-domain editions consulted through Internet Archive, especially:

- *Elements of logic*, New York: Harper & Brothers, 1853. Internet Archive item: `elementsoflogic04what`.
- OCR text consulted from: `https://archive.org/download/elementsoflogic04what/elementsoflogic04what_djvu.txt`

## Week 5 focus passages

Week 5 draws from Whately's treatment of categorical syllogisms after the practical introduction to syllogistic structure in Week 4. The reading especially uses Whately's explanations that:

1. the propositions in a syllogism may be classified by quantity and quality using the A/E/I/O forms;
2. the mood of a syllogism is the A/E/I/O order of major premise, minor premise, and conclusion;
3. the figure of a syllogism concerns the placement of the middle term in the premises;
4. validity depends on whether the form carries the conclusion, not on whether the subject matter sounds plausible;
5. rules about the middle term, negative premises, and particular conclusions protect the mind from overclaiming.

## Adaptation approach

The student reading is adapted and modernized rather than quoted continuously. It preserves Whately's formal concern with mood, figure, and validity while limiting the amount of traditional terminology students must memorize. Traditional mnemonic names are mentioned only lightly; the core student task is to classify simple forms, identify the middle term, and explain whether the conclusion follows.

## Pedagogical emphasis

Week 5 is the second syllogism week. Week 4 established a practical map of premises, conclusion, and terms. Week 5 asks students to test the shape of the map: A/E/I/O mood, placement of the middle term, and basic validity failures such as the undistributed middle or two negative premises. The lab produces a portfolio-ready mood-and-figure classification card.

## Literary connection note

The Week 5 reading and lab include light *Macbeth* and *Julius Caesar* applications, but the full lit anchor is intentionally reserved for the next build step. The current Shakespeare examples ask whether arguments about Brutus's honor or Macbeth's fate have a form that proves their conclusions, rather than whether the whole literary interpretation is true.
