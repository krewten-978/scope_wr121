# Source Notes — Whately, Elements of Logic, Week 6

## Primary source

Richard Whately, *Elements of Logic*. Public-domain editions consulted through Internet Archive, especially:

- *Elements of logic*, New York: Harper & Brothers, 1853. Internet Archive item: `elementsoflogic04what`.
- OCR text consulted from: `https://archive.org/download/elementsoflogic04what/elementsoflogic04what_djvu.txt`

## Week 6 focus passages

Week 6 draws from Whately's treatment of logic's proper province and from his distinction between the correctness of reasoning and the truth of the matters reasoned about. The reading especially uses Whately's emphasis that:

1. logic tests whether conclusions follow from premises;
2. logic does not itself supply all material facts needed by an argument;
3. a formally correct argument may fail when a premise is false;
4. arguments should be judged by both structure and truth of premises;
5. disciplined thinkers should name the point of failure rather than merely calling an argument "bad."

## Adaptation approach

The student reading is adapted and modernized rather than quoted continuously. It preserves Whately's modest account of logic: logic is powerful for testing inference, but it does not replace historical evidence, moral judgment, scientific observation, testimony, or literary interpretation. The classroom language uses the modern terms "form test," "fact test," and "soundness" to make the distinction usable for students.

## Pedagogical emphasis

Week 6 prevents students from treating syllogisms as truth machines. The central student task is a two-column diagnosis: form test and fact test. The lab asks students to distinguish invalid form, false premise, doubtful premise, overclaim, sound argument, unsound argument, and not-yet-proved argument.

## Literary connection note

The Week 6 lit-anchor stays with *Julius Caesar*. It uses Brutus's and Antony's Act 3, Scene 2 funeral speeches to test the difference between formal validity and soundness. Brutus's argument can be reconstructed as a valid form, but Antony attacks the pressure-point premise that Caesar's ambition would enslave Rome.

## Julius Caesar lit-anchor sources

The Week 6 lit-anchor uses original Shakespeare text from:

- William Shakespeare, *Julius Caesar*, Act 3, Scene 2: Brutus's funeral speech to the citizens. Source used: https://shakespeare.mit.edu/julius_caesar/julius_caesar.3.2.html
- William Shakespeare, *Julius Caesar*, Act 3, Scene 2: Antony's funeral speech responding to Brutus's claim that Caesar was ambitious. Source used: https://shakespeare.mit.edu/julius_caesar/julius_caesar.3.2.html
