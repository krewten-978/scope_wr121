# U1L3LE – Lit Example Worksheet Answer Key & Rubric

**Total Points:** 16

**Paired reader:** `units/elements-of-logic-week3/lit-examples/U1L3LE_lit_example_reader.tex` / `.pdf`.

**Paired worksheet:** `graded-assignments/U1L3LE/worksheet.tex` / `.pdf` (Assignment ID **U1L3LE**).

**Purpose:** Students apply the Week 3 Whately habit of identifying propositions, categorical form, quality, quantity, and distribution to Macbeth's apparitions. Grade for logical translation and classification, not for plot summary.

## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

Use these terms consistently when evaluating student answers:

- **Proposition:** a claim that can be treated as true or false; commands and warnings are not propositions by themselves, though a proposition may be inferred from them.
- **Subject:** the class or thing the proposition is about.
- **Predicate:** what the proposition affirms or denies of the subject.
- **Quality:** whether the proposition is affirmative or negative.
- **Quantity:** whether the proposition is universal or particular.
- **Distribution:** whether a term is spoken of in its whole extension. In standard categorical forms: A distributes the subject only; E distributes both subject and predicate; I distributes neither; O distributes the predicate only.

Do not require students to use advanced terminology beyond the prompt if their classification is clear. Do require them to distinguish translation from proof.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Warning versus proposition (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong answer says “Beware Macduff” is not a proposition in the strict Week 3 sense because it is a command or warning rather than a claim that is directly true or false. Macbeth might infer a proposition such as “Macduff is dangerous to Macbeth,” “Macduff may harm Macbeth,” or “Macbeth should be cautious about Macduff.” The inferred proposition should be stated as a claim, not another command.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Correctly identifies the line as not a strict proposition and supplies a plausible inferred proposition
- 1 pt: Gives only one side of the answer or confuses command and proposition but shows partial understanding
- 0 pts: Treats the warning as a standard categorical proposition with no explanation

**Notes for grading:**
- Accept varied inferred propositions if they preserve the warning's logical content.

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Subject and predicate of the apparition's claim (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong answer says the line is a proposition because it makes a claim about who will or will not harm Macbeth. A clear subject is “persons/men born of woman” or “people born of woman.” A clear predicate is “persons who will harm Macbeth” or “people able/destined to harm Macbeth.” The line denies the predicate of the subject class.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Identifies the line as a proposition and gives accurate subject and predicate
- 1 pt: Recognizes the general claim but gives vague or incomplete subject/predicate terms
- 0 pts: Gives plot summary or identifies Macbeth as the subject without clarifying the class claim

**Notes for grading:**
- Macbeth is involved, but the logical subject of the categorical translation is the class “persons born of woman.”

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – E-form translation (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong translation: “No persons born of woman are persons who will harm Macbeth.” This is E form: universal negative. Quality is negative. Quantity is universal. Equivalent wording is acceptable if it keeps the No S are P structure.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Gives a correct E-form translation with negative quality and universal quantity
- 1 pt: Gives the right general idea but mislabels either quality or quantity
- 0 pts: Does not translate into standard form or changes the claim substantially

**Notes for grading:**
- “No men born of woman are people who can harm Macbeth” is acceptable if “men” is being used broadly as persons/humans in context.

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Macbeth's expanded A-form confidence (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Macbeth's translated confidence, “All men I will meet in battle are people who cannot harm me,” is A form: universal affirmative. Quality is affirmative. Quantity is universal. A strong answer explains that this may go beyond the exact wording because the apparition spoke about persons born of woman, not explicitly every enemy Macbeth will meet. Macbeth assumes that everyone he will face belongs to that class and that “shall not harm” means practical invulnerability.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Correctly classifies the A-form claim and explains how Macbeth expands the wording
- 1 pt: Correct classification with weak explanation, or good explanation with a classification error
- 0 pts: Treats Macbeth's confidence as identical to the apparition's wording with no translation issue

**Notes for grading:**
- Full credit should distinguish the original E-form claim from Macbeth's broader confidence.

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Translating “only” (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong translation recognizes that “Only someone not born in the ordinary way can harm Macbeth” means: “All persons who can harm Macbeth are persons not born in the ordinary way.” Subject: persons who can harm Macbeth. Predicate: persons not born in the ordinary way. This is an A-form universal affirmative proposition.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Correctly translates the “only” claim into All S are P and states subject/predicate
- 1 pt: Gives a plausible translation but reverses subject/predicate or leaves them unclear
- 0 pts: Treats “only” as “some” or gives no standard-form claim

**Notes for grading:**
- The most common error is reversing it to “All persons not born in the ordinary way can harm Macbeth,” which says too much.

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – Distribution in the A-form translation (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
For the A-form proposition “All persons who can harm Macbeth are persons not born in the ordinary way,” the subject term (“persons who can harm Macbeth”) is distributed because the claim speaks about all members of that class. The predicate term (“persons not born in the ordinary way”) is undistributed because the claim does not say that all such persons can harm Macbeth.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Correctly identifies distributed subject and undistributed predicate with explanation
- 1 pt: Correctly names one term's distribution or gives a partial explanation
- 0 pts: Reverses distribution or gives no link to the standard form

**Notes for grading:**
- If a student used a different but logically equivalent A-form translation in Part 3 Q1, grade distribution according to that translation.

## Question 7: Part 4 Q1 – Macduff and the subject class (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong answer explains that Macduff's birth challenges whether he belongs inside the subject class “persons born of woman.” He was born from his mother's body, but not by ordinary birth; the apparition's phrase is ambiguous enough for Macbeth to assume ordinary human birth while Macduff can be treated as outside the intended class. The logical issue is classification: if Macduff is outside the subject class, the proposition “No persons born of woman are persons who will harm Macbeth” does not protect Macbeth from him. If he is inside it, the prophecy would be false or misleading.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Explains the subject-class issue and connects it to whether the proposition applies to Macduff
- 1 pt: Notices Macduff is exceptional but gives little logical analysis of the class term
- 0 pts: Gives only plot summary or says simply “the witches lied” without classification analysis

**Notes for grading:**
- Do not require a single metaphysical answer about birth; require clear explanation of how the class boundary affects the inference.

## Question 8: Part 4 Q2 – Translating before trusting (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Answers will vary. A strong answer writes the exact proposition Macbeth must test, such as “No persons born of woman are persons who will harm Macbeth,” classifies it as E form if using that wording, and states a danger: dramatic language may be conditional, ambiguous, class-dependent, or narrower than Macbeth's confidence. Translation clarifies the claim but does not prove that the speaker is trustworthy or that Macbeth's expanded conclusion follows.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Gives a clear proposition, identifies form if possible, and states a logical danger of relying on unclear language
- 1 pt: Gives a relevant proposition or danger but leaves classification or explanation incomplete
- 0 pts: Gives only a moral/theme statement or plot summary

**Notes for grading:**
- Accept discussion of the Birnam Wood conditional if the student distinguishes it from a simple categorical proposition.

**Overall Notes for Grading This Worksheet:**
- Whole points only. No half points.
- Grade translation discipline: proposition status, subject/predicate, quality/quantity, and distribution where asked.
- Do not reward long plot summaries unless they support precise logical classification.
- Total: 8 questions × 2 points = 16 points.
