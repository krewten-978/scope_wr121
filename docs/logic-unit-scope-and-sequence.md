# Scope and Sequence — Whately's *Elements of Logic*

## Working unit title

**Thinking Straight: Richard Whately and the Art of Reasoning**

## Unit purpose

This unit uses Richard Whately's *Elements of Logic* as the spine for a twelfth-grade course in disciplined reasoning. The course is not merely a survey of formal logic, and it is not a generic critical-thinking unit. It is a long, text-centered study of how a mature reader, writer, speaker, and citizen learns to distinguish real reasoning from verbal confusion, defective proof, and persuasive nonsense.

Whately is especially useful because he presents logic as both modest and powerful. Logic cannot discover every truth, supply missing facts, or make a foolish person wise. But it can teach a thinker to examine whether a conclusion really follows from the reasons offered. That makes the unit valuable for literature, rhetoric, history, philosophy, theology, civic argument, and the student's own writing.

The unit now assumes a twelfth-grade audience. Readings may preserve more of Whately's technical vocabulary and argumentative sequence than an earlier tenth-grade test run. The student materials should remain readable, but they should not avoid conceptual difficulty.

## Central question

**How can we tell the difference between real reasoning, verbal confusion, and persuasive nonsense?**

## Final unit question

**What does it mean to be a disciplined thinker?**

## Grade level and format

- **Intended level:** 12th grade.
- **Length:** long unit / semester-scale sequence.
- **Primary format:** weekly student reading handout, argument/focus lab, teacher guide, source notes, and PDF deliverables.
- **Reading approach:** adapted and modernized from Whately while preserving more of his distinctions, order of thought, and technical vocabulary than a lower-grade version would require.
- **Student work pattern:** each week contributes a portfolio artifact: argument skeleton, term investigation, proposition translation, syllogism map, fallacy autopsy, evidence test, dispute map, literary application, or public-argument analysis.
- **Recurring literary anchors:** Shakespeare's *Macbeth* and *Julius Caesar*. These should recur throughout the unit so students can apply new logical tools to familiar texts without constantly learning new plot contexts.

## Whately as spine; other texts as illuminations

Whately should remain the sequence-setter. His text gives the course its order:

1. what logic is and is not;
2. terms;
3. propositions;
4. arguments and syllogisms;
5. validity and truth;
6. fallacies;
7. induction and evidence;
8. discovery and judgment;
9. verbal and real disputes.

Other texts should illuminate rather than replace this spine.

### Recommended philosophical and rhetorical illuminations

- **Aristotle:** use selectively for syllogism, demonstration, rhetorical proof, and the difference between valid form and persuasive force. Aristotle should support Weeks 4-6 and Week 16, not overwhelm them.
- **Plato / Socrates / Sophists:** use selectively for definition, verbal confusion, and the danger of winning by words rather than truth. These pair well with Weeks 2, 7, and 15.
- **Francis Bacon:** use when the unit turns to induction, evidence, and discovery. Bacon helps students see why formal reasoning alone is not enough when inquiry depends on observation and method.
- **John Henry Newman:** use near the end of the course to connect logic with intellectual formation, judgment, and liberal education. Newman helps the final weeks ask what kind of person disciplined reasoning forms.

### Required literary anchors

- **Macbeth:** use for prophecy, equivocation, hidden assumptions, ambition, self-deception, false inference, and the difference between prediction and compulsion.
- **Julius Caesar:** use for public rhetoric, civic persuasion, emotional proof, irrelevant proof, conflicting interpretations of honor, and the difference between persuading an audience and proving a claim.

The scope should avoid adding many literary anchors. Reusing *Macbeth* and *Julius Caesar* will let the class go deeper because students will already know the dramatic situation and can focus on the logic.

## Portfolio as final assessment

The official final product is a **Logic Portfolio**. Students should build it throughout the unit rather than assemble it only at the end.

### Required portfolio sections

1. **Argument Skeletons**
   - conclusion
   - stated reason
   - hidden assumption
   - test of whether the conclusion follows

2. **Term Investigations**
   - contested word
   - possible meanings
   - meaning in the argument
   - clarified definition

3. **Proposition Translations**
   - ordinary sentence
   - standard form
   - subject and predicate
   - quality and quantity
   - distribution when relevant

4. **Syllogism Maps**
   - major premise
   - minor premise
   - conclusion
   - major, minor, and middle terms
   - validity check

5. **Fallacy Autopsies**
   - apparent proof
   - hidden assumption
   - point of failure
   - repaired version or narrowed conclusion

6. **Evidence and Induction Tests**
   - evidence offered
   - size of claim
   - possible counterexample
   - revised claim

7. **Dispute Maps**
   - verbal dispute
   - real dispute
   - mixed dispute
   - clarified terms
   - remaining disagreement

8. **Macbeth / Julius Caesar Applications**
   - character or speaker claim
   - stated or implied reason
   - rhetorical force
   - logical strength or weakness

9. **Final Reflection / Defense**
   - What does it mean to be a disciplined thinker?
   - Which Whately tool most changed the way the student reads, writes, or judges arguments?

## Existing sequence status

The first three weeks are already represented in the repository and should now be treated as the opening of a twelfth-grade long unit rather than a tenth-grade test run.

### Week 1 — Logic Is Not Magic

- **Source base:** Whately's introductory defense of logic.
- **Essential question:** What can logic actually do for a thinker, and what should we not expect it to do?
- **Core idea:** Logic studies reasoning as reasoning. It helps us ask whether a conclusion follows from premises, but it does not supply missing facts or replace wisdom.
- **Student skill:** identify conclusion, stated reason, hidden assumption, and test whether the conclusion follows.
- **Macbeth / Caesar application:** Macbeth's response to prophecy; Brutus's reasoning about Caesar as a future danger.
- **Portfolio artifact:** argument skeleton and short reflection on logic's proper office.
- **Current repo status:** built; revised for 12th-grade framing and recurring Shakespeare applications.

### Week 2 — One Word, Two Ideas

- **Source base:** Whately's treatment of terms and ambiguity.
- **Essential question:** How can the same word mean two different things inside a single argument, and what can we do about it?
- **Core idea:** Arguments often fail because a key term shifts meaning without being noticed.
- **Student skill:** identify equivocation, fix the meaning of a term, and then retest the argument.
- **Macbeth / Caesar application:** the witches' equivocal promises in *Macbeth*; competing uses of honor, ambition, and liberty in *Julius Caesar*.
- **Portfolio artifact:** term investigation.
- **Current repo status:** built; source note aligned to Whately's treatment of terms and ambiguity.

### Week 3 — What We Say and What We Mean

- **Source base:** Whately's treatment of propositions.
- **Essential question:** How can we state claims clearly enough that others know exactly what we are asserting?
- **Core idea:** Before an argument can be judged, its claims must be made precise. Students must know what the statement is about, what is being said, whether it affirms or denies, and whether it concerns all, none, or some of the subject.
- **Student skill:** identify subject, predicate, quality, quantity, and the A/E/I/O forms of categorical propositions.
- **Macbeth / Caesar application:** translate claims such as “Macbeth is fated,” “Caesar is ambitious,” or “honorable men can be dangerous” into more precise proposition form.
- **Portfolio artifact:** proposition translation and window note.
- **Current repo status:** built; O-proposition distribution corrected.

### Week 4 — Two Claims and a Conclusion

- **Source base:** Whately on arguments and syllogistic structure.
- **Essential question:** When do two claims actually force a conclusion?
- **Core idea:** A syllogism tests whether a conclusion follows from two propositions because of their form and shared terms.
- **Student skill:** separate premises from conclusion and identify the major, minor, and middle terms without yet requiring full mood-and-figure classification.
- **Macbeth / Caesar application:** previewed in the reading and lab through Macbeth's reasoning from prophecy and Brutus's argument that Caesar must be stopped before he becomes tyrannical.
- **Portfolio artifact:** practical syllogism map and window note.
- **Current repo status:** built with student reading, argument lab, Julius Caesar lit-anchor, and teacher guide.

### Week 5 — The Shape of an Argument

- **Source base:** Whately on syllogistic form, mood, figure, and formal validity.
- **Essential question:** Can the shape of an argument tell us whether the conclusion follows?
- **Core idea:** Once propositions are classified, arguments have recognizable forms. Some forms preserve valid inference; others only appear to do so.
- **Student skill:** identify mood first, figure second, and test a small set of valid and invalid forms without memorizing every traditional valid mood.
- **Macbeth / Caesar application:** previewed in the reading and lab through form tests of arguments about Brutus's honor and Macbeth's fate.
- **Portfolio artifact:** mood-and-figure classification card with explanation of formal validity.
- **Current repo status:** built with student reading, argument lab, Julius Caesar lit-anchor, and teacher guide.

### Week 6 — Valid Does Not Mean True

- **Source base:** Whately on the province of logic and the distinction between formal correctness and material truth.
- **Essential question:** Can an argument be logically valid and still fail to prove what we should believe?
- **Core idea:** Logic can show whether a conclusion follows from premises, but other disciplines are needed to determine whether the premises are true.
- **Student skill:** classify arguments as valid/invalid and then ask whether the premises are true.
- **Macbeth / Caesar application:** focused on *Julius Caesar*: Brutus's argument can be reconstructed as valid, while Antony attacks the pressure-point premise about Caesar's ambition.
- **Portfolio artifact:** two-column argument diagnosis: form test and fact test.
- **Current repo status:** built with student reading, argument lab, Julius Caesar lit-anchor, and teacher guide.

## Full-unit sequence

The sequence below expands the earlier compact unit into a long twelfth-grade course. The extension weeks are now part of the core plan.

### Week 4 — Two Claims and a Conclusion

- **Likely title:** *Because: How Syllogisms Work*
- **Source base:** Whately on arguments and syllogistic structure.
- **Essential question:** When do two claims actually force a conclusion?
- **Core concepts:** syllogism, major term, minor term, middle term, major premise, minor premise, conclusion.
- **Core idea:** A syllogism tests whether a conclusion follows from two propositions because of their form and shared terms.
- **Student skill:** separate premises from conclusion and identify the middle term without yet requiring full mood-and-figure classification.
- **Macbeth / Caesar application:** map Brutus's argument that Caesar must be killed before he becomes tyrannical; map Macbeth's reasoning from prophecy to action.
- **Portfolio artifact:** practical syllogism map.
- **Why it belongs here:** Weeks 1-3 have prepared the tools: conclusion/reason, stable terms, and clear propositions. Week 4 combines those tools without overloading students with formal notation.

### Week 5 — The Shape of an Argument

- **Likely title:** *Mood, Figure, and Formal Validity*
- **Source base:** Whately on syllogistic form, supplemented lightly by Aristotle.
- **Essential question:** Can the shape of an argument tell us whether the conclusion follows?
- **Core concepts:** mood, figure, A/E/I/O pattern, valid form, invalid form.
- **Core idea:** Once propositions are classified, arguments have recognizable forms. Some forms preserve valid inference; others only appear to do so.
- **Student skill:** identify mood first, figure second, and test a small set of valid and invalid forms. Students should not be required to memorize every traditional valid mood.
- **Macbeth / Caesar application:** compare a formally valid classroom syllogism with a rhetorically powerful but formally unstable Shakespearean argument.
- **Portfolio artifact:** mood-and-figure classification with explanation of validity.
- **Why it belongs here:** The first syllogism week should be practical; the second can introduce formal classification after students understand the parts.
- **Current repo status:** built with student reading, argument lab, Julius Caesar lit-anchor, and teacher guide.

### Week 6 — Valid Does Not Mean True

- **Likely title:** *Good Form, Bad Facts*
- **Source base:** Whately on the province of logic and the distinction between formal correctness and material truth.
- **Essential question:** Can an argument be logically valid and still fail to prove what we should believe?
- **Core concepts:** validity, soundness, false premise, true conclusion, form test, fact test.
- **Core idea:** Logic can show whether a conclusion follows from premises, but other disciplines are needed to determine whether the premises are true.
- **Student skill:** classify arguments as valid/invalid and then ask whether the premises are true.
- **Macbeth / Caesar application:** focused on *Julius Caesar*: Brutus's argument can be reconstructed as valid, while Antony attacks the pressure-point premise about Caesar's ambition.
- **Portfolio artifact:** two-column argument diagnosis: form test and fact test.
- **Why it belongs here:** It prevents students from treating syllogisms as truth machines and returns to Whately's modest account of logic.
- **Current repo status:** built with student reading, argument lab, Julius Caesar lit-anchor, and teacher guide.

### Week 7 — When an Argument Looks Stronger Than It Is

- **Likely title:** *What Is a Fallacy?*
- **Source base:** Whately's opening treatment of fallacies.
- **Essential question:** Why do bad arguments often feel persuasive?
- **Core concepts:** fallacy, appearance of proof, defect in reasoning, emotional force, rhetorical force.
- **Core idea:** A fallacy is not merely a false statement. It is reasoning that seems to prove something but fails to do so.
- **Student skill:** distinguish false claims from bad inferences and explain where the reasoning breaks.
- **Macbeth / Caesar application:** examine a persuasive speech or soliloquy for apparent proof versus actual proof.
- **Portfolio artifact:** fallacy autopsy.
- **Why it belongs here:** Students are now ready to diagnose failure because they know terms, propositions, and basic argument structure.

### Week 8 — The Word That Changed Meaning Halfway Through

- **Likely title:** *Equivocation and the Ambiguous Middle*
- **Source base:** Whately's treatment of ambiguity and fallacies involving language.
- **Essential question:** How can a single shifting word make an argument appear valid?
- **Core concepts:** equivocation, ambiguous middle, verbal ambiguity, same word / different idea.
- **Core idea:** Some arguments appear to connect premises and conclusions only because one term is quietly used in two senses.
- **Student skill:** expose the shifting term and rewrite the argument with fixed meanings.
- **Macbeth / Caesar application:** analyze “fair is foul,” the witches' equivocations, or Antony's repeated “honorable men.”
- **Portfolio artifact:** ambiguous-middle diagnosis and repaired version.
- **Why it belongs here:** This revisits Week 2 at a higher level. Students first learned to fix terms; now they see why term-shifts corrupt full arguments.

### Week 9 — When the Proof Secretly Assumes the Answer

- **Likely title:** *Circular Reasoning and Begging the Question*
- **Source base:** Whately on petitio principii and arguments that assume what they must prove.
- **Essential question:** How can an argument sound like proof while merely repeating its conclusion?
- **Core concepts:** circular reasoning, begging the question, assumed conclusion, hidden premise.
- **Core idea:** An argument fails when the reason only works if the conclusion is already granted.
- **Student skill:** identify when a premise smuggles in the conclusion and rewrite the argument so it gives independent support.
- **Macbeth / Caesar application:** test whether claims about ambition, tyranny, loyalty, or manhood depend on assumptions the speaker has not proved.
- **Portfolio artifact:** circular-argument rewrite.
- **Why it belongs here:** It deepens the hidden-assumption work from Week 1 and gives students a common tool for essay revision.

### Week 10 — Answering a Question No One Asked

- **Likely title:** *Irrelevant Proof and Missing the Point*
- **Source base:** Whately on ignoratio elenchi and irrelevant conclusion.
- **Essential question:** How can an argument prove something and still fail to prove the point at issue?
- **Core concepts:** irrelevant conclusion, question at issue, redirection, partial proof, nearby claim.
- **Core idea:** A speaker may prove a true or interesting point while failing to prove the conclusion that actually needed proof.
- **Student skill:** identify the exact question under debate and determine whether the evidence answers it.
- **Macbeth / Caesar application:** distinguish proving that Caesar is powerful from proving that Caesar must be killed; distinguish proving Macbeth is tempted from proving Macbeth is compelled.
- **Portfolio artifact:** prompt-match diagnosis: claim, actual proof, missing question, repaired proof.
- **Why it belongs here:** This connects directly to student writing. Many weak essays prove a nearby claim instead of the assigned claim.

### Week 11 — When Comparisons Prove Too Much

- **Likely title:** *Analogy and Relevant Similarity*
- **Source base:** Whately on comparison, probability, and practical reasoning, supplemented by rhetorical examples.
- **Essential question:** When does a comparison actually prove something?
- **Core concepts:** analogy, relevant similarity, disanalogy, comparison, overextension.
- **Core idea:** A comparison supports an argument only when the compared cases are alike in the respects that matter.
- **Student skill:** test whether an analogy proves the claim or merely decorates it.
- **Macbeth / Caesar application:** evaluate comparisons used to describe Caesar as a serpent's egg or Macbeth as a trapped/fated man.
- **Portfolio artifact:** analogy test.
- **Why it belongs here:** Students often reason by analogy in essays. This week teaches them to test comparisons rather than merely admire them.

### Week 12 — Finding Evidence Is Not the Same as Proving a Claim

- **Likely title:** *Induction, Examples, and General Claims*
- **Source base:** Whately's discussion of induction and its relation to reasoning, supplemented by Bacon.
- **Essential question:** When do examples justify a general conclusion?
- **Core concepts:** induction, example, observation, generalization, evidence, counterexample.
- **Core idea:** Evidence matters, but examples must be weighed carefully. A few cases do not automatically prove a universal claim.
- **Student skill:** judge whether evidence supports the size of the conclusion being drawn.
- **Macbeth / Caesar application:** ask whether one act, one speech, or one prophecy is enough evidence for a general claim about character.
- **Portfolio artifact:** evidence ladder: example, pattern, limited claim, overclaim, revised claim.
- **Why it belongs here:** It moves from deductive form to the kind of evidence students often use in literary, historical, and civic argument.

### Week 13 — Whom Should We Trust?

- **Likely title:** *Testimony, Authority, and Credibility*
- **Source base:** Whately on reasoning from testimony and probability, with possible support from Bacon or a short modern source on expertise.
- **Essential question:** When should we trust a source?
- **Core concepts:** testimony, authority, credibility, expertise, bias, corroboration, probability.
- **Core idea:** Much of what we believe comes through testimony. Disciplined reasoning must ask whether a witness or authority is competent, honest, relevant, and corroborated.
- **Student skill:** evaluate a source without dismissing authority as useless or accepting authority as proof by itself.
- **Macbeth / Caesar application:** assess the witches as sources; assess public claims about Caesar's ambition or Brutus's honor.
- **Portfolio artifact:** credibility map.
- **Why it belongs here:** Seniors need a bridge from logical form to research, public argument, and historical/literary evidence.

### Week 14 — Can Logic Discover Anything New?

- **Likely title:** *Reasoning, Discovery, and Judgment*
- **Source base:** Whately's discussion of the discovery of truth and the limits of reasoning alone, supplemented by Bacon.
- **Essential question:** Does reasoning discover truth, or does it only arrange what we already know?
- **Core concepts:** discovery, proof, observation, testimony, memory, imagination, judgment.
- **Core idea:** Logic helps test and arrange thought, but discovery often requires observation, experience, testimony, imagination, and judgment.
- **Student skill:** distinguish kinds of intellectual work: discovering facts, defining terms, drawing inferences, testing proof, judging application.
- **Macbeth / Caesar application:** ask what each tragic world discovers too late and what kind of reasoning failed before the discovery.
- **Portfolio artifact:** intellectual-tool map.
- **Why it belongs here:** This returns to Week 1 at a more mature level and prevents overclaiming for formal logic.

### Week 15 — Are We Actually Disagreeing?

- **Likely title:** *Verbal and Real Disputes*
- **Source base:** Whately on verbal and real questions, with optional Socratic support.
- **Essential question:** How can we tell whether people disagree about reality or only about words?
- **Core concepts:** verbal dispute, real dispute, definition, contested term, shared claim, genuine disagreement.
- **Core idea:** Some arguments continue because people use words differently; others remain even after terms are clarified because the disagreement is real.
- **Student skill:** classify a disagreement as verbal, real, or mixed; clarify terms; identify what remains disputed.
- **Macbeth / Caesar application:** examine whether conflicts over honor, loyalty, ambition, tyranny, manhood, or justice are verbal, real, or mixed.
- **Portfolio artifact:** dispute map.
- **Why it belongs here:** This gives the unit a powerful civic and literary application: students learn how to reduce confusion without pretending all disagreements disappear.

### Week 16 — Logic and Rhetoric

- **Likely title:** *Persuasion Is Not Proof*
- **Source base:** Whately on logic's province, supplemented by Aristotle's rhetoric.
- **Essential question:** How do style, emotion, character, and persuasion relate to proof?
- **Core concepts:** logos, ethos, pathos, proof, persuasion, audience, rhetorical force.
- **Core idea:** Rhetoric can help truth become visible, but persuasive force is not the same as logical proof.
- **Student skill:** separate what a speech makes an audience feel from what it actually proves.
- **Macbeth / Caesar application:** Antony's funeral speech is the central case; Lady Macbeth's persuasion can serve as a second case.
- **Portfolio artifact:** rhetoric-vs-proof analysis.
- **Why it belongs here:** It prepares students to judge public speech and literary rhetoric without flattening rhetoric into mere manipulation.

### Week 17 — Literary Self-Deception

- **Likely title:** *How Characters Reason Badly About Themselves*
- **Source base:** Whately as a full toolkit applied to Shakespeare.
- **Essential question:** How do people use reasoning to hide from themselves?
- **Core concepts:** self-deception, motivated reasoning, hidden assumption, equivocation, rationalization, tragic error.
- **Core idea:** Logic is not only for public arguments. It also exposes the private arguments by which people justify what they already desire.
- **Student skill:** apply multiple Whately tools to one character's reasoning.
- **Macbeth / Caesar application:** Macbeth, Lady Macbeth, Brutus, Cassius, Antony, or another central speaker.
- **Portfolio artifact:** extended literary logic application.
- **Why it belongs here:** It turns the unit into literary reading rather than an isolated skills course.

### Week 18 — Logic Portfolio Workshop

- **Likely title:** *Revising the Record of Thought*
- **Source base:** student portfolio artifacts and selected review passages from Whately.
- **Essential question:** Which habits of logic have become visible in my own work?
- **Core concepts:** revision, reflection, transfer, intellectual habit.
- **Core idea:** A portfolio is not a pile of assignments. It should show development in judgment.
- **Student skill:** revise selected artifacts, write reflective captions, and organize evidence of growth.
- **Macbeth / Caesar application:** choose at least one Shakespeare artifact to revise in light of later tools.
- **Portfolio artifact:** revised portfolio with captions.
- **Why it belongs here:** Seniors should practice metacognitive revision before final defense.

### Week 19 — Final Portfolio Defense

- **Likely title:** *What It Means to Think Straight*
- **Source base:** Whately as a whole, plus the student's accumulated work.
- **Essential question:** What does it mean to be a disciplined thinker?
- **Core concepts:** disciplined reasoning, proof, terms, propositions, inference, evidence, fallacy, rhetoric, disagreement.
- **Core idea:** Logic is a set of intellectual habits: define terms, state claims clearly, test inference, inspect evidence, notice hidden assumptions, and distinguish real disputes from verbal confusion.
- **Student skill:** defend a claim about intellectual growth using evidence from the portfolio.
- **Macbeth / Caesar application:** final reflection may compare the student's growth in judgment with a Shakespearean failure of judgment.
- **Portfolio artifact:** final defense essay or oral defense notes.
- **Why it belongs here:** The unit should end with transfer. Students should be able to use Whately outside the logic unit.

## Comprehensive-enough test

A student who finishes the full unit should be able to:

1. Explain what logic can and cannot do.
2. Identify conclusions and stated reasons in complex arguments.
3. Surface hidden assumptions.
4. Fix ambiguous terms.
5. State claims in clear proposition form.
6. Distinguish universal, particular, affirmative, and negative claims.
7. Identify subject, predicate, and distribution when needed.
8. Identify practical syllogistic structure.
9. Classify basic mood and figure without treating memorization as the goal.
10. Distinguish validity from truth and soundness.
11. Explain why a fallacy can be persuasive.
12. Diagnose equivocation.
13. Diagnose circular reasoning.
14. Diagnose irrelevant proof.
15. Test analogies for relevant similarity.
16. Judge whether evidence supports the size of a claim.
17. Evaluate testimony and authority.
18. Distinguish discovery, proof, and judgment.
19. Distinguish verbal disputes from real disputes.
20. Separate rhetorical force from logical proof.
21. Repair weak arguments by narrowing claims, clarifying terms, or adding relevant support.
22. Apply logic repeatedly to *Macbeth*, *Julius Caesar*, public argument, classroom debate, and their own writing.

If the unit reaches those outcomes, it is comprehensive enough for a strong twelfth-grade introduction to logic as disciplined reasoning. It will not be a complete college course in symbolic or mathematical logic, and it should not pretend to be. Its purpose is broader and more humane: to form habits of judgment that seniors can carry into reading, writing, citizenship, and further study.

## Final assessment: Logic Portfolio

The portfolio is now the official final assessment. It should include both required artifacts and student-selected revisions.

### Required final portfolio contents

1. One argument skeleton from the opening weeks.
2. One term investigation.
3. One proposition translation set.
4. One practical syllogism map.
5. One mood-and-figure classification.
6. One validity/truth diagnosis.
7. Two fallacy autopsies.
8. One analogy test.
9. One induction/evidence test.
10. One credibility map.
11. One verbal-vs-real dispute map.
12. One rhetoric-vs-proof analysis.
13. One extended *Macbeth* or *Julius Caesar* application.
14. Final reflection or defense: “What does it mean to be a disciplined thinker?”

### Final defense prompt

**Prompt:** What does it mean to be a disciplined thinker? Defend your answer using Whately and at least three artifacts from your own portfolio. At least one artifact must come from *Macbeth* or *Julius Caesar*.

Requirements:

- clear thesis about disciplined thinking;
- accurate use of at least three logic concepts;
- direct reflection on the student's own growth;
- at least one revised artifact;
- at least one Shakespeare application;
- explanation of how the habit transfers beyond the logic unit.
