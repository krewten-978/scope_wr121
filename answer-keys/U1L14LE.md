**Purpose:** Students apply Week 14 discovery / proof / judgment tools to Macbeth's late discoveries and Caesar's response to warnings and interpretations.

## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

- **Discovery:** learning a fact or meaning not previously known.
- **Proof:** showing that a conclusion follows from reasons or evidence.
- **Judgment:** deciding how to act or believe when evidence, interpretation, and uncertainty remain.
- **Clarified term/sign:** a word, phrase, sign, prophecy, or image whose meaning must be made precise before reasoning from it.
- **Inference test:** asking whether the conclusion actually follows from the evidence or statement.
- **Tool map:** question; needed discovery; clarified term or sign; inference test; judgment.

Plot summary alone does not earn points.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Macbeth's late facts (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Macbeth discovers that Birnam Wood can “move” because soldiers carry branches toward Dunsinane, and he discovers that Macduff was not born in the ordinary way but “from his mother's womb untimely ripp'd.” These facts reveal limits in Macbeth's earlier understanding of the apparitions.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names both late discoveries accurately
- 1 pt: Names one discovery or gives a vague answer
- 0 pts: Incorrect or plot summary only

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Macbeth's phrase judged too quickly (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Students may use either phrase. “Birnam Wood shall come” did not have to mean literal trees walking; it could be fulfilled by soldiers carrying branches. “None of woman born” did not cover Macduff in the way Macbeth assumed. Later events reveal that Macbeth treated ambiguous words as settled guarantees.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Identifies a key phrase and explains the later revealed meaning
- 1 pt: Identifies a phrase but gives little explanation
- 0 pts: Says only that the witches lied or that Macbeth was unlucky

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – Apparitions not proof of safety (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The apparitions' words were testimony from a dangerous and ambiguous source, not a complete proof of safety. Macbeth had not clarified the terms, tested the source's reliability, or considered alternative meanings. One interpretation that favors his ambition does not prove the conclusion “I am safe.”

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Explains ambiguity/source limits and why safety does not follow
- 1 pt: Says the prophecy was unclear but gives little inference analysis
- 0 pts: Treats the apparitions as straightforward proof or only summarizes events

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Macbeth tool map (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong map includes: question (is Macbeth safe from defeat/harm?); needed discovery (what Birnam Wood and “woman born” can mean in actual events, and whether the source is trustworthy); clarified term (moving wood or woman-born); inference test (the words do not prove invulnerability); judgment (do not act with certainty or moral permission on ambiguous testimony).

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Gives a coherent Week 14 tool map with all major elements
- 1 pt: Includes some elements but incomplete
- 0 pts: Plot summary without tool-map reasoning

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Calpurnia and Decius (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Calpurnia offers warnings: frightening signs, reported sights, and her dream or fear that danger threatens Caesar. Decius offers an interpretation that turns the bloody statue image into a flattering sign of Caesar giving life to Rome. Calpurnia presents warning material; Decius reframes its meaning.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Distinguishes warning evidence/testimony from Decius's interpretation
- 1 pt: Mentions both speakers but blurs evidence and interpretation
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – Caesar's distorted judgment (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Caesar's pride, desire to appear fearless, concern for public honor, and susceptibility to Decius's flattering interpretation may distort his judgment. He prefers the meaning that makes him seem great and brave rather than carefully weighing danger.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names pressure/desire and connects it to Caesar's judgment
- 1 pt: Says Caesar is proud but gives little connection to interpretation
- 0 pts: Treats Decius's interpretation as obviously proven

## Question 7: Part 4 Q1 – Comparing tragic failures (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Macbeth acts on ambiguous testimony before discovering its possible meanings and before judging the source rightly. Caesar acts after receiving warnings but accepts a flattering interpretation without enough careful judgment. Both act before discovery and judgment are complete: Macbeth over-infers safety from prophecy; Caesar lets pride and interpretation overpower caution.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Compares both characters using discovery/proof/judgment terms
- 1 pt: Discusses one character well or gives a thin comparison
- 0 pts: Plot summary only

## Question 8: Part 4 Q2 – Alternative careful judgment (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
For Macbeth, a careful judgment might be: the apparitions' words are ambiguous and morally dangerous, so they should not be treated as proof of safety or permission to act. For Caesar, a careful judgment might be: the warnings and dream are uncertain, but Decius's flattering interpretation is also not proof; Caesar should investigate, delay, or weigh the danger without pride.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Gives a plausible careful alternative judgment grounded in the chosen passage
- 1 pt: Gives advice but with little Week 14 reasoning
- 0 pts: Unsupported moralizing or plot summary only

**Overall Notes for Grading This Worksheet:**
- Whole points only. Total: 8 × 2 = 16 points.
- Credit students who distinguish evidence from interpretation and who show that late discovery exposes earlier failures of judgment.
