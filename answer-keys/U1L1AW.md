# U1L1AW – Macbeth Anchor Worksheet Answer Key & Rubric

**Total Points:** 13

This answer key is based on the paired Week 1 Macbeth lit-anchor reading (`units/elements-of-logic-week1/tex/Whately_Logic_Week1_Macbeth_Lit_Anchor.tex` / `.pdf`) and the paired anchor worksheet (`units/elements-of-logic-week1/tex/Whately_Logic_Week1_Macbeth_Anchor_Worksheet.tex` / `.pdf`). Model answers use Whately's Week 1 four-part argument test: Conclusion, Stated reason, Hidden assumption, and Follows. Accept clear paraphrases, but require students to use the Macbeth reading rather than general plot memory alone.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Prophecy versus command (1 point)

**Expected Answer:**  
A strong answer says that the witches greet Macbeth as thane of Glamis, thane of Cawdor, and future king. They do not tell him to murder Duncan, do not say that murder is necessary, do not say kingship would be morally good, and do not give permission to do evil. The key distinction is that a prediction says what may or will happen, while a command tells someone what to do.

**Rubric:**
- 1 pt: Fully correct and complete — identifies what the witches tell Macbeth and what they do not tell him, and clearly distinguishes prediction from command
- 0 pts: Incorrect, missing, or treats the prophecy as if it directly commands murder

**Notes for grading:**
- Students do not need to quote every line, but they should name the three titles or clearly summarize them.
- Reward answers that explicitly say Macbeth adds interpretation beyond the witches' words.

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Macbeth's happy prologues inference (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**  
A strong answer identifies Macbeth's conclusion as something like: the confirmed titles point toward his becoming king, or the prophecy's final promise is now credible. The stated reason is that two titles have come true: he is already Glamis and Ross confirms him as Cawdor. The hidden assumption is that if part of the prophecy is true, the rest is reliable or should be treated as a guide. The follows judgment should be careful: it may give Macbeth reason to wonder, but it does not prove certainty, action, murder, or moral permission.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Fully correct and complete — accurately identifies conclusion, stated reason, hidden assumption, and a careful follows/not-follows judgment
- 1 pt: Partially correct — identifies some parts of the argument but misses or blurs one major element
- 0 pts: Incorrect, missing, or only retells the scene without analyzing Macbeth's reasoning

**Notes for grading:**
- Accept either "does not prove" or "does not fully follow" if the student explains the gap.
- Full credit should distinguish plausible evidence from proof or permission.

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – Banquo's source-credibility warning (1 point)

**Expected Answer:**  
A strong answer explains that Banquo asks whether a deceptive or evil source can tell the truth in one point. His warning matters logically because truth in one detail does not prove that the whole source is trustworthy. Banquo gives Macbeth a rival interpretation: the prophecy might be bait, using small truths to lead Macbeth toward harm in a larger matter.

**Rubric:**
- 1 pt: Fully correct and complete — explains Banquo's question as a source-credibility warning and names the rival interpretation
- 0 pts: Incorrect, missing, or treats Banquo's line as only fear or superstition with no logical importance

**Notes for grading:**
- Strong answers may quote or paraphrase "honest trifles" and "deepest consequence."
- Do not require theological analysis; grade the reasoning issue.

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Prediction is not permission (1 point)

**Expected Answer:**  
A strong answer explains that a predicted event does not automatically authorize a person to make it happen by any means. A possibility does not become a command simply because someone desires it. Macbeth has evidence that the prophecy may matter, but the reading shows that murder enters Macbeth's imagination before any command has been given. Evidence may raise a question; it does not prove permission.

**Rubric:**
- 1 pt: Fully correct and complete — explains the claim in the student's own words and uses a specific Act 1, Scene 3 moment as evidence
- 0 pts: Incorrect, missing, or gives only a vague statement with no Macbeth evidence

**Notes for grading:**
- Accept evidence from Ross's confirmation of Cawdor, Macbeth's "happy prologues" line, Banquo's warning, Macbeth's murder-imagination, or "without my stir."

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Macbeth's conclusion and reasons against murder (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**  
A strong answer says Macbeth's soliloquy seems to support the conclusion that he should not murder Duncan, or that murder is morally and practically dangerous. Reasons include: violent actions return to punish the doer; Duncan is Macbeth's kinsman; Macbeth is Duncan's subject; Macbeth is Duncan's host and should protect him; Duncan has ruled meekly and virtuously; public pity and moral outrage would condemn the murder. Students need at least three accurate reasons.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Fully correct and complete — states the likely conclusion and gives at least three accurate reasons from Act 1, Scene 7
- 1 pt: Partially correct — gives the conclusion with only one or two accurate reasons, or gives reasons but states the conclusion unclearly
- 0 pts: Incorrect, missing, or gives reasons not grounded in the passage

**Notes for grading:**
- Accept paraphrases of kinship, subjecthood, hospitality, Duncan's virtue, consequences, and pity/public judgment.
- Do not require line numbers.

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – Ambition as spur (1 point)

**Expected Answer:**  
A strong answer explains that Macbeth names ambition as his only positive spur toward murder. Ambition is primarily a desire or motive, not a proof that he should act. It may explain why Macbeth wants the crown, but it does not show that murder is justified, wise, or morally permissible. Some students may call it both a motive and a weak reason, but full credit requires saying that ambition alone does not prove the action should be taken.

**Rubric:**
- 1 pt: Fully correct and complete — identifies ambition as desire/motive and explains why it does not prove Macbeth should murder Duncan
- 0 pts: Incorrect, missing, or treats ambition alone as sufficient proof

**Notes for grading:**
- Accept "both" only if the answer still distinguishes motive from valid proof.

## Question 7: Part 3 Q3 – Testing the argument against murder (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**  
A strong answer tests the argument that Macbeth should not murder Duncan. The conclusion is that he should not murder Duncan. The stated reasons include consequences returning on the murderer, duties as kinsman/subject/host, Duncan's virtue, and the public/moral horror of the deed. The hidden assumption is that these moral duties and consequences are strong reasons not to murder. The conclusion follows strongly from the reasons Macbeth gives. Macbeth's reasoning against murder is much stronger than any argument from prophecy or ambition for murder.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Fully correct and complete — uses Macbeth's reasons to explain why the conclusion follows, with clear attention to reasoning structure
- 1 pt: Partially correct — says Macbeth should not murder Duncan but gives limited explanation or weak connection to the reasons
- 0 pts: Incorrect, missing, or ignores the Act 1, Scene 7 reasoning

**Notes for grading:**
- Students may say the argument is morally strong rather than formally deductive; accept this if the explanation is clear.
- Full credit should not merely state that murder is bad in general; it should use Macbeth's stated reasons.

## Question 8: Part 4 Q1 – Macbeth argument skeleton (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**  
A strong answer chooses one listed claim and gives a clear four-part skeleton. Examples: For "The prophecy proves Macbeth will be king," the conclusion is that Macbeth will be king; the stated reason is the prophecy and the confirmation of Cawdor; the hidden assumption is that one fulfilled part proves the rest; the follows judgment is that it gives some evidence but does not prove certainty. For "If Macbeth is destined to be king, he is not responsible," the hidden assumption is that destiny removes choice or responsibility; the conclusion does not follow from prediction alone. For "Macbeth should not kill Duncan," the stated reasons come from Act 1, Scene 7 and the conclusion follows strongly. For "Ambition is Macbeth's only real reason," the evidence is Macbeth's own "no spur" line, but students should distinguish reason, motive, and proof.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Fully correct and complete — chooses one claim and accurately provides claim/conclusion, stated reason, hidden assumption, and follows judgment
- 1 pt: Partially correct — chooses a claim and gives some correct parts but leaves one or more major elements vague or missing
- 0 pts: Incorrect, missing, or summarizes Macbeth without completing the four-part skeleton

**Notes for grading:**
- Accept any of the four listed claims.
- The student's follows judgment must match the claim chosen.

## Question 9: Part 4 Q2 – Logic, reasoning, and virtue (1 point)

**Expected Answer:**  
A strong paragraph explains that logic helps expose the gap between Macbeth's evidence and his conclusions: prophecy is not permission, possibility is not command, and ambition is not proof. It also explains Whately's limit: logic can show Macbeth that murder is not justified, but logic cannot make Macbeth virtuous or force him to obey better judgment. The paragraph should use at least one specific moment, such as Banquo's warning, Macbeth's "without my stir," the Act 1, Scene 7 reasons against murder, or the "vaulting ambition" line.

**Rubric:**
- 1 pt: Fully correct and complete — explains both what logic can do and what it cannot do, using a specific Macbeth moment
- 0 pts: Incorrect, missing, or gives only a vague statement about logic or Macbeth

**Notes for grading:**
- Reward answers that explicitly connect Macbeth to Whately's modest view of logic.
- The paragraph does not need to be long, but it must make a complete claim and support it.
