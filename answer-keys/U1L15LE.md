**Purpose:** Students apply Week 15 verbal / real / mixed dispute mapping to Macbeth and Lady Macbeth's dispute over manhood and Brutus / Antony's public dispute over Caesar's ambition and honor.

## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

- **Verbal dispute:** a disagreement mainly over how a word is being used.
- **Real dispute:** a disagreement that remains after the word is clarified.
- **Mixed dispute:** a disagreement containing both contested word meaning and a real disagreement underneath.
- **Contested term:** the powerful word whose meaning is disputed or pressured.
- **Shared claim:** something both sides could accept once terms are clarified.
- **Remaining disagreement:** what still needs evidence, proof, or judgment after clarification.
- **Dispute map:** contested term; each side's meaning; shared claim; remaining disagreement; classification.

Plot summary alone does not earn points.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Lady Macbeth's use of man (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The contested term is “man.” Lady Macbeth uses it to mean bold, daring, and willing to carry out the murder. She treats Macbeth's masculinity or courage as something proved by doing the violent deed he had promised.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Identifies “man” and explains Lady Macbeth's meaning accurately
- 1 pt: Identifies the term but gives a thin meaning
- 0 pts: Plot summary only or incorrect term

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Macbeth's meaning of man (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Macbeth's reply suggests that manhood includes moral limits: he dares what “may become a man,” but doing more than that makes someone less than truly human or morally monstrous. His meaning connects courage to fitting action, not mere violence.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Explains Macbeth's contrasting meaning with moral boundary/fitting action
- 1 pt: Says Macbeth disagrees but gives little definition
- 0 pts: Treats Macbeth as simply cowardly without analyzing the term

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – Remaining Macbeth disagreement (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
After clarifying the word, they still disagree about the deed itself: whether murdering Duncan is courage or moral excess, whether ambition justifies action, whether loyalty and hospitality should restrain Macbeth, and whether doing the deed would make him more admirable or less human.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names a real disagreement that remains after word clarification
- 1 pt: Gives a vague disagreement about murder or courage
- 0 pts: Says the disagreement disappears once “man” is defined

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Macbeth dispute classification (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The dispute is mixed. It is verbal because the word “man” carries different meanings for the speakers. It is real because even after defining manhood, Macbeth and Lady Macbeth still disagree about whether murder is courageous, fitting, loyal, or morally corrupt.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Classifies as mixed and explains both verbal and real parts
- 1 pt: Gives a plausible classification with weak explanation
- 0 pts: Classifies without reasons or summarizes plot

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Brutus's claim and contested term (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Brutus claims Caesar was ambitious and that he killed Caesar because of that ambition. The contested term is “ambitious” (with “honourable” also pressured in the broader speech). The crowd must know what ambition means before judging whether Brutus's claim proves Caesar deserved death.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: States Brutus's claim and identifies the contested term
- 1 pt: Gives claim or term only
- 0 pts: Incorrect or plot summary only

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – Antony's examples and pressure (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Antony points to Caesar bringing captives and ransom money to Rome and weeping for the poor. These examples pressure Brutus's use of “ambitious” because they suggest public benefit and compassion rather than the hard self-seeking ambition Antony says should be “made of sterner stuff.”

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names Antony's examples and explains how they challenge the term
- 1 pt: Names examples but gives little connection to ambition
- 0 pts: Says only that Antony is sarcastic or persuasive

## Question 7: Part 4 Q1 – Caesar dispute map (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong map includes: contested term: ambitious. Brutus's meaning: dangerously power-seeking, a threat to Roman freedom. Antony's pressure: Caesar's ransom money and tears for the poor do not fit that harsh meaning. Shared claim: if Caesar were dangerously ambitious, that would be serious; Caesar was powerful and politically important. Remaining disagreement: whether Caesar's actions prove dangerous ambition and whether killing him was justified. Classification: mixed, because word meaning matters but evidence and political judgment remain disputed.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Completes a coherent dispute map with classification
- 1 pt: Includes some map elements but incomplete
- 0 pts: Plot summary without dispute analysis

## Question 8: Part 4 Q2 – Why clarification helps but does not settle (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
In both passages, clarifying the word helps reveal the real issue: manhood is not just daring, and ambition is not just power. But clarification does not settle whether Macbeth should kill Duncan or whether Caesar was dangerous enough to kill. The real conflict still needs moral judgment, evidence, and proof.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Compares both passages and explains clarification plus remaining dispute
- 1 pt: Discusses one passage well or gives a thin comparison
- 0 pts: Says definitions solve everything or gives only plot summary

**Overall Notes for Grading This Worksheet:**
- Whole points only. Total: 8 × 2 = 16 points.
