# U1L2LE – Lit Example Worksheet Answer Key & Rubric

**Total Points:** 12

**Paired reader:** `units/elements-of-logic-week2/lit-examples/U1L2LE_lit_example_reader.tex` / `.pdf`.

**Paired worksheet:** `graded-assignments/U1L2LE/worksheet.tex` / `.pdf` (Assignment ID **U1L2LE**).

**Purpose:** Students apply the Week 2 Whately habit of defining terms and detecting ambiguity/equivocation to Macbeth passages. Grade for precise term work and logical explanation, not for plot summary or literary-theme naming.

## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

Use these terms consistently when evaluating student answers:

- **Term:** the important word or phrase whose meaning affects the reasoning.
- **Sense:** one specific meaning of a term in context.
- **Ambiguity:** a term has more than one possible meaning and needs clarification before reasoning from it.
- **Equivocation:** a term shifts from one sense to another inside an argument, making the inference look stronger than it is.
- **Inference check:** the student's explanation of whether the argument still works once the term's senses are separated.

Do not require students to use the word “equivocation” if they clearly identify the word-shift and its logical effect. Do require them to state the relevant senses in ordinary language.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Fair and foul as shifting terms (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong answer identifies that **fair** can mean beautiful, attractive, favorable, honorable, or seemingly good, while **foul** can mean ugly, filthy, unfavorable, corrupt, or morally evil. The answer should explain that confusing the senses could mislead Macbeth: a thing may look promising or bring success without being morally good, and a grim or bloody day may still be militarily successful. The logical danger is moving from “this seems favorable” to “this is right” or from “this is ugly/frightening” to “this is morally false” without proof.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Gives plausible multiple senses for both terms and explains the logical danger of confusing them
- 1 pt: Gives partial senses or a general theme answer with limited logical explanation
- 0 pts: Gives only plot summary or says the words mean the same thing with no clarification

**Notes for grading:**
- Accept varied phrasing if the student distinguishes outward/favorable senses from moral senses.
- Do not require a claim that the witches' line is a formal fallacy; the prompt asks about possible misleading confusion.

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Contradiction or different senses? (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong answer says Macbeth's line need not be a strict contradiction if **foul** and **fair** are being used in different senses. The day can be foul because it is bloody, stormy, ugly, or morally unsettling, and fair because it is victorious, favorable, or promising. A contradiction would require the same thing being affirmed and denied in the same respect. If the respects differ, the line is paradoxical or layered, not simple logical contradiction.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Distinguishes contradiction from different senses/respects and applies it to Macbeth's line
- 1 pt: Notices two meanings but does not clearly explain why that matters logically
- 0 pts: Treats the line as merely confusing or summarizes the scene without term analysis

**Notes for grading:**
- Full credit should include the “same respect” idea even if not in those exact words.

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – Macbeth's sense of man (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong answer identifies **man** as the key term. Macbeth uses it to mean a morally bounded human being or honorable man: courage is real, but it must stay within what “becomes” a man. His limit is that daring beyond moral boundaries does not make someone more manly; it makes him “none,” less than the proper human standard. The answer may describe Macbeth's sense as ethical manhood, honorable courage, or human self-command.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Correctly states Macbeth's sense of **man** and the moral limit he draws
- 1 pt: Understands Macbeth is resisting murder but gives a vague account of the term
- 0 pts: Says only that Macbeth is afraid or weak without explaining his use of **man**

**Notes for grading:**
- Reward answers that show Macbeth is not rejecting courage but redefining courage under moral restraint.

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Lady Macbeth's sense of man (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong answer identifies Lady Macbeth's sense of **man** as daring, boldness, completion of the promised deed, or refusal to be a coward. Her pressure depends on shifting from Macbeth's ethical sense of manhood to a performative or aggressive sense: if he dares the murder, he is a man; if he refuses, he is less than a man. The inference is unreliable if the two senses are not the same, because proving boldness does not prove moral manhood or right action.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: States Lady Macbeth's sense of **man** and explains the word-shift from Macbeth's sense
- 1 pt: Notices she is challenging his courage but does not clearly identify the equivocation
- 0 pts: Gives plot summary or treats her insult as proof that Macbeth should act

**Notes for grading:**
- Students may call this ambiguity, equivocation, pressure, or redefinition; grade the logical explanation.

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Done as physical completion versus settlement (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong answer identifies at least two senses of **done**: physically completed/performed, finished in its consequences, morally settled, safely over, or free from further judgment. Macbeth imagines that if the murder could be “done” in the small physical sense and also “done” in the larger consequence/moral sense, quick action might seem attractive. But those senses are not equivalent. A blow can be completed while consequences, guilt, justice, retaliation, or judgment continue.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Identifies two relevant senses of **done** and explains why the inference from completion to settlement fails
- 1 pt: Identifies a general distinction but leaves the logical consequence vague
- 0 pts: Paraphrases the passage with no term distinction

**Notes for grading:**
- Full credit should not require theological claims; practical consequence and moral judgment are enough.

## Question 6: Part 4 Q1 – Building and clarifying an equivocation (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Answers will vary. A strong answer chooses one of the listed terms, builds a short argument where the term shifts, and then rewrites it so the shift is explicit. Examples: “The prophecy is fair because it is favorable; fair things are morally good; therefore the prophecy is morally good” should be clarified by separating favorable from morally good. “A real man dares danger; murder is dangerous; therefore murder proves manhood” should be clarified by separating reckless daring from honorable manhood. “The murder can be done quickly; what is done is settled; therefore the murder will be settled quickly” should be clarified by separating physical completion from moral/practical settlement.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Creates a relevant shifted-term argument and rewrites it so the two senses are visible
- 1 pt: Chooses a relevant term but gives an incomplete argument or weak clarification
- 0 pts: Gives only a theme statement, plot summary, or no clear term-shift

**Notes for grading:**
- Because the prompt allows choice, grade method and clarity rather than one predetermined answer.

**Overall Notes for Grading This Worksheet:**
- Whole points only. No half points.
- Grade the student's term discipline: exact word, first sense, second sense, and inference check.
- Do not reward long plot summaries unless they support precise term analysis.
- Total: 6 questions × 2 points = 12 points.
