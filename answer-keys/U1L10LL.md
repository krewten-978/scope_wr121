## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

- **Point at issue:** the exact question the argument must answer for its conclusion to be proved.
- **Irrelevant proof / missing the point:** evidence may prove something, but not the conclusion that needed proof.
- **Nearby claim:** a related claim that may be true or supported while the larger conclusion remains unproved.
- **Partial proof:** support that proves only part of what the argument needs.
- **Repair:** adding evidence that answers the real issue or narrowing the conclusion to fit the evidence.
- **Review terms:** prior-week skills only: proposition/syllogism structure, validity, loaded definition, and circular reasoning.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Senior trip bus drivers (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The point at issue is whether the trip should be moved from Friday to Monday. Friendly drivers may show the bus company is pleasant or easy to work with, but it does not prove the date should change. Relevant evidence would concern scheduling, safety, conflicts, weather, cost, or availability.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: States the issue and explains why the evidence misses it
- 1 pt: Vague “not relevant” without clear issue
- 0 pts: Treats friendly drivers as sufficient proof

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Easy article (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The evidence proves, at most, that the article is readable/accessible. It does not prove reliability for research. Reliability would require source expertise, evidence, citations, publication context, accuracy, corroboration, and lack of major bias or distortion.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Distinguishes readability from reliability and names missing proof
- 1 pt: Partial distinction
- 0 pts: Says easy reading proves reliability

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – Riverfront plan (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The nearby claim is that the current riverfront is ugly or needs improvement. The larger conclusion still needing proof is that this particular riverfront plan should be chosen. Students may note missing evidence about cost, alternatives, environmental effects, usefulness, or priority.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Nearby claim plus unproved larger conclusion
- 1 pt: Names one side only
- 0 pts: Treats ugliness as full proof for the plan

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Team captain points (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Scoring most points may prove Jordan is a strong scorer/player. It does not by itself prove Jordan should be captain, because captaincy may require leadership, judgment, communication, reliability, sportsmanship, and team trust.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Clear nearby claim and missing captaincy criteria
- 1 pt: Partial
- 0 pts: Treats scoring as automatically sufficient

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Homework policy repair (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong answer either adds evidence that the policy is fair (reasonable workload, equal application, chance to ask questions, accommodations, consistent enforcement) or narrows the conclusion (“the policy was clearly announced,” not “therefore fair”). Clarity alone is not fairness.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Non-mismatched repair or narrowed conclusion
- 1 pt: Some improvement but still thin
- 0 pts: Repeats “fair because announced”

## Question 6: Part 4 Q1 – Roof and scoreboard paragraph (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Main conclusion: buy scoreboards before replacing the roof. Evidence offered: sports unify the school, team success, louder cheering, modern gym appearance. Nearby point proved: sports/scoreboards may have morale value. Unanswered question: whether that priority should outrank roof repair, water damage, cost, safety, and library needs.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Conclusion + offered evidence + nearby point + unanswered issue
- 1 pt: Two or three elements
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 7: Part 5 Q1 – Review: fair rules (1 point)

**Expected Answer:**
Structure: All fair rules are rules that let everyone speak; this rule lets everyone speak; therefore this rule is fair. This is an invalid converse-style move / affirming the predicate or undistributed middle problem. The conclusion does not follow from the stated premise.

**Rubric:**
- 1 pt: Identifies the structural problem and says conclusion does not follow
- 0 pts: Says valid without qualification or misses structure

## Question 8: Part 5 Q2 – Review: true leader (1 point)

**Expected Answer:**
Main defect: loaded definition / circular reasoning. “True leader” is defined so that changing one's mind automatically disqualifies Maya; the verdict is built into the definition rather than independently proved.

**Rubric:**
- 1 pt: Names loaded definition/circularity and explains briefly
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing
