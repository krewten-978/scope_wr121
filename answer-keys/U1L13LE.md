**Purpose:** Students apply Week 13 testimony / credibility-map diagnosis to the witches in *Macbeth* and Brutus in *Julius Caesar*.

## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

- **Testimony:** a claim offered by a source, witness, speaker, or record.
- **Credibility:** the degree to which a source deserves trust in this case.
- **Access:** whether the source was in a position to know the claim.
- **Bias / pressure:** a motive, interest, fear, ambition, or need for self-defense that may tilt testimony.
- **Corroboration:** independent support from events, evidence, or other sources.
- **Careful conclusion:** a trust judgment stated at the right strength rather than all-or-nothing trust or rejection.

Plot summary alone does not earn points.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Witches' claims and corroboration (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The witches call Macbeth Thane of Glamis, Thane of Cawdor, and future king. Macbeth already is Glamis; the surprising claim soon corroborated by Ross is that Macbeth has been named Thane of Cawdor. The kingship claim remains a prediction, not yet confirmed.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names the claims and identifies Cawdor as the soon-corroborated claim
- 1 pt: Names some claims but unclear corroboration
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Why corroboration tempts Macbeth (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The Cawdor confirmation makes the witches tempting because they appear to know hidden or future information Macbeth did not know. One accurate and unexpected detail increases the psychological force of the rest of their testimony.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Explains why accurate hidden knowledge increases apparent credibility
- 1 pt: Says only that one prediction came true
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – What remains uncertain (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The kingship claim remains uncorroborated. The witches' source of knowledge, moral trustworthiness, meaning, and purpose remain uncertain. Cawdor being true does not prove that Macbeth must become king, should seek the crown, or should trust every implication of their speech.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Identifies uncorroborated kingship plus limits of meaning/source/trust
- 1 pt: Notes only that Macbeth is not king yet
- 0 pts: Treats Cawdor as proof of everything

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Careful conclusion about witches (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A careful conclusion: Macbeth has reason to take the witches seriously because one claim is corroborated, but he should not treat them as fully trustworthy guides. The confirmed Cawdor claim supports attention, not obedience or certainty about kingship.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Gives a balanced trust judgment with probability language
- 1 pt: Overtrusts or dismisses them but includes some reason
- 0 pts: Says simply “trust them” or “ignore them” with no credibility analysis

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Brutus's claim and access (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Brutus testifies that Caesar was ambitious and that killing him served Rome's freedom more than Brutus's personal feeling for Caesar. Brutus has access because he knew Caesar, was a Roman political actor, and participated in the decision. He has some competence as a public man judging political danger.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: States central claim and relevant access/competence
- 1 pt: States claim or access only
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – Brutus's pressure and needed corroboration (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Brutus has strong pressure to justify an assassination he helped commit and to preserve his honor before the crowd. Corroboration would include specific public actions by Caesar, independent witnesses, laws or records, Caesar's own words, or evidence that Caesar intended tyranny. Antony's later counterexamples would also test Brutus's testimony.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names pressure/bias and specific corroborating evidence
- 1 pt: Mentions bias or evidence only
- 0 pts: Treats Brutus's honor as enough proof

## Question 7: Part 4 Q1 – Comparing source credibility (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Strong answers compare both sources without flattening them. The witches have one startlingly corroborated detail, but their purpose and reliability remain suspect. Brutus is honorable and close to events, but he is defending his own violent act and gives limited evidence in the speech. One accurate detail or one honorable speaker can increase credibility, but neither settles every claim without relevant corroboration.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Compares both sources and explains why credibility remains limited
- 1 pt: Discusses one source well or gives a thin comparison
- 0 pts: Plot summary only

## Question 8: Part 4 Q2 – Credibility map (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Students may choose either source. A strong witches map includes: claim (Cawdor/kingship), source (witches), access (strange knowledge of Cawdor), bias/pressure (unclear motive, tempting ambition), corroboration (Ross confirms Cawdor; kingship not confirmed), careful conclusion (attend but do not fully trust). A strong Brutus map includes: claim (Caesar was ambitious/dangerous), source (Brutus), access (knew Caesar/public role), bias/pressure (must justify assassination), corroboration/conflict (needs Caesar's actions, records, Antony's counterevidence), careful conclusion (plausible but not settled by speech alone).

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Completes a coherent credibility map with careful conclusion
- 1 pt: Includes some elements but incomplete
- 0 pts: Summary or unsupported trust judgment

**Overall Notes for Grading This Worksheet:**
- Whole points only. Total: 8 × 2 = 16 points.
- Credit students who avoid all-or-nothing answers. Week 13 asks how far testimony deserves trust, not whether every source is either perfect or worthless.
