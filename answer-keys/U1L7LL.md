## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

- **Fallacy:** reasoning that seems to prove a conclusion but fails to prove it.
- **False claim:** an untrue statement used in the argument.
- **Bad inference:** a leap from reasons to conclusion that does not follow, even if some claims are true.
- **Appearance of proof:** the surface look of support (confidence, moral pressure, crowd language) without earned proof.
- **Fallacy autopsy:** diagnosis of conclusion, apparent proof, defect, and repair/narrowed claim.
- **Form test:** if the premises were true, would the conclusion have to follow?
- **Fact test:** are the premises true, false, doubtful, or unproved?
- **Undistributed middle:** a formal flaw in which the middle term never covers its whole class, so the extremes are not forced together.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Transfer students (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Main failure is a **false claim** (the universal premise that all transfer students skip advisory is false or at least grossly unsupported). The inference shape may look tidy, but the argument fails because the general premise is not true. “Both” is acceptable if the student notes that the universal premise is false and therefore cannot support the particular conclusion.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names false claim as main failure and briefly explains
- 1 pt: Partial identification without clear explanation
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

**Notes for grading:**
- Do not require the word “major premise.”

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Devon and learning (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Main failure is a **bad inference**. Even if Devon spoke against the phone policy, it does not follow that Devon does not care about learning. Policy disagreement is compatible with serious concern for school.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names bad inference and explains the leap
- 1 pt: Says the argument is weak without naming the leap clearly
- 0 pts: Treats the conclusion as forced or only calls a random claim false

**Notes for grading:**
- Accept “does not follow” language.

**Expected Answer:**
**Appearance of strength:** moral/loyalty language, either/or pressure, confident “therefore,” social shame for dissent. **Defect:** first premise is doubtful/false, and questioning a schedule does not prove opposition to school pride (bad inference / overclaim).

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names both appearance and defect
- 1 pt: Names only one side clearly
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

**Notes for grading:**
- Credit emotional force or rhetorical force wording.

## Question 4: Part 3 Q1 – Real teammate autopsy (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
(1) **Conclusion:** you are not a real teammate. (2) **Apparent proof:** loyalty/identity test plus vote as supposed evidence of character. (3) **Defect:** false or stacked definition of “real teammate,” plus bad inference from one vote to full character. (4) **Repair:** a fairer claim such as “you disagree about overnight camp” or “we should debate the training plan on its merits,” not a character expulsion.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: All four autopsy pieces present and coherent
- 1 pt: Two or three pieces clear
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

**Notes for grading:**
- Order may vary if all four ideas appear.

## Question 5: Part 4 Q1 – Dress code paragraph (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
**Conclusion:** the dress code must be accepted without debate / objectors should stop opposing it. **Apparent proof:** loyalty language, “every loyal student already supports it,” silence as proof, troublemaker label. **Defect:** bad inference and pressure; loyalty and silence do not prove the policy is right or that debate is illegitimate. Distractors (soup, noise, teacher) are not the main argument.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Clear conclusion + apparent proof + defect on the main argument
- 1 pt: Partial autopsy or mixes distractors as the main claim
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

**Notes for grading:**
- Students should not treat cafeteria noise or soup jokes as the argument.
