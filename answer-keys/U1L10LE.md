# U1L10LE – Lit Example Worksheet Answer Key & Rubric

**Total Points:** 16

**Paired reader:** `units/elements-of-logic-week10/lit-examples/U1L10LE_lit_example_reader.tex` / `.pdf`.

**Paired worksheet:** `graded-assignments/U1L10LE/worksheet.tex` / `.pdf` (Assignment ID **U1L10LE**).

**Purpose:** Students apply Week 10 irrelevant-proof / point-at-issue diagnosis to Brutus and Antony in *Julius Caesar*.

## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

- **Point at issue:** the exact claim the speaker must prove for the argument to succeed.
- **Nearby claim:** a related claim that may be proved without proving the full conclusion.
- **Irrelevant proof / missing the point:** proof that answers a different or narrower question than the conclusion requires.
- **Partial proof:** evidence that supports only part of the case.
- **Repair or narrow:** add missing proof for the stronger conclusion or reduce the conclusion to what the evidence supports.

Plot summary alone does not earn points.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Brutus's required conclusion (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Brutus needs to prove that killing Caesar was justified or necessary for Rome's freedom, not merely that Caesar was imperfect, powerful, or ambitious.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Clear necessary/justified killing conclusion
- 1 pt: Vague “Caesar was bad/ambitious”
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Nearby ambition claim (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Brutus tries to prove that Caesar was ambitious and that ambition threatens Roman freedom or slavery. This is related to the assassination question but does not automatically settle it.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Identifies ambition/freedom nearby claim and relation to issue
- 1 pt: Mentions ambition only
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – Ambition vs death gap (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Even if Caesar was ambitious, Brutus still needs to prove that his ambition was dangerous enough, imminent enough, and unmanageable enough to justify killing him. Ambition alone may support caution or resistance, but not necessarily assassination.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Explains the proof gap clearly
- 1 pt: General doubt about Brutus without naming the gap
- 0 pts: Treats ambition as sufficient proof of death

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Slaves/free men alternative (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The alternative may prove that slavery would be terrible and freedom valuable, but it assumes that leaving Caesar alive means slavery. That nearby point pressures the crowd without fully proving the causal danger.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Distinguishes valuable freedom claim from unproved Caesar-causes-slavery claim
- 1 pt: Calls it emotional pressure only
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Antony's smaller claims (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Acceptable examples: captives' ransoms support that Caesar benefited Rome financially; weeping for the poor supports compassion; refusing the crown supports restraint or at least hesitation about kingship. Students should state what each piece actually proves without overstating it.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Two pieces of evidence with accurate smaller claims
- 1 pt: One accurate piece or vague listing
- 0 pts: Incorrect or plot-only

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – Antony's narrower answer (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Antony's evidence strongly answers or weakens the charge that Caesar was simply ambitious in the tyrannical sense. It does not by itself fully prove every conspirator was morally wrong, every motive was corrupt, or that no danger existed. Strong answers may say Antony gives partial but powerful proof.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Distinguishes narrower charge from larger verdict
- 1 pt: Says “Antony proves Brutus wrong” without nuance
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 7: Part 4 Q1 – Repair Brutus's case (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong rewrite either adds evidence needed for the strong conclusion (specific plans to enslave Rome, abuses of power, failed lawful remedies, imminent danger) or narrows the conclusion (“Caesar's ambition gave Romans reason for vigilance,” not “therefore assassination was justified”).

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Clear added evidence or narrowed conclusion
- 1 pt: Partial repair
- 0 pts: Still mismatched or circular

## Question 8: Part 4 Q2 – Capstone (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong paragraph explains that Brutus and Antony can be powerful and partly truthful while still needing a prompt-match test. Week 10 helps readers ask whether a speech proves the exact issue or only a nearby claim, so literary power is respected but not mistaken for completed proof.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Applies Week 10 tool to both speech/proof and literary power
- 1 pt: Thin application
- 0 pts: Plot summary only

**Overall Notes for Grading This Worksheet:**
- Whole points only. Total: 8 × 2 = 16 points.
- Credit students who distinguish “partly proves” from “proves nothing.” Week 10 is about proof-to-claim match, not dismissing all rhetoric.
