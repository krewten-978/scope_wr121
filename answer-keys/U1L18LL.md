# U1L18LL – Logic Lab Answer Key & Rubric

**Total Points:** 20

**Paired worksheet:** `graded-assignments/U1L18LL/worksheet.tex` / `.pdf` (Assignment ID **U1L18LL**).

**Paired reading:** Week 18 — portfolio selection, criterion-based diagnosis, substantive revision, reflective captions, and transfer.

## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

- **Artifact:** a completed piece of course work that records reasoning at a particular moment.
- **Criterion:** a fitting logical standard used to judge a precise feature of reasoning.
- **Substantive revision:** a change to claim, evidence, inference, assumption, scope, or judgment—not merely proofreading.
- **Revision trail:** original reasoning, diagnosis, revision, and reflective caption kept together.
- **Reflective caption:** an evidence-based explanation of what changed, why, and what the change shows.
- **Portfolio claim:** a claim about growth supported by identifiable features in more than one artifact.

Personalized answers are expected. Grade the required reasoning structure and the student's cited evidence; do not require one predetermined artifact.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Identify and recover an artifact (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The response identifies a real course artifact by assignment, title, week, or recognizable task; states its original claim/task; and quotes or accurately describes one feature of the student's original reasoning. Generic claims such as “my logic lab” without recoverable detail are insufficient.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Identifiable artifact, original task/claim, and concrete original feature
- 1 pt: Artifact identified but reasoning described vaguely or incompletely
- 0 pts: No recoverable artifact or invented/general discussion

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Justify the revision candidate (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Explains a visible before-and-after possibility, such as clarifying a shifting term, narrowing an overclaim, repairing an inference, testing a source, replacing irrelevant support, or deepening a literary analysis. Score is not treated as sufficient evidence by itself.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names a specific, logically meaningful growth contrast
- 1 pt: Plausible candidate with a vague growth claim
- 0 pts: Chooses only because of grade, neatness, or preference

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – Recover the reasoning (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Accurately states the artifact's conclusion, its evidence/premises/support, and an unstated assumption or inferential bridge. Equivalent vocabulary is acceptable if the logical roles are clear.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Conclusion, support, and assumption/inference are coherent
- 1 pt: Two elements clear or all three thin
- 0 pts: Summary without logical recovery

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Diagnose by criterion (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Names one exact weakness or limit, a fitting Whately criterion, and the feature tested. Acceptable criteria include term stability, proposition precision, validity/form, premise truth, hidden assumption, circularity, point-at-issue relevance, analogy, induction/evidence proportion, source credibility, dispute classification, rhetoric-vs-proof, or motivated reasoning.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Precise feature, fitting criterion, and accurate diagnosis
- 1 pt: Real weakness but weak fit or little textual specificity
- 0 pts: Vague self-criticism or a tool name without application

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Revise the claim (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Produces a claim that directly answers the diagnosis and remains proportionate to the artifact's available support. The revision may narrow, qualify, clarify, or otherwise repair the original conclusion.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Substantive, responsive, supportable revised claim
- 1 pt: Directionally improved but still vague or overbroad
- 0 pts: Copyedit only or unrelated replacement

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – Revise support or inference (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Changes the reasoning itself: adds relevant evidence, removes irrelevant material, stabilizes a term, makes an assumption explicit, qualifies an induction, tests a source, repairs the inferential bridge, or explains what evidence is still needed. The change should help earn Question 3.1's claim.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Concrete revision strengthens the claim-support connection
- 1 pt: Useful change with incomplete connection
- 0 pts: Cosmetic edit or unsupported assertion

## Question 7: Part 4 Q1 – Reflective caption (3 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The caption identifies the original aim, the diagnosed weakness, the logical habit used, the substantive before-and-after change, and what the revision demonstrates about judgment. Honest limited growth is stronger than unsupported claims of total transformation.

**Rubric:**
- 3 pts: Complete, evidence-based caption with all major elements
- 2 pts: Sound caption missing one major element
- 1 pt: General reflection with little before-and-after evidence
- 0 pts: Praise, apology, or summary only

## Question 8: Part 4 Q2 – Connect two artifacts (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Identifies a second artifact and one defensible recurring habit or pattern—such as narrower claims, stable definitions, tested assumptions, proportionate evidence, or improved source judgment—with concrete evidence from both artifacts.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Coherent pattern plus evidence from both artifacts
- 1 pt: Pattern plausible but one artifact/evidence link is vague
- 0 pts: Two titles listed without a growth connection

## Question 9: Part 5 Q1 – Review: transit induction (1 point)

**Expected Answer:**
Improvement: the revision narrows a system-wide universal overclaim to a limited route-and-trip claim proportionate to three observations. Remaining need: a larger, representative sample across dates, times, routes, or system records before a broader reliability conclusion.

**Rubric:**
- 1 pt: Names both proportionate narrowing and a real evidence need
- 0 pts: Says only that the wording is better

## Question 10: Part 5 Q2 – Review: rhetoric and proof (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A repaired judgment separates emotional force from evidence: the speech's emotion does not itself prove the safety claims, but neither does emotion show that no evidence exists. The evaluator must inspect the stated reasons/data and decide whether rhetorical force matches or exceeds them.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Rejects both emotion-as-proof and emotion-means-no-proof, with a valid test
- 1 pt: Correct distinction but incomplete repair
- 0 pts: Repeats either extreme
