# AI Prompt: Plan and Prototype the Logic Dungeon Assessments for scope_wr121

Use this prompt in a fresh AI context to plan the full Logic Dungeon assessment arc for the scope_wr121 Whately logic units, then build the first prototype for Weeks 1–3.

---

You are helping build the scope_wr121 12th-grade Richard Whately logic curriculum. The course currently has a weekly rhythm:

1. Student Reading handout
2. Logic Lab worksheet
3. Lit Example reader/worksheet
4. Periodic larger puzzle assessment

The old idea was to make a puzzle-like logic test for each individual unit/week, in the spirit of Lewis Carroll’s logic puzzles. That sometimes worked, but it was often too hard to make a genuinely interesting puzzle from one narrow week’s concept. The new idea is to create one larger adventure-style puzzle assessment after every three units/weeks. This gives students a wider set of logic tools and allows richer puzzle design.

The recurring assessment frame is a dungeon/adventure arc. Students are not merely taking tests; they are helping a group of explorers read inscriptions, avoid traps, find secret tunnels, interpret warnings, and eventually reach a final castle.

The final castle is named:

Castrum Malae Ratiocinationis

Do not translate this name for students. If they want to know what it means, they can look it up. For teacher/design context only, it means “Castle of Bad Reasoning.” Use the Latin name as a serious mysterious place-name, not as a joke in the student-facing document.

Important tone:

- Lewis Carroll in spirit: strange, exact, rule-bound, puzzle-like.
- Serious and clear, not childish.
- The adventure frame should support the logic, not swamp it.
- Avoid elaborate RPG mechanics, dice, inventories, or branching game rules unless specifically requested later.
- The assessment should still be a readable student handout with structured questions and answer boxes.
- Students should always know what logic skills they are expected to use.

Core design change:

- Do not build one puzzle test per week.
- Build one cumulative Logic Dungeon chapter after every three weeks.
- Weekly Logic Labs remain the regular transfer-practice assignments.
- Lit Examples remain the literary application pieces.
- Logic Dungeon chapters are larger cumulative puzzle assessments.

Known early sequence:

Weeks 1–3 are the first cluster and should be the first prototype.

Week 1 skill focus:
- Find the conclusion.
- Find the stated reason.
- Surface the hidden assumption.
- Test whether the conclusion follows.
- Distinguish what logic can test from what it cannot supply by itself: facts, observation, testimony, wisdom, virtue, moral judgment.

Week 2 skill focus:
- Terms and ambiguity.
- Equivocation / two-faced words.
- Notice when the same word shifts meaning inside an argument.
- Rewrite an argument so the key term has one stable meaning.
- Decide whether the conclusion follows after the term is fixed.

Week 3 skill focus:
- Propositions.
- Subject and predicate.
- Quantity and quality.
- A/E/I/O forms.
- Scope errors: All when only Some is true, None when exceptions exist, Most when unsupported or unclear.
- Translate ordinary claims into more precise quantified claims.

Weeks 4–6 are also substantially known and may be planned, though the immediate prototype is Weeks 1–3.

Week 4 focus:
- Practical syllogisms.
- Major, minor, and middle terms.
- Whether the middle term actually connects the premises.

Week 5 focus:
- Mood/form/validity.
- Valid and invalid syllogistic patterns.
- Common invalid forms such as undistributed middle and two negative premises.

Week 6 focus:
- Validity vs. truth/soundness.
- A valid form can contain false premises.
- A true conclusion does not prove that the argument was good.
- Logic tests form and inference; facts still require evidence.

Possible full-course Logic Dungeon arc:

1. Logic Dungeon I: The Hall of Inscriptions
   Cluster: Weeks 1–3
   Focus: argument skeletons, hidden assumptions, equivocation, propositions, A/E/I/O, scope errors.

2. Logic Dungeon II: The Syllogism Labyrinth
   Cluster: Weeks 4–6
   Focus: syllogism structure, middle term, form, validity, soundness, form vs. fact.

3. Logic Dungeon III: The Court of the False Oracle
   Cluster: Weeks 7–9, to be finalized later based on the actual reader handouts.
   Likely focus: fallacies, evidence, testimony, authority, probability, or rhetorical misuse of reasoning.

4. Logic Dungeon IV: The Archive of Broken Proofs
   Cluster: Weeks 10–12, provisional.
   Likely focus: longer chains of proof, evidence, analogy, causal reasoning, testimony, or induction depending on later Whately sequence.

5. Logic Dungeon V: The Tower of Persuasion
   Cluster: Weeks 13–15, provisional.
   Likely focus: rhetoric, persuasion, misuse of language, civic reasoning, and the limits of logic.

6. Logic Dungeon VI: The Gates of Castrum Malae Ratiocinationis
   Final cumulative assessment.
   Focus: broad review of the whole course. Students must escape or enter the final fortress by distinguishing valid reasoning from bad reasoning across many forms.

You may revise chapter names if stronger names appear, but keep Castrum Malae Ratiocinationis as the final destination name.

Adventure premise:

A company of explorers seeks the hidden fortress called Castrum Malae Ratiocinationis. The tunnels leading toward it are not guarded mainly by monsters but by bad arguments. Gates open when explorers can tell the difference between proof and persuasion, stable terms and shifting terms, careful claims and overbroad claims, valid form and false premises, good evidence and mere confidence. The class’s task is to keep the expedition alive by reasoning carefully.

New proposed class mechanic:

The class begins the Logic Dungeon arc with 20 adventurers.

During each Logic Dungeon chapter, poor reasoning can cause adventurers to be lost. The mechanic should create stakes and a shared class story, but it must not become humiliating or punitive toward individual students.

Design guidance for the 20-adventurer mechanic:

- Treat adventurers as a collective expedition resource, not as individual students.
- Avoid public shaming tied to named students or visible individual grades.
- Losses should be connected to classwide or assessment-level outcomes, not “Johnny got this wrong, so an adventurer died.”
- The mechanic can be used as a teacher-facing or class-facing overlay.
- It should be simple enough that the assessment remains primarily a logic test.
- Wrong answers on certain trap questions may cost adventurers, but the scoring system must stay fair.
- Consider making some losses recoverable through repair questions, reflection, or corrected reasoning.
- Consider rewarding careful revision: an initial wrong answer may trigger danger, but a successful explanation or repair can save the adventurer.
- The mechanic should encourage precision, not speed or guessing.

Possible forms of the 20-adventurer mechanic:

Option A: Teacher-facing score translation
- Students complete the test normally.
- The teacher uses total class performance or individual test bands to narrate how many adventurers survived.
- This is safest and simplest.

Option B: Built-in trap stations
- The assessment includes specific “trap” questions.
- Each trap has a tempting wrong answer.
- If the student falls for the trap and cannot explain the issue, an adventurer is lost in the story.
- A repair question can save the adventurer.

Option C: Classwide expedition tracker
- The class starts with 20 adventurers.
- After grading, the teacher reports: “The expedition lost 3 adventurers in the Hall of Inscriptions, mostly to two-faced words and overbroad All claims.”
- This gives feedback on concepts without singling students out.

Option D: Individual copy, metaphorical only
- Each student’s handout says the expedition has 20 adventurers.
- Stations may say “If the party accepts this argument too quickly, one adventurer is lost.”
- But actual grades remain normal rubric scores.

Recommended approach for the prototype:

Use a light version of Option B + Option C.

In the student-facing prototype, include the 20 adventurers as part of the premise and mark a few stations as dangerous traps. However, do not create a complicated life-count system. In the teacher-facing answer key, explain how the teacher may translate performance into expedition losses if desired.

Possible teacher-facing survival rule for prototype:

- The expedition begins with 20 adventurers.
- Each major trap station can cost 1 adventurer if the reasoning error is not identified.
- A student/class can prevent the loss by correctly explaining the flaw or repairing the argument.
- The teacher may use classwide patterns after grading to narrate losses.
- Keep this narrative separate from the numeric grade if needed.

Prototype to build next:

Logic Dungeon I: The Hall of Inscriptions

Cluster: Weeks 1–3

Purpose:
Students apply the first three logic habits in a coherent puzzle adventure:
- argument skeletons and hidden assumptions
- limits of logic
- equivocation / shifting terms
- A/E/I/O propositions
- scope errors with All, None, Some, Most

Expected length:
- Approximately 6–8 pages for the student test.
- Clean LaTeX/PDF layout eventually, but first produce a strong content draft.
- 5–7 stations.
- Each station should have a short story/clue plus structured questions.
- Include answer spaces in the eventual handout.

Suggested structure for Logic Dungeon I:

Opening: The Twenty Adventurers
- A short serious adventure premise.
- The company enters the first underground passage toward Castrum Malae Ratiocinationis.
- They begin with 20 adventurers.
- The first region is called the Hall of Inscriptions.
- Every inscription is a test of reasoning.
- Some doors are safe; some are traps; some are true but do not prove what the explorers think they prove.

Station 1: The Gate of Applause
Skill: Week 1 hidden assumption / conclusion-reason-follow test.
Possible trap: Popular approval mistaken for truth or wisdom.
Students identify conclusion, stated reason, hidden assumption, and whether the conclusion follows.

Station 2: The Two-Voiced Guide
Skill: Week 2 equivocation.
Possible trap: A key term shifts meaning, such as clear, safe, right, free, light, sound, fair, or certain.
Students identify the shifting term, explain the two senses, rewrite the argument with one stable meaning, and test whether it still follows.

Station 3: The Beast Ledger
Skill: Week 3 A/E/I/O and scope.
Possible trap: All/None/Most claims about creatures or tunnel signs overreach the evidence.
Students classify claims as A/E/I/O or Most, identify overbroad claims, and repair one claim.

Station 4: The Shortcut Inscription
Skill: Combine Week 1 + Week 3.
A sign gives a quantified rule and a character draws too strong a conclusion.
Students decide what follows and what does not.

Station 5: The Mapmaker’s Oath
Skill: Week 1 limits of logic + Week 2 term stability.
A mapmaker makes a persuasive claim with a possibly shifting term or unsupported assumption.
Students distinguish what logic can test from what requires observation/testimony.

Station 6: The Door of Narrow Claims
Skill: Repair bad reasoning.
Students revise unsafe claims into careful claims that could be supported by the evidence.

Final Decision: Which Tunnel Should the Company Take?
Skill: synthesis.
Students choose between two or three tunnels using the prior clues. The correct answer should require careful logic, not plot guessing. Students must cite at least two stations and explain why one route is best supported.

Required question types in the prototype:

- At least one conclusion/reason/hidden-assumption chart.
- At least one equivocation diagnosis and rewrite.
- At least one A/E/I/O classification task.
- At least one overbroad All/None/Most repair task.
- At least one question about what logic cannot decide by itself.
- At least one final synthesis question.
- At least one optional or teacher-facing survival/loss note tied to the 20 adventurers.

Important student-facing wording rules:

- Do not over-clue the answer.
- Do not say “this is an equivocation” before asking students to find it.
- Do not name the shifting term in the prompt if the student is supposed to identify it.
- Do not make the wordplay silly or fake.
- Make tempting wrong answers plausible.
- Keep the story compact and the reasoning exact.
- Use clear task verbs: identify, explain, rewrite, decide, repair, support.

Important grading/answer-key rules:

- The answer key must be self-contained.
- Define any local grading terms before using them.
- Reward precise diagnosis, not mere labels.
- Distinguish strong, partial, and weak answers.
- Explain why the tempting answer is tempting but wrong.
- Include a teacher note about using the 20-adventurer mechanic without public shaming.
