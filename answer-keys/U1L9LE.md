**Purpose:** Students apply Week 9 circular-reasoning / begging-the-question diagnosis to Brutus’s private deliberation and public defense in *Julius Caesar*.

## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

- **Circular reasoning / begging the question:** the reason assumes the conclusion or only works if the conclusion is already granted.
- **Independent support:** evidence acceptable before the verdict is granted.
- **Restatement:** the conclusion repeated in other words.
- **Loaded alternative:** an either/or frame that assumes the disputed danger.
- **Rewrite:** a version that states what would need to be shown independently.
- **Present vs future danger:** evidence about what Caesar is versus speculation about what he may become.

Plot summary alone does not earn points.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Opening conclusion (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Caesar must die / it must be by his death.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Clear death/must-die conclusion
- 1 pt: Vague “Brutus is worried”
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Present vs future (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Present: little personal cause; Caesar’s affections have not clearly outrun reason. Future: crowning may change him; ambition’s ladder; serpent’s egg / possible extremities. He relies on preventative future danger.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Both present weakness and future reliance
- 1 pt: One side only
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – Fair opponent test (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A fair opponent could accept that crowns can corrupt and still reject that Caesar **must** die now, because the specific future tyranny is not independently proved. Strong answers use the “accept reason without accepting conclusion” test.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Applies independent-support test clearly
- 1 pt: Partial
- 0 pts: Says the speech fully proves the necessity of death

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Fashion it thus / serpent’s egg (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
“Fashion it thus” shows Brutus shaping the case toward a needed quarrel. The serpent’s-egg comparison assumes Caesar’s “kind” will be mischievous once “hatched,” which builds future guilt into the image rather than proving present capital cause.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Clear assumption-of-verdict analysis
- 1 pt: Notes the image without circularity
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Ambition to death (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
If “ambitious” already means “deserves death for enslaving Rome,” then “as he was ambitious, I slew him” restates the verdict. Without independent proof that ambition equaled imminent tyranny, the move begs the question.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Links unproved ambition to circular justification
- 1 pt: General doubt about ambition
- 0 pts: Treats ambition as self-proving

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – Slaves/free men (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
The alternative assumes that leaving Caesar alive means all die slaves. That frames the disputed danger as already settled, pressuring the crowd without independent proof of enslavement.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names assumed danger in the either/or
- 1 pt: Calls it emotional only
- 0 pts: Incorrect or missing

## Question 7: Part 4 Q1 – Non-circular rewrite (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Example: “If independent evidence shows Caesar will use a crown to destroy Roman liberty—specific acts, plans, or abuses—then preventive action may be debated; without that evidence, death is not yet justified.” Must demand independent support, not restatement.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Clear claim + independent evidence requirement
- 1 pt: Partial rewrite
- 0 pts: Still circular

## Question 8: Part 4 Q2 – Capstone (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong paragraph explains that Brutus’s speeches can be coherent and public-spirited while still assuming future tyranny or loading ambition with the death verdict. Week 9 asks whether reasons can be granted before the conclusion; if not, the speech is not yet proof.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Week 9 tool + these passages
- 1 pt: Thin application
- 0 pts: Plot only
