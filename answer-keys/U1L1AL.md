# U1L1AL – Argument Lab Answer Key & Rubric

**Total Points:** 10

This answer key is based on the paired Week 1 Argument Lab (`units/elements-of-logic-week1/tex/Whately_Logic_Week1_Argument_Lab.tex` / `.pdf`) and the paired Whately student reading. Model answers use the lab's four-part argument test: Conclusion, Stated reason, Hidden assumption, and Follows. Accept clear paraphrases, but require students to identify reasoning structure rather than merely state agreement or disagreement.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Model the four-step argument test (1 point)

**Expected Answer:**  
A strong answer applies the four-step test to the model argument, "This rule is popular with most students, so it must be fair." Conclusion: the rule must be fair. Stated reason: the rule is popular with most students. Hidden assumption: what is popular with most students is fair, or majority approval is a reliable sign of fairness. Follows: the conclusion does not necessarily follow. Popularity may be relevant, but it does not prove fairness; a rule can be popular with most students and still be unfair to some students, especially a minority or less powerful group.

**Rubric:**
- 1 pt: Fully correct and complete — uses the required labels (Conclusion, Stated reason, Hidden assumption, Follows) and accurately applies them to the model argument about popularity and fairness
- 0 pts: Incorrect, missing, unlabeled, or only defines the four-part test without applying it to the model argument

**Notes for grading:**
- Accept clear paraphrases of the four labels and the model argument's parts.
- Do not require the exact word "minority," but a full-credit answer should recognize that popularity alone does not prove fairness.
- The response must show that logic tests the movement from reason to conclusion, not just whether the student likes the rule.

## Question 2: Part 2 Q1 – Statue argument (1 point)

**Expected Answer:**  
Conclusion: the man must have lived nobly. Stated reason: the city placed a bronze statue of him in the town square. Hidden assumption: a city would only honor someone with a statue if that person truly lived nobly, or public honors reliably prove nobility. Follows: the conclusion does not necessarily follow. A statue may show that someone was famous, powerful, useful to the city, or admired by some people, but the statue alone does not prove noble character.

**Rubric:**
- 1 pt: Fully correct and complete — identifies the conclusion, stated reason, hidden assumption, and gives a sound follows/not-follows judgment
- 0 pts: Incorrect, missing, or only gives an opinion about statues without analyzing the argument

**Notes for grading:**
- Accept either "does not follow" or "does not necessarily follow."
- A full-credit answer should distinguish public honor from proof of moral nobility.

## Question 3: Part 2 Q2 – Difficult words and better reasoning (1 point)

**Expected Answer:**  
Conclusion: the first essay must have better reasoning. Stated reason: the first essay uses more difficult words than the second essay. Hidden assumption: harder vocabulary means stronger reasoning. Follows: the conclusion does not follow. Good reasoning depends on clear claims, relevant evidence, and valid connections between ideas; difficult words can hide weak reasoning or make an argument less clear.

**Rubric:**
- 1 pt: Fully correct and complete — identifies all four parts and explains why vocabulary difficulty alone does not prove reasoning quality
- 0 pts: Incorrect, missing, or treats difficult vocabulary as automatically better reasoning

**Notes for grading:**
- Reward answers that connect this to Whately's concern with reasoning rather than appearance.
- Students may say complex vocabulary can sometimes help precision, but it is not enough by itself.

## Question 4: Part 2 Q3 – Tradition and best method (1 point)

**Expected Answer:**  
Conclusion: this way must be the best way to do the assignment. Stated reason: the class has always done the assignment this way. Hidden assumption: the way something has always been done must be the best way. Follows: the conclusion does not follow. A long-standing method may be familiar or may have worked in the past, but tradition alone does not prove that it is best; it still needs reasons showing effectiveness, fairness, or fit for the goal.

**Rubric:**
- 1 pt: Fully correct and complete — identifies the conclusion, stated reason, hidden assumption, and explains why habit/tradition alone is insufficient
- 0 pts: Incorrect, missing, or only says whether the student personally likes tradition

**Notes for grading:**
- Accept answers that say the argument is weak rather than absolutely false.
- Full credit requires a clear hidden assumption about tradition or past practice.

## Question 5: Part 2 Q4 – Same action, same rule (1 point)

**Expected Answer:**  
Conclusion: the rule is not just. Stated reason: every just rule should treat the same action the same way, but this rule punishes one student and excuses another student for the same action. Hidden assumption: the two cases are truly the same in the relevant details, and a just rule must apply consistently to like cases. Follows: this argument is stronger than the others if the two students really did the same action under the same relevant circumstances. If important circumstances differ, the argument would need more information.

**Rubric:**
- 1 pt: Fully correct and complete — identifies all four parts and recognizes that the argument follows or is strong if the cases are genuinely alike
- 0 pts: Incorrect, missing, or fails to address the consistency principle in the argument

**Notes for grading:**
- Accept "follows" if the student notes that the action and relevant circumstances are truly the same.
- Accept "mostly follows but needs more information" if the student explains that just treatment depends on whether the cases are really alike.

## Question 6: Part 3 Q1 – Literary anchor argument (1 point)

**Expected Answer:**  
For the Macbeth claim, a strong answer identifies the conclusion as Macbeth is not responsible for what he later chooses, the stated reason as the witches predicted his future, the hidden assumption as prediction removes responsibility or forces the later action, and the follows judgment as weak or not necessary because prediction by itself does not prove that Macbeth lacked choice. For the Caesar claim, a strong answer identifies the conclusion as Brutus is justified in killing Caesar now, the stated reason as Caesar may become dangerous later, the hidden assumption as possible future danger justifies present killing, and the follows judgment as weak or not necessary unless there is stronger evidence that the danger is real, serious, and cannot be handled another way.

**Rubric:**
- 1 pt: Fully correct and complete — analyzes one chosen literary claim with conclusion, stated reason, hidden assumption, and follows judgment
- 0 pts: Incorrect, missing, or summarizes the play without analyzing the argument

**Notes for grading:**
- Students only need to analyze one of the two claims.
- Do not require detailed Shakespeare knowledge; grade the reasoning structure.

## Question 7: Part 4 Q1 – Repair a weak argument (1 point)

**Expected Answer:**  
A strong answer chooses one weak argument from Part 2 and repairs it by narrowing the conclusion, adding a more relevant reason, or making the hidden assumption more defensible. For example, the statue argument could be repaired as: "The city placed a bronze statue of this man after reviewing his public service and sacrifices, so the city likely judged him worthy of public honor." This is more careful because it changes "must have lived nobly" into a narrower claim about public judgment and adds stronger support.

**Rubric:**
- 1 pt: Fully correct and complete — names or clearly uses one weak Part 2 argument and revises it so the conclusion is better supported or more careful
- 0 pts: Incorrect, missing, or repeats the original weak argument without repair

**Notes for grading:**
- Accept many repairs if the student improves the reasoning rather than merely rewording it.
- A repair may change the conclusion, add a missing reason, or narrow the claim.

## Question 8: Part 4 Q2 – Original argument and analysis (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**  
A strong answer writes an original argument about one of the allowed topics and then analyzes it with the four required labels. The argument should contain a conclusion and a stated reason. The analysis should identify the conclusion, identify the stated reason, name a plausible hidden assumption, and judge whether the conclusion follows. Strong answers may also explain how the argument could be strengthened.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Fully correct and complete — includes an original argument and accurately analyzes it with Conclusion, Stated reason, Hidden assumption, and Follows
- 1 pt: Partially correct — includes an original argument but the analysis is incomplete, mislabeled, or missing one major part
- 0 pts: Incorrect, missing, or does not provide an original argument

**Notes for grading:**
- The topic may be a school rule, Macbeth or Julius Caesar, a public speech, a sports decision, or a family disagreement.
- Grade the clarity of argument structure, not whether the grader agrees with the student's claim.

## Question 9: Part 5 Q1 – Exit claim about logic (1 point)

**Expected Answer:**  
A strong paragraph completes the claim with a clear statement about when logic helps a reader and why. It should explain that logic helps when a reader needs to test whether a conclusion really follows from its reasons, uncover a hidden assumption, or avoid being impressed by popularity, tradition, difficult language, prediction, or public honor without sufficient support. The paragraph should use one relevant example from Whately, Macbeth, or Julius Caesar.

**Rubric:**
- 1 pt: Fully correct and complete — gives a clear claim about the value of logic and supports it with one relevant example from Whately, Macbeth, or Julius Caesar
- 0 pts: Incorrect, missing, or gives only a vague statement about logic with no relevant example

**Notes for grading:**
- Accept a range of examples if the student connects the example to testing reasons and conclusions.
- The paragraph does not need to be long, but it should make a complete claim and give support.
