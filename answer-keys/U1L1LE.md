# U1L1LE – Lit Example Worksheet Answer Key & Rubric

**Total Points:** 12

**Paired reader:** `units/elements-of-logic-week1/lit-examples/U1L1LE_lit_example_reader.tex` / `.pdf`.

**Paired worksheet:** `units/elements-of-logic-week1/lit-examples/U1L1LE_lit_example_worksheet.tex` / `.pdf` (Assignment ID **U1L1LE**).

**Purpose:** Students apply the Week 1 Whately four-step method to two Macbeth passages. Grade for logical structure and textual grounding, not for plot summary. Accept varied phrasing when the logical roles are clear.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Macbeth's first inference from prophecy (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**  
A strong answer identifies a conclusion Macbeth is tempted toward, such as that the kingship may be coming to him, that the prophecy has real force, or that the confirmed Cawdor prediction gives weight to the larger royal prediction. The stated reason should be that one part of the witches' prophecy has already come true: Macbeth has been named Thane of Cawdor. The hidden assumption should connect partial confirmation to the larger conclusion, for example: if one prediction came true, the remaining prediction is reliable or destined. The follow-test should be cautious: the Cawdor news gives Macbeth a reason to take the prophecy seriously, but it does not by itself prove that he must be king, that murder is justified, or that future events are forced.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Correct conclusion/reason/hidden assumption with a cautious follow-test grounded in Act 1, Scene 3
- 1 pt: Identifies the general prophecy issue but gives a weak or incomplete hidden assumption/follow-test
- 0 pts: Mostly plot summary, unsupported moralizing, or no clear logical roles

**Notes for grading:**  
- Do not require one exact conclusion; the prompt allows “one conclusion Macbeth is tempted to draw.”  
- Do not award full credit if the student treats prophecy as automatically proving murder, necessity, or innocence.

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Banquo's warning about truthful predictions (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**  
**Conclusion:** Macbeth should be cautious about trusting or following the witches' predictions.  
**Stated reason:** Evil or deceptive powers may tell small truths / “honest trifles” in order to lead people into deeper harm.  
**Hidden assumption:** A source can use true statements deceptively, so partial truth does not guarantee trustworthy guidance or good consequences.  
**Follows:** Largely yes as a warning. Banquo does not prove the witches are lying about everything; he gives a reason for caution because true details can still be used to mislead.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Reconstructs Banquo's warning with conclusion, reason, hidden assumption, and a nuanced follow-test
- 1 pt: Understands that Banquo advises caution but leaves the reasoning vague
- 0 pts: Says only that Banquo is afraid/suspicious without reconstructing the argument

**Notes for grading:**  
- Full credit should distinguish “true statement” from “trustworthy guide.”

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – Macbeth's argument against murdering Duncan (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**  
**Conclusion:** Macbeth should not murder Duncan / the murder is wrong, dangerous, or self-defeating.  
**Stated reasons:** Accept any two well-grounded reasons from the passage: violent teaching returns to plague the inventor; justice turns the poison back on the murderer; Duncan is Macbeth's kinsman and subject; Macbeth is Duncan's host and should protect him; Duncan has ruled virtuously; Duncan's virtues will testify against the murder.  
**Hidden assumption:** If an act violates loyalty/hospitality/justice and brings destructive consequences back on the actor, Macbeth ought not do it.  
**Follows:** Yes, the reasons strongly support not murdering Duncan, especially when taken together.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Uses two textual reasons and supplies a coherent hidden assumption and follow-test
- 1 pt: Gives a correct general answer but only one reason, weak textual grounding, or incomplete follow-test
- 0 pts: Summarizes the soliloquy without identifying argument structure

**Notes for grading:**  
- Students may frame the conclusion as prudential (“it will return against him”) or moral (“it is wrong”); either can receive full credit if supported by the chosen reasons.

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Motive is not proof (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**  
A strong answer explains that a motive is what pushes a person to want or do something, while proof is a reason that shows a conclusion is true, justified, necessary, or rightly chosen. Macbeth's “vaulting ambition” explains why he is tempted toward murder, but ambition does not prove that murder is justified, that Duncan deserves death, that Macbeth must act, or that the prophecy excuses him. In fact, Macbeth's naming of ambition may weaken the case for action because it exposes the desire driving him.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Clearly distinguishes motive from proof and applies the distinction to Macbeth without mere plot summary
- 1 pt: Gives a partial distinction or application but remains vague
- 0 pts: Treats ambition itself as proof or gives only theme/plot summary

**Notes for grading:**  
- Reward answers that say ambition explains Macbeth psychologically but does not justify him logically.

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Testing a too-quick inference (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**  
Answers will vary. A strong answer chooses a specific possible overreach and analyzes it with the four steps. Examples include: from “the Cawdor prediction came true” to “the kingship is guaranteed”; from “the witches predicted kingship” to “Macbeth is not responsible”; from “Macbeth imagines murder” to “murder is necessary”; from “Duncan's death could bring success” to “the consequences can be controlled.” The answer should name the actual support, explain the hidden assumption, and judge whether the conclusion follows. In most strong answers, the follow-test will be no or only partly: the evidence may create possibility or temptation, but not proof.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Chooses a precise moment and gives all four logical elements with a defensible follow-test
- 1 pt: Chooses a relevant moment but leaves one or more logical elements underdeveloped
- 0 pts: Gives a general theme statement with no argument analysis

**Notes for grading:**  
- Because the prompt allows choice, grade for disciplined method rather than one predetermined passage.

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – What follows from what? (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**  
A strong answer identifies one supported claim and one unsupported or not-yet-proved claim. Supported claims might include: the witches have correctly predicted Macbeth's new title; Macbeth has reasons to fear the moral and practical consequences of murdering Duncan; Macbeth is driven by ambition; Banquo has reason to urge caution. Unsupported claims might include: Macbeth must become king by murder; prophecy removes responsibility; ambition proves the action is justified; the future is settled because one prediction came true. The explanation should contrast the quality of support: textual evidence may establish a fact or temptation, but it does not automatically establish necessity, moral permission, or proof.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Clearly contrasts one supported and one unsupported claim with logical explanation
- 1 pt: Names plausible claims but gives a thin explanation of the difference
- 0 pts: Offers only plot summary or unsupported opinion

**Notes for grading:**  
- Full credit requires a contrast, not just two separate observations.

**Overall Notes for Grading This Worksheet:**  
- Whole points only. No half points.  
- Grade the logic of the student's analysis: conclusion, stated reason, hidden assumption, follow-test.  
- Do not require extensive Shakespeare quotation. Brief textual grounding is enough when the argument structure is clear.  
- Total: 6 questions × 2 points = 12 points.
