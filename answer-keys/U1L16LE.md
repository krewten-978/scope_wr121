**Purpose:** Students apply Week 16 rhetoric-vs-proof analysis to Antony’s funeral oration and Lady Macbeth’s persuasion of Macbeth.

## Grading Vocabulary for This Key

- **Persuasion / rhetorical force:** audience movement and felt power of the speech.
- **Proof:** support that actually establishes the claim under debate.
- **Logos / ethos / pathos:** reason and examples; speaker character/stance; emotion.
- **Claim under proof:** the exact conclusion still needing support.
- **Force matches / exceeds / replaces proof:** judgment about whether persuasion is backed by, outruns, or substitutes for support.

Plot summary alone does not earn points.

## Question 1: Part 1 Q1 – Antony’s claim and desired response (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Antony presses that Caesar was not simply the ambitious tyrant Brutus described; that the killing is grievous/treasonous; and that the crowd should grieve, distrust the conspirators, and turn against them. Desired response: pity for Caesar, anger at the conspirators, and political reversal.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names claim-pressure and audience response
- 1 pt: One element only or thin wording
- 0 pts: Plot summary only

## Question 2: Part 1 Q2 – Ethos and pathos in Antony (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Ethos: “He was my friend, faithful and just to me”; reluctant “not to praise him”; repeated “honourable man” stance that borrows Brutus’s credit while undercutting it. Pathos: poor who cried, shared fall of countrymen, wounds/mantle grief, “bloody treason” language.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: One clear ethos and one clear pathos move
- 1 pt: Only one appeal clearly identified
- 0 pts: Says Antony is emotional without examples

## Question 3: Part 2 Q1 – Antony’s logos-style evidence (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Examples such as captives/ransoms filling public coffers and Caesar weeping for the poor pressure the “ambitious” charge. Limited point: those acts do not look like hard, self-seeking ambition of “sterner stuff.” That may weaken Brutus’s label without fully proving Caesar’s whole character or settling whether killing him was justified.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names evidence and a limited supported point
- 1 pt: Names evidence only
- 0 pts: Treats examples as total victory

## Question 4: Part 2 Q2 – Force vs stronger conclusions (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Antony’s force often exceeds or begins to replace full proof for stronger conclusions: that the conspirators are simply bloody traitors, that Caesar’s death is wholly unjustified, or that the crowd’s next political act is settled. The speech powerfully moves judgment; it does not complete a full logical case for every conclusion the crowd may adopt.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Judges force vs proof on stronger conclusions with reason
- 1 pt: Says he is persuasive without distinguishing claims
- 0 pts: Plot only or “Antony proves everything”

## Question 5: Part 3 Q1 – Lady Macbeth’s aim and appeals (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
She wants Macbeth to carry out the murder / recover the courage of his earlier hope. Appeals include pathos/shame (green and pale; love accounted little), identity/ethos pressure on manhood and valor matching desire, and practical logos-like reassurance (“we’ll not fail” with a plan).

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: States aim and identifies appeals
- 1 pt: Aim or appeals only
- 0 pts: Calls her mean without analysis

## Question 6: Part 3 Q2 – Remaining burden after Lady Macbeth (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Still unproved: that murder is morally permissible; that manhood requires the deed; that success is assured; that Duncan’s trust duties no longer bind; that ambition plus shame equals justification. Emotional settlement is not moral or factual proof.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Names a real remaining moral/factual burden
- 1 pt: Vague “murder is bad”
- 0 pts: Says her speech settles the matter

## Question 7: Part 4 Q1 – Rhetoric-vs-proof map (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
A strong map for either passage includes claim, audience, key appeals, what is actually supported, what is only suggested, and a force-vs-proof judgment. Example (Antony): claim = Caesar wronged / conspirators dishonorable; audience = Roman crowd; appeals = friendship ethos, grief pathos, anti-ambition examples; proved/limited = some public-benefiting acts; suggested = full treason narrative and required political revenge; force exceeds full proof.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Coherent complete map
- 1 pt: Partial map
- 0 pts: Plot summary

## Question 8: Part 4 Q2 – Comparison (2 points)

**Expected Answer:**
Both speeches can be powerful because they recruit identity, emotion, and selective reasons. Power does not settle the claim that needs proof: whether Caesar’s death is justified, what Rome should do, or whether Macbeth should murder Duncan. Rhetoric can prepare attention or distort judgment; logic still asks what follows from what.

**Rubric:**
- 2 pts: Compares both and explains force without proof
- 1 pt: One passage well or thin comparison
- 0 pts: Plot only

**Overall Notes for Grading This Worksheet:**
- Whole points only. Total: 8 × 2 = 16 points.
- Credit students who refuse both cynicism (“all rhetoric is fake”) and naivete (“moving speech = proved claim”).
